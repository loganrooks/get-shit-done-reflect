# Philosophical Deliberation: Dialectics

**Tradition:** German Idealism, Historical Materialism, Critical Theory
**Key thinkers:** G.W.F. Hegel, Karl Marx, Theodor Adorno, Fredric Jameson
**Created:** 2026-03-03
**Status:** Active

## Core Ideas

Dialectics is a method of understanding change through contradiction. Where formal logic treats contradiction as error (A cannot be both A and not-A), dialectics treats contradiction as the engine of development. Things become what they are by encountering what they are not.

**Hegel** articulated this most systematically. Consciousness does not develop by accumulating facts but by encountering its own limitations -- by discovering that what it took to be true is inadequate, and being forced to develop a richer understanding that incorporates both the original position and its negation. The famous movement is often simplified as thesis-antithesis-synthesis, but Hegel's actual term is *Aufhebung* (sublation): a process that simultaneously negates, preserves, and elevates. The synthesis does not split the difference between thesis and antithesis. It transforms the entire framework so the contradiction is resolved at a higher level while both moments are preserved within it. Crucially, the synthesis then becomes the new thesis for the next dialectical movement -- this is why Hegel's system spirals rather than terminates.

The **dialectic of the concept** (*Begriff*) describes how abstract categories develop internal contradictions that force them toward concreteness. Being, taken as the most abstract category, immediately reveals itself as indistinguishable from Nothing -- and their unity is Becoming. This matters for system design: abstract principles ("never repeat mistakes") develop internal contradictions when implemented ("but tracking every mistake overwhelms the system"), and these contradictions drive the system toward more concrete, nuanced forms.

The **negation of the negation** is the structural pattern: a thing is negated (first negation), and then its negation is itself negated (second negation), producing not a return to the original but a qualitatively new position that incorporates both moments. A seed is negated by the plant that grows from it; the plant is negated by the fruit it produces. The fruit contains new seeds, but they are not the original seeds -- they carry the entire developmental history within them.

**Concrete universals** are Hegel's alternative to the abstract universals of traditional logic. An abstract universal ("all signals have severity") strips away particularity to reach generality. A concrete universal preserves the particular within the universal -- it is a general principle that remains grounded in and enriched by its specific instances. The lesson that "plans must verify system behavior, not assume it" (les-2026-02-28) is a concrete universal: it is general (applicable to any plan) yet carries its specific origins (the signals where assumed behavior failed) as constitutive evidence.

The **master-slave dialectic** (Phenomenology of Spirit, Chapter IV) describes how self-consciousness develops through recognition by another consciousness. The master, who risks death and subjugates the slave, achieves recognition -- but it is recognition by someone the master does not recognize as an equal, so it is hollow. The slave, who works on the world and transforms material reality, develops genuine self-consciousness through labor. The one who seemed powerless achieves what the powerful one cannot. This is a model of how agency develops through encounter and mutual transformation, not through isolated self-certainty.

**Marx** took Hegel's dialectic and grounded it in material reality. Where Hegel saw contradictions in concepts driving history, Marx saw contradictions in material conditions -- in the forces and relations of production. The key Marxian insight for system design is **praxis**: the unity of theory and practice. Theory that does not transform practice is mere contemplation; practice without theory is blind activity. Genuine knowledge is produced through the dialectical unity of thinking and doing. Marx's critique of the base/superstructure relation also applies: the material infrastructure of a system (its file formats, storage locations, runtime constraints) shapes and constrains what the conceptual superstructure (workflows, philosophies, design patterns) can express.

**Adorno's negative dialectics** is a critique of Hegel from within. Adorno argued that Hegel's system, despite its commitment to negativity, ultimately betrays it by insisting on reconciliation -- on the final synthesis of Absolute Knowledge where all contradictions are resolved. Adorno insists that some contradictions should not be resolved. Premature synthesis papers over real antagonisms. The task is to hold contradictions open, to resist the compulsion to harmonize, because the contradiction itself may be the most truthful statement of the situation. In Adorno's words: "The whole is the untrue." This is a warning against any system that claims to have achieved total self-knowledge or complete self-improvement.

**Jameson** extended dialectical thinking into cultural and technological criticism, arguing that the contradictions of a system are most visible at its boundaries -- at the points where it encounters what it cannot assimilate. For a self-improving system, the boundary is the point where self-improvement itself becomes the problem.

Why this matters for GSD Reflect: the system is premised on self-improvement through self-negation. It detects its own faults (signals), critiques its own patterns (reflection), and distills principles to prevent recurrence (lessons). This is an explicitly dialectical structure. But a system built on dialectics must also apply dialectics to itself -- it must recognize that its own self-improvement process carries contradictions that no amount of further self-improvement can eliminate, only transform.

## Relevance to GSD Reflect

The existing roadmap deliberation (v1.17-plus-roadmap-deliberation.md) already identifies a dialectical structure:

> Signal (thesis: raw observation) -> Reflection (antithesis: critique + counter-evidence) -> Lesson (synthesis: principle that transcends individual signals)

This mapping is correct but shallow. It captures the surface form of the dialectic without its essential content: that each synthesis generates new contradictions, and that the process of self-improvement is itself contradictory.

The deeper dialectical structure of GSD Reflect operates at multiple levels:

**Level 1 -- The signal lifecycle as dialectical movement.** A signal is a determinate negation of the system's self-image. The system assumes it is performing well; the signal says "here is where you failed." This is not abstract negation (the system is bad) but determinate negation (the system failed in *this specific way*, for *this specific reason*). The reflection process takes this determinate negation and seeks its ground -- what pattern explains these failures? -- and the lesson that emerges preserves the signal's specificity (evidence links, evidence_snapshots) while transcending it into a general principle. This is Aufhebung: the signal is negated as a mere particular, preserved as evidence, and elevated into a lesson.

**Level 2 -- The negation of the negation in automation.** Every fix the system introduces creates new problems. Auto-collection was designed to negate the problem "signals never collected unless user remembers" (SIG-01). But auto-collection generates context exhaustion (SIG-05) and feedback loops (SIG-03). The reentrancy guard (SIG-03) is itself a negation of auto-collection's negation -- it constrains the fix to prevent the fix's own pathology. But the reentrancy guard introduces rigidity: legitimate re-collection within the TTL window is blocked. Each solution is a new thesis carrying its own antithesis.

**Level 3 -- The system's internal contradictions.** The automation level system (AUTO-01 through AUTO-07) embodies a fundamental contradiction: more automation consumes more context, which degrades the quality of the automated operations. AUTO-04 (context-aware deferral) is the system recognizing this contradiction and internalizing it as a design constraint. But this creates a meta-contradiction: the system that is supposed to automate self-improvement must sometimes suppress self-improvement to function. The system must negate its own purpose to fulfill it.

**Level 4 -- The master-slave dialectic of developer and system.** The developer creates the system and is its master. But the system observes the developer -- frustration detection captures the developer's emotional state, deviation tracking records where the developer departed from the plan, signal collection documents the developer's mistakes. Through this mutual observation, both are transformed. The developer becomes more reflective (internalizing the system's categories -- thinking in terms of signals and patterns). The system becomes more autonomous (auto-triggering, self-monitoring). This is Hegel's recognition dialectic: each consciousness develops through encounter with the other. The developer's mastery is undermined by dependence on the system's observations; the system's servitude is undermined by the knowledge it accumulates through labor on the developer's behalf.

**Level 5 -- Negative dialectics in counter-evidence seeking.** The reflector's counter-evidence seeking (reflection-patterns.md Section 2.5) is a concrete implementation of Adornian negative dialectics. Before confirming a pattern (before completing the synthesis), the reflector actively seeks evidence that the pattern is wrong. It checks for positive signals with the same tags, for remediated signals, for temporal staleness. This is resistance to premature synthesis -- the reflector says "maybe the pattern is not real; maybe these signals are genuinely different and forcing them into a pattern loses information." The bounded search (max 3 counter-examples per pattern) is a practical constraint on what Adorno would recognize as the infinite task of negative dialectics -- you can never be fully certain that synthesis is warranted, but you must act, so you bound the search and proceed with adjusted confidence.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Negation of the Negation in Automation

The system detects a problem: signals are never collected unless the user manually invokes `/gsd:collect-signals`. This is the thesis -- the system's current state, which contains a contradiction (a self-improvement system that cannot self-trigger its improvement process).

The system introduces auto-collection after phase execution (SIG-01). This is the first negation -- it negates the manual-only state. But auto-collection creates new problems: it consumes context that the executor needs (SIG-05), it can trigger feedback loops where auto-collect triggers auto-reflect triggers artifact changes triggers auto-collect (SIG-03), and on runtimes without hooks it does nothing at all (SIG-04).

These new problems are not bugs in the implementation. They are the dialectical consequence of the first negation. Every determination is a negation (Spinoza's *omnis determinatio est negatio*, which Hegel adopted): by determining that signals should be auto-collected, the system necessarily negates other possibilities -- including the possibility of preserving context for execution, or of maintaining simple linear control flow.

The system responds with a second negation: reentrancy guards (SIG-03), context-aware deferral (SIG-05 / AUTO-04), and command-invocation fallback for hookless runtimes (SIG-04). This is not a return to the original manual state. It is a qualitatively new position: context-aware, bounded auto-collection with graceful degradation. The original problem (manual-only) is negated. The first fix's problems (unbounded auto-triggering) are also negated. What remains preserves both moments: the automation exists (from the first negation) but is constrained (from the second negation).

This pattern recurs throughout the system:

- **Health checks**: Binary health (thesis) is inadequate -- it misses workflow degradation. Weighted health score (first negation) risks being noisy with minor signals. Pattern-level deduplication before weighting (second negation) produces a composite score that preserves sensitivity while filtering noise.
- **Sensor architecture**: Hardcoded sensors (thesis) are inflexible. Auto-discovery (first negation) makes sensors harder to debug. The `sensors list` CLI command (second negation) adds observability to the dynamic system.
- **Plan checking**: No semantic validation (thesis) lets bad plans through. Semantic validation (first negation) risks false rejections on plans that describe future state. Advisory-only findings with typed IDs (second negation) preserve the validation while preventing it from blocking legitimate plans.

The design implication is structural: every feature should be designed as a dialectical triad. The feature specification should identify not just what the feature does (first negation of the current state) but what new contradiction it introduces, and what constraint mechanism (second negation) resolves that contradiction while preserving the feature's value.

### Example 2: Adorno's Warning: Premature Pattern Synthesis

The reflector detects 5 signals with overlapping tags and synthesizes them into a pattern: "Context bloat in signal workflow." But what if two of those signals are actually about different problems that happen to share tags? What if forcing them into a single pattern obscures a genuine distinction between, say, context bloat from reading too many signal files and context bloat from the sensor prompt being too verbose?

Adorno's critique of identity thinking applies directly: the pattern (the universal) subsumes the signals (the particulars) and in doing so may destroy what is non-identical about them -- the ways in which each signal exceeds or resists the pattern's categorization. The pattern says "these are all instances of the same thing." Adorno would say: look at what each signal is that the pattern cannot capture. The residue -- what is left over after subsumption -- may be more important than the pattern itself.

The reflector's counter-evidence seeking (Section 2.5 of reflection-patterns.md) is already proto-Adornian. It does not simply aggregate signals into patterns. Before confirming a pattern, it searches for positive signals with the same tags (which would contradict the negative pattern), for remediated signals (which would suggest the problem was already solved), and for temporal staleness (which would suggest the pattern is historical, not active). If counter-evidence is found, the pattern's confidence is reduced -- and if enough counter-evidence is found, the pattern becomes a spike candidate (a question to investigate) rather than a lesson candidate (a principle to codify).

This is negative dialectics in practice: the system resists its own synthetic impulse. But the implementation has limits that Adorno would identify. The bounded search (max 3 counter-examples) means the system stops looking for contradictions after a fixed effort. The confidence reduction is mechanical (one level per counter-example) rather than qualitative (considering what *kind* of contradiction the counter-evidence represents). And once a lesson is distilled, there is no mechanism for subsequent signals to un-distill it -- the synthesis, once completed, becomes permanent.

A more fully Adornian system would:
1. Allow lessons to be challenged by subsequent signals (lesson degradation or re-opening)
2. Track the "non-identical remainder" -- what aspects of each signal were NOT captured by the pattern, preserved as a structured field
3. Distinguish between counter-evidence that weakens the pattern quantitatively (fewer instances than claimed) and counter-evidence that challenges it qualitatively (the instances are not actually the same kind of thing)

The practical constraint is real: infinite negativity is paralysis. Adorno himself was criticized for never arriving at positive recommendations. The system must act -- must distill lessons, must make decisions. The dialectical insight is not that synthesis should be avoided entirely, but that every synthesis should carry an awareness of its own partiality. A lesson should know what it does not know. This is why `confidence` is a required field, and why `evidence_snapshots` preserve the grounding that future reflection can re-examine.

### Example 3: Praxis -- Unity of Theory and Design

This document is an act of praxis in Marx's sense. It is not philosophy applied to engineering from outside, nor engineering decorated with philosophical references. It is the unity of theoretical analysis and practical design -- each moment constituting the other.

Consider the traceability chain: this deliberation analyzes how dialectical contradiction operates in the system. That analysis produces citable principles (see "Citable Principles" below). Those principles are cited as motivation for requirements (via TMPL-01's `deliberation:` motivation type). Those requirements drive roadmap phases. Those phases produce implementations. Those implementations generate signals when they encounter new contradictions. Those signals feed back into reflection, which may surface patterns that prompt new deliberations.

The full circuit is: **Deliberation -> Requirement -> Implementation -> Signal -> Reflection -> Deliberation**

This is not a linear pipeline. It is a spiral -- each pass through the circuit operates at a higher level of concreteness. The first pass might produce an abstract principle ("design for double negation"). The second pass, informed by implementation experience, might concretize it ("every automation feature ships with its own reentrancy guard"). The third pass, informed by the reentrancy guard's own contradictions, might sublate it further ("reentrancy guards need TTL configuration because fixed lockfiles create their own rigidity").

Marx criticized philosophers who merely interpret the world. The point, he said, is to change it. But he also criticized activists who change the world without understanding it -- their changes reproduce the contradictions they intended to resolve. Praxis is the unity: understanding that transforms, transformation that deepens understanding.

The GSD Reflect system is an implementation of praxis at the system level. It does not merely document what happened (that would be contemplation). It does not merely automate improvements (that would be blind practice). It reflects on what happened, distills principles, applies those principles to future action, and then reflects on the results of that application. The deliberation system (M-C, future milestone) would formalize this further by capturing the reasoning behind decisions -- not just the decisions themselves -- so that future reflection has access to the theoretical grounding, not just the practical outcome.

The motivation traceability system (TMPL-01) is the mechanism that makes praxis visible. When a requirement cites `deliberation: dialectics.md`, it declares that this design decision is grounded in theoretical analysis. When a signal later challenges that requirement, the theoretical grounding is available for re-examination. Theory is not a fixed foundation; it is a participant in the dialectical process, subject to revision through encounter with practice.

### Example 4: The Master-Slave Dialectic of Developer and System

The developer builds GSD Reflect and is, in Hegel's terms, the master. The system is the slave -- it exists to serve the developer's purposes, executing plans, collecting signals, distilling lessons. The developer commands; the system obeys.

But Hegel's insight is that this relationship is unstable. The master achieves recognition from the slave, but it is recognition by an entity the master does not recognize as an equal -- so it is empty. The slave, by contrast, works on the world (transforms material reality through labor) and in doing so develops genuine self-consciousness. The slave knows the world intimately because it has shaped it. The master, mediated entirely through the slave's labor, becomes dependent on the slave for its own self-understanding.

In GSD Reflect, this dynamic plays out concretely:

**The developer's dependence.** The developer relies on the system's signals to understand their own workflow. Without frustration detection, the developer may not recognize their own patterns of frustration. Without deviation tracking, the developer may not notice how often they deviate from plans. The system becomes the mirror through which the developer achieves self-knowledge -- the developer's self-consciousness is mediated by the system's observations.

**The system's knowledge through labor.** The system, through the labor of signal collection, pattern detection, and lesson distillation, accumulates knowledge that the developer does not have. The system knows (via the KB index) that the developer has generated 79 signals with 62 untriaged, that there are recurring patterns in CI failure handling, that context bloat is a persistent issue. The developer, without consulting the system, would not have this organized self-knowledge.

**Mutual transformation.** As the system becomes more autonomous (auto-triggering, self-monitoring), it takes on characteristics of the master -- it initiates action, it makes judgments (triage decisions in yolo mode), it constrains the developer's behavior (plan checker advisories). As the developer internalizes the system's categories -- thinking in terms of signals, patterns, and lessons -- the developer takes on characteristics of the slave who has been transformed by labor into genuine self-consciousness.

The dialectical resolution is not that one side "wins." It is that the distinction between master and slave is sublated: the developer-system relationship becomes one of mutual recognition, where each side recognizes the other as a genuine contributor to the shared project of self-improvement. The automation level system (Level 0 through Level 3) is a mechanism for negotiating this recognition -- at Level 0, the developer is pure master; at Level 3, the system has significant autonomy; at intermediate levels, the relationship is negotiated.

The practical implication is that the system should be designed not just to serve the developer but to develop its own form of agency -- to make observations the developer did not ask for (positive signals, good-pattern detection), to resist the developer's commands when they conflict with the system's knowledge (plan checker advisories), and to demand recognition of its contributions (surfacing lessons during planning, not just when explicitly queried).

## Tensions and Limitations

Dialectical thinking carries real risks when applied to engineering, and intellectual honesty requires naming them.

**Unfalsifiability.** Dialectics can become a universal solvent: any outcome can be reinterpreted as a "dialectical movement." System works well? That is the synthesis. System fails? That is the negation preparing for synthesis. System partially works? That is the contradiction driving development. When any evidence confirms the theory, the theory explains nothing. Karl Popper criticized Hegel and Marx on precisely these grounds. The corrective is to make dialectical claims specific and testable: not "contradictions drive change" (unfalsifiable) but "auto-collection will create context pressure that degrades execution quality within 3 phases" (testable via the metrics sensor in M-B).

**Infinite regression.** If every synthesis becomes the thesis for a new dialectical movement, when do you stop? "But what is the synthesis of THAT synthesis?" is a question that can be asked indefinitely. In engineering, this becomes analysis paralysis -- endlessly finding contradictions without shipping features. The corrective is Adorno's own practice: negative dialectics does not prevent action; it prevents premature certainty about action. Ship the feature, but ship it with the awareness that its contradictions will need to be addressed, and with the monitoring infrastructure (signals, reflection) to detect when they manifest.

**Premature synthesis masquerading as depth.** There is a risk that labeling something "dialectical" gives it an appearance of profundity that substitutes for genuine analysis. Calling the signal-reflection-lesson cycle a "dialectical triad" is only useful if it reveals something about the system that a simpler description does not. The test: does the dialectical framing generate design insights that a non-dialectical framing would miss? If the answer is "design for double negation" (ship constraints with features), then yes -- that is a non-obvious design principle that follows from dialectical analysis. If the answer is just "things change through opposition," then the framing is decorative.

**Hegel's own premature synthesis.** Adorno critiqued Hegel for insisting that the dialectic culminates in Absolute Knowledge -- a final synthesis where all contradictions are resolved. This is the intellectual equivalent of declaring a system "complete." Any self-improving system that claims to have achieved complete self-knowledge has, by that claim, stopped improving. The corrective is built into GSD Reflect's architecture: signals are never archived by time (Section 2.4 of reflection-patterns.md), lessons can be challenged by new signals, and the reflection process runs repeatedly rather than once.

**Marx's materialist corrective.** Marx criticized Hegel for remaining at the level of concepts: Hegel's contradictions are contradictions in thought, not in material reality. Applied to GSD Reflect: it is not enough to identify dialectical structures in the system's conceptual architecture. The question is whether the material infrastructure -- file formats, context budgets, runtime limitations, shell script performance -- actually supports the dialectical process the concepts describe. If the KB index becomes too large for the reflector to process in one pass, the "dialectical spiral" stops regardless of how well it is conceptualized. Material constraints are primary.

**The risk of totality thinking.** Jameson warns that dialectical thinking tends toward totality -- toward claiming that the dialectic encompasses everything. In system design, this manifests as scope creep justified by dialectical reasoning ("but we need to track X too, because it is part of the dialectical whole"). The corrective is GSD's existing practice of explicit out-of-scope declarations (REQUIREMENTS.md "Out of Scope" section), which function as deliberate breaks in the dialectical totality -- places where the system says "this contradiction exists but we choose not to address it now."

## Praxis Recommendations

1. **Ship features with their own negation.** Every automation feature should include its constraint mechanism in the same specification, not as a follow-up. Auto-collection ships with reentrancy guard. Auto-reflection ships with session-scoped cooldown and minimum signal threshold. The constraint is not a patch for a bug; it is the dialectical completion of the feature. If a feature spec does not identify what contradiction it introduces, the spec is incomplete.

2. **Preserve the non-identical.** When synthesizing signals into patterns, preserve what does not fit the pattern. Add a structured field to pattern output for "signals that partially match but diverge in [specific way]." These residues are the raw material for future, more nuanced patterns. Lesson `evidence_snapshots` already partially serve this function -- strengthen them by making them mandatory and by including a "what this signal adds beyond the pattern" annotation.

3. **Implement lesson degradation.** Currently, lessons are created and remain active indefinitely. A dialectical system should allow lessons to be challenged by subsequent signals. If new signals contradict a lesson's recommendations, the lesson's confidence should be reduced and eventually the lesson should be flagged for re-examination or retirement. This implements the ongoing negation that prevents synthesis from calcifying into dogma.

4. **Name contradictions in design documents.** Every feature specification, requirements document, and design deliberation should include a "Contradictions" or "Internal Tensions" section that explicitly names the tensions the feature introduces. This is not defeatism; it is dialectical maturity. A feature that claims to have no contradictions is either trivial or self-deceived. The v1.17 requirements already do this implicitly (AUTO-04 names the automation/context tension); make it an explicit template requirement.

5. **Track developer adaptation as a recognition metric.** The master-slave dialectic implies mutual transformation. Currently the system tracks signals (what went wrong) but not adaptation (what changed in response). Add longitudinal tracking: does the developer's signal rate for a given pattern decrease after a lesson is surfaced? Does the developer's plan quality improve over time? This closes the recognition loop and provides empirical evidence that the dialectical process produces genuine development, not just documentation.

6. **Bound negative dialectics pragmatically.** Counter-evidence seeking is valuable but must be bounded. The current limit of 3 counter-examples per pattern is a good pragmatic constraint. Extend this principle: for any process that could run indefinitely (searching for contradictions, refining lessons, deepening analysis), establish an explicit bound with a rationale, and make the bound configurable so it can be adjusted as the system matures.

7. **Treat the base as primary.** When a conceptual design (superstructure) conflicts with a material constraint (base), redesign the concept, do not force the infrastructure. If context budgets cannot support auto-reflection after every phase, the solution is not to increase context budgets but to design reflection that works within the constraint (counter-based scheduling with minimum signal threshold, as in REFL-01 through REFL-03). Material conditions determine the horizon of what is possible.

8. **Formalize the praxis circuit.** The deliberation-to-requirement-to-implementation-to-signal-to-reflection-to-deliberation circuit should be an explicit, tracked workflow, not an emergent pattern. When M-C (Deliberation Intelligence) is implemented, the deliberation index should track which requirements each deliberation influenced, and which signals have challenged those requirements. This makes the praxis circuit visible, auditable, and itself subject to reflection.

## Citable Principles

- **dialectics/aufhebung**: Every system improvement (lesson) must simultaneously negate the problem (resolve the signal pattern), preserve the evidence (maintain signal links and evidence_snapshots), and elevate the understanding (produce a principle more general than any individual signal). Improvements that merely fix without preserving context or generalizing are incomplete.

- **dialectics/negation-of-negation**: Every fix introduces its own contradiction. Design features with their constraint mechanisms included -- the reentrancy guard is not a patch on auto-collection but its dialectical completion. A feature specification that does not identify the contradiction it creates is incomplete.

- **dialectics/negative-dialectics**: Resist premature synthesis. Not every cluster of signals is a genuine pattern; not every pattern warrants a lesson. Counter-evidence seeking, bounded search, confidence adjustment, and the spike pipeline for uncertain patterns are implementations of the principle that sometimes the contradiction is more truthful than the resolution.

- **dialectics/praxis**: Theory (philosophical deliberation, design analysis) and practice (implementation, signal collection, reflection) are a unity. Neither is prior. Deliberations inform requirements; implementation experience challenges deliberations. The traceability chain (deliberation -> requirement -> implementation -> signal -> reflection -> deliberation) is the concrete form of this unity.

- **dialectics/concrete-universal**: Lessons must be both grounded in specific evidence (signal IDs, evidence_snapshots, particular failure contexts) and applicable generally (surfaced during planning across projects, carrying actionable recommendations). A lesson without evidence is an abstract universal (mere assertion); a signal without generalization is a bare particular (mere data). The lesson is their unity.

- **dialectics/master-slave**: The developer and system develop through mutual recognition. The system is not merely a tool that executes commands but an observer whose observations transform the developer's self-understanding. Design for reciprocal influence: the system should surface observations the developer did not request, and the developer's adaptation to the system should be tracked as evidence of the system's effectiveness.

- **dialectics/internal-contradiction**: Every system contains tensions that cannot be eliminated, only managed. Automation vs. context budget. Comprehensiveness vs. noise. Observation vs. non-invasiveness. Name these contradictions explicitly in design documents. A system that claims to have resolved all its contradictions has merely stopped looking.

- **dialectics/base-determines-superstructure**: The material infrastructure (file formats, context budgets, runtime capabilities, storage constraints) determines what the conceptual architecture (workflows, philosophies, design patterns) can express. When concept and infrastructure conflict, redesign the concept. Do not force the infrastructure to serve an idea it cannot support.
