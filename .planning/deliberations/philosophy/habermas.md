# Philosophical Deliberation: Discourse Ethics & Communicative Rationality

**Tradition:** Critical Theory / Discourse Ethics
**Key thinkers:** Jurgen Habermas (The Theory of Communicative Action, 1981; Moral Consciousness and Communicative Action, 1983), Karl-Otto Apel
**Created:** 2026-03-03
**Status:** Active

## Core Ideas

Habermas's project, developed across four decades of work, is an attempt to ground rationality not in the isolated subject (as in Descartes or Kant) but in the structure of communication itself. His central claim is that language use oriented toward mutual understanding -- what he calls **communicative action** -- presupposes validity claims that speakers implicitly raise and hearers implicitly evaluate. These presuppositions are not optional features of communication; they are constitutive of what it means to communicate at all. From this analysis of communication, Habermas derives a theory of ethics, a diagnosis of modernity, and a framework for evaluating the legitimacy of social institutions. All four are relevant to the design of a self-improving system that aspires to deliberation.

### Communicative vs. Strategic Action

Habermas distinguishes two fundamental orientations of action. **Communicative action** is oriented toward reaching understanding (Verstandigung). The speaker raises claims and is prepared to redeem them with reasons; the hearer can accept, reject, or challenge them. The outcome is not predetermined -- it emerges from the exchange itself. **Strategic action** is oriented toward achieving predetermined outcomes. The speaker treats the hearer as a means to an end, using language instrumentally -- to persuade, manipulate, or coerce rather than to genuinely understand.

The distinction is not about politeness or intent in the psychological sense. It is structural. In communicative action, the validity of a claim is settled by the force of the better argument. In strategic action, it is settled by the power, resources, or rhetorical skill of the speaker. A system that presents predetermined conclusions as though they emerged from genuine deliberation is engaged in strategic action disguised as communicative action -- what Habermas calls **systematically distorted communication**.

### The Three Validity Claims

Every communicative utterance implicitly raises three validity claims:

1. **Truth (propositional):** The factual content of the claim corresponds to reality. "The CI pipeline has been failing for 23 days" is a truth claim that can be verified against the actual CI logs.

2. **Rightness (normative):** The claim is appropriate given the shared norms of the social context. "We should prioritize fixing CI over new features" is a normative claim that appeals to shared standards about what matters.

3. **Sincerity (expressive):** The speaker genuinely means what they say -- their utterance accurately expresses their actual beliefs, intentions, or feelings. "I believe this architecture is sound" is a sincerity claim that the speaker is not dissembling.

When communication proceeds smoothly, all three claims are tacitly accepted. When one is challenged, discourse shifts to that domain: empirical investigation for truth claims, moral-practical discourse for normative claims, and therapeutic discourse for sincerity claims. The key insight is that *all three are always operative*. A factual statement that is true but normatively inappropriate ("the code works but violates every convention we agreed on") does not satisfy communicative rationality. Neither does a normatively appropriate statement that is factually false.

### The Ideal Speech Situation

For discourse to yield genuinely valid results -- results that all participants could rationally accept -- Habermas argues it must approximate what he calls the **ideal speech situation**. This is not a concrete goal but a counterfactual presupposition: anyone who enters discourse seriously is already presupposing these conditions, whether or not they obtain:

1. **No coercion:** Outcomes are determined by the force of the better argument, not by threats, incentives, or power differentials.
2. **Inclusivity:** All affected parties have the opportunity to participate. No one whose interests are at stake is excluded.
3. **Equality:** All participants have equal opportunity to raise and challenge claims. No one's contributions are systematically privileged or suppressed.
4. **Sincerity:** Participants express their actual beliefs and intentions, not strategic positions designed to manipulate.

These conditions are never fully met in practice. That is not the point. The point is that they function as a *critical standard* against which actual discourse can be evaluated. When we notice that a "consensus" was reached because one party had more power, or because affected parties were absent, or because relevant objections were suppressed, we are invoking the ideal speech situation as a norm -- even if we have never heard of Habermas.

### Discourse Ethics

From the ideal speech situation, Habermas derives a principle of moral validity: **(U) A norm is valid only if all affected persons could agree to it in a rational discourse.** This is the universalization principle of discourse ethics. It does not say that people will agree; it says that a norm's validity depends on whether they *could* agree under conditions approximating the ideal speech situation. Apel formulated a complementary principle: the very act of argumentation presupposes norms (sincerity, non-coercion, inclusivity) that cannot be denied without performative self-contradiction -- you cannot argue against the norms of argumentation without presupposing them.

### Lifeworld and System

Habermas distinguishes two mechanisms of social coordination. The **lifeworld** (Lebenswelt) is the background of shared understandings, cultural knowledge, social norms, and personal competences that makes communication possible. It is the taken-for-granted context within which communicative action occurs. The **system** comprises institutions that coordinate action through non-communicative media -- money (the economy) and power (the state). System mechanisms are efficient precisely because they bypass understanding: a market transaction does not require the participants to reach mutual understanding about values, only to agree on a price.

### Colonization of the Lifeworld

Habermas's central diagnosis of modernity is that system mechanisms -- efficiency, measurability, instrumental rationality -- progressively invade and colonize domains of life that properly require communicative coordination. When education is reduced to measurable outcomes, when healthcare is driven by profit metrics, when relationships are mediated by platforms optimizing engagement -- the lifeworld is colonized. The pathology is not that systems exist (they are necessary for complex societies) but that system logic displaces communicative rationality in domains where understanding, not efficiency, should govern.

## Relevance to GSD Reflect

Habermas's framework poses a challenge to GSD Reflect that is more fundamental than those raised by the other philosophical traditions in this series. Pragmatism asks whether the system's knowledge is warranted. Falsificationism asks whether it is testable. Cybernetics asks what happens when it observes itself. Habermas asks whether the system is *capable of genuine discourse at all* -- and the honest answer may be that it is not.

**The automation paradox is the central issue.** GSD Reflect aspires to automated deliberation -- multi-agent systems that argue from different perspectives, weigh evidence, and reach conclusions. But Habermas would immediately ask: is this communicative action or strategic action? The answer is uncomfortable. An automated system cannot have genuine communicative intent. It cannot sincerely mean what it says. It cannot be motivated by the desire to reach understanding rather than to produce output. Every act of an AI system is, in Habermas's terms, strategic -- it is oriented toward producing a predetermined type of output (a signal, a lesson, a recommendation), not toward understanding.

This does not mean automated deliberation is worthless. It means it is a *simulation* of communicative action, not an instance of it. The question is whether the simulation preserves enough of the structural features of genuine discourse to produce results that approximate what a genuine discourse would yield. This is an empirical question, not a philosophical one, and the answer depends on design.

**Signal validity is a direct application.** Every signal implicitly makes all three validity claims. A signal reporting "CI pipeline failed on 5 consecutive pushes" claims truth (this actually happened), rightness (this is worth reporting -- it matters), and sincerity (the sensor is reporting honestly, not gaming metrics or generating false positives). Making these claims explicit improves signal quality: a sensor that separates its factual observation ("CI failed") from its normative judgment ("this matters because...") from its confidence assessment ("based on these data sources...") produces more auditable, challengeable output. Currently, signals bundle all three claims into a single narrative. Unbundling them enables targeted challenge -- a triager can accept the truth claim while questioning the normative claim ("yes it failed, but it was a known flaky test").

**The automation level system is a consent mechanism.** Discourse ethics holds that norms are valid only if all affected parties could accept them in rational discourse. The automation level system (0-3) is precisely this: the user consents, in advance, to how much the system acts autonomously. Level 0 (manual) means the system acts only when explicitly commanded -- maximum user consent required. Level 3 (auto) means the system acts on its own within defined thresholds -- broad pre-consent. The legitimacy of any automated action depends on whether it falls within the scope of the user's consented level. An action that exceeds the consented level -- the system auto-triggering at level 2 when the user configured level 1 -- is a violation of the discourse-ethical compact, regardless of whether the action was beneficial.

**The reflector's status as a discourse participant is questionable.** When the reflector distills lessons from a set of signals, it performs something that *looks like* discourse -- weighing evidence, considering counter-evidence, forming conclusions. But it is a monologue, not a dialogue. No one challenges the reflector's claims. No affected party participates. The "conclusions" are not tested against dissent. Habermas would call this a *monological* exercise dressed in the language of deliberation. The multi-agent deliberation design (where different agents argue different perspectives) attempts to address this by creating structural dissent -- but the agents are generated by the same system, share the same training, and operate under the same optimization pressure. Their "disagreement" may be performative rather than genuine.

**Cross-project knowledge transfer raises consent problems.** When a lesson from Project A is surfaced in Project B, a communicative act occurs: the system tells the Project B developer "here is something relevant from another context." But the Project B developer was not a participant in the discourse that produced the lesson. They had no opportunity to challenge its premises, question its evidence, or reject its normative framing. The lesson arrives as a fait accompli -- take it or leave it. Habermas's (U) principle asks: could the Project B developer have agreed to this norm in rational discourse? If the lesson is sufficiently general ("always run tests before merging"), probably yes. If it is context-specific ("use path.resolve instead of path.join for installer paths"), the answer is less clear -- the recommendation may reflect Project A's specific constraints, not universal practice.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: The Deliberation Design Conversation Through Habermas's Lens

The conversation that produced this series of philosophical deliberations -- and the broader conversation about v1.17 design -- can itself be analyzed through Habermas's framework. The analysis reveals a pattern of alternation between communicative and strategic moments.

**Communicative moments:** When the conversation explored "what should v1.17 prioritize?" without a predetermined answer, it was approximating communicative action. The question was genuinely open. Multiple perspectives were considered (automation-first vs. sensor-first vs. deliberation-first). The eventual decision (automation-first, with sensors as a separate foundational phase) emerged from the exchange rather than being predetermined. The validity claims were examined: the factual claim that "CI failures went undetected for 23 days" was verified against git history; the normative claim that "this matters more than new features" was argued on grounds of system integrity; the sincerity claim was not in question (there was no reason for strategic dissembling in a single-developer-plus-AI context).

**Strategic moments:** When the conversation shifted to "how should we implement the automation level resolver?" the orientation shifted to strategic. The goal was predetermined (implement the resolver), and the discussion was about means, not ends. This is not a criticism -- strategic action is appropriate when the normative questions have already been settled communicatively. The problem arises when strategic framing is applied to questions that are still genuinely open.

**Where validity claims went unexamined:** The normative claim that "the system should never make the same mistake twice" (the core value statement) was never subjected to discourse. It was accepted as a premise, not argued for. Habermas would note that this norm shapes the entire system design -- every sensor, every lesson, every verification mechanism serves this norm -- but it has never been explicitly justified to all affected parties. What if the norm is wrong? What if some mistakes are worth repeating because the cost of prevention exceeds the cost of recurrence? What if "never" is too strong -- what if "rarely" is the achievable and appropriate standard? These questions were never raised because the norm functions as lifeworld background, not as a discursive claim.

**The most significant unexamined claim** is the assumption that philosophical frameworks from the human domain of social interaction apply to human-AI system design. This entire deliberation series treats Habermas, Dewey, Popper, and others as relevant authorities. But their work concerns human communication, human knowledge, human social institutions. Applying their frameworks to an AI system is itself a normative move that has not been justified through discourse. It could be a productive analogy, or it could be a category error. The pragmatist response is: "try it and see if it produces useful design insights." The Habermasian response would be: "who consented to this framing, and were alternative framings considered?" The honest answer is that this framing was proposed by the developer and accepted without challenge -- a mild case of systematically distorted communication, where the power differential (the developer decides, the AI complies) prevented genuine discourse about whether the framing itself is appropriate.

### Example 2: Designing a Discourse-Ethically Valid Multi-Agent Deliberation

If GSD Reflect implements multi-agent deliberation -- multiple AI agents arguing from different perspectives to reach conclusions about system improvements -- what would a discourse-ethically valid design look like? Habermas's conditions provide specific constraints.

**Condition 1: No coercion.** In a multi-agent system, "coercion" translates to structural bias. If the orchestrator frames the question ("Should we add a CI sensor?"), the framing constrains the possible answers. If one agent gets more tokens or is given the "last word," its position is structurally privileged. A discourse-ethical design would:
- Present the question as genuinely open: "What should we do about undetected CI failures?" rather than "Should we add a CI sensor?"
- Give all agents equal token budgets and equal positional opportunity (rotate who speaks first and last)
- Prevent the orchestrator from filtering or reframing agent outputs before synthesis

**Condition 2: Inclusivity.** All affected perspectives must be represented. For a question about CI sensor design, relevant perspectives include: the developer (whose workflow will change), the system's resource constraints (context budget), the existing sensor architecture (compatibility), the user base (if the tool is distributed), and future maintainability. A discourse-ethical design would:
- Explicitly enumerate the affected perspectives before deliberation begins
- Assign at least one agent to each perspective
- Include a "devil's advocate" agent whose role is to challenge the emerging consensus
- Include a "silence audit" at the end: "What perspectives were not represented?"

**Condition 3: Equality.** No agent's contribution should be systematically weighted higher. But multi-agent systems often have an "arbiter" or "synthesizer" agent that integrates the others' outputs. This agent has disproportionate power -- it decides what to keep and what to discard. A discourse-ethical design would:
- Make the synthesis transparent: the synthesizer must cite which arguments from which agents it accepted and which it rejected, with reasons
- Allow agents to challenge the synthesis: a second round where agents can object to how their positions were characterized
- Never let the synthesizer introduce new arguments not raised by the deliberating agents

**Condition 4: Sincerity.** This is where the framework breaks down most visibly. AI agents cannot be sincere in Habermas's sense. They do not have beliefs to accurately express. They generate text that matches patterns in their training data and instruction set. A "devil's advocate" agent does not genuinely believe the position it argues -- it is performing a role. This is not sincerity; it is theater.

**The honest design conclusion:** A multi-agent deliberation system can satisfy conditions 1-3 structurally, but it cannot satisfy condition 4 at all. This means its results do not have the legitimacy that genuine discourse would confer. The system should therefore:
- Present deliberation results as "structured analysis from multiple perspectives," not as "conclusions reached through deliberation"
- Always surface the deliberation to the human user for genuine discursive validation
- Never auto-act on deliberation results at any automation level -- the discourse-ethical deficit means the human must close the loop

This is a strong constraint and it conflicts with the automation-maximalist direction of v1.17. The tension is genuine. A Habermasian design would cap multi-agent deliberation at automation level 2 (prompt) at most, never level 3 (auto), because the results lack the discursive legitimacy required for autonomous action.

## Tensions and Limitations

### 1. The Impossibility of Ideal Speech in Automated Systems

The ideal speech situation requires that outcomes be determined by the force of the better argument. But in an automated system, there is no "better argument" in Habermas's sense -- there is only pattern matching, token prediction, and instruction following. An AI agent does not evaluate arguments by their rational force; it generates text that is statistically likely given its training and context. Two AI agents "debating" are not engaged in argumentation -- they are generating complementary text sequences. The "winner" of a multi-agent deliberation is not the agent with the better argument but the agent whose output the synthesizer's pattern matching favors.

**Mitigation:** Accept that automated deliberation is a heuristic, not a discourse. Its value lies not in the legitimacy of its conclusions (which have no discursive legitimacy) but in the breadth of considerations it surfaces. A multi-agent system that generates arguments from five perspectives gives the human user more material to reason with than a single-agent system that generates one perspective. The value is in the *input to human discourse*, not in the *output of automated discourse*. Design the system to produce inputs, not conclusions.

### 2. AI Cannot Have Genuine Communicative Intent

Habermas's entire framework rests on the distinction between communicative and strategic action, which depends on the speaker's orientation -- toward understanding or toward success. AI systems have no orientation. They do not intend to reach understanding; they do not intend to achieve success. They generate outputs. Attributing communicative intent to an AI system is a category error, and building a system on the assumption that AI agents can participate in discourse as genuine interlocutors is architecturally unsound.

**Mitigation:** Reframe the AI's role. The AI is not a *participant* in discourse; it is a *tool* for discourse. A calculator does not "argue" that 2+2=4; it provides a result that participants in a discourse can use. Similarly, an AI agent that generates "here are three arguments against adding a CI sensor" is not arguing against the CI sensor -- it is providing discursive material that a human participant can evaluate. The system's design language should reflect this: agents "generate perspectives," they do not "argue"; the synthesizer "structures the analysis," it does not "reach a conclusion"; the output is "material for decision," not "a decision."

### 3. Whether Discourse Ethics Applies to Human-AI Interaction at All

Habermas's (U) principle -- norms are valid only if all affected parties could accept them in rational discourse -- presupposes that the affected parties are capable of rational discourse. An AI system is not. It cannot accept or reject norms; it can only process them. The automation level system treats the user's configuration as consent, but genuine consent requires understanding what one is consenting to. When a user sets automation level 3, do they understand all the actions the system might take? Have they considered the edge cases? Habermas would require that the user could accept each specific automated action in rational discourse -- not just blanket-consent to a level.

**Mitigation:** Make the scope of consent concrete. Instead of "automation level 3 means the system auto-triggers," specify exactly what auto-triggering means for each feature: "signal collection will run after every phase, consuming approximately N tokens and adding M seconds to phase transitions." This transforms abstract consent into informed consent. It also means the automation level documentation is not just a feature description -- it is a consent instrument, and it must be written with the clarity and completeness that implies.

### 4. The Performative Contradiction of Critiquing Automation Using Habermas

There is a performative tension in using Habermas's framework to design a better automated system. Habermas's work is a sustained argument that communicative rationality -- human understanding through dialogue -- is the proper basis for social coordination, and that replacing it with system mechanisms (efficiency, automation) is pathological. Using his framework to *improve automation* is like using Marx to design a better factory -- technically possible, but in tension with the spirit of the project. A fully Habermasian design might conclude: do not automate deliberation at all; keep it human.

**Mitigation:** Treat Habermas as a critical voice, not a design manual. His framework is most valuable not for telling us how to build automated deliberation, but for telling us what automated deliberation *cannot* legitimately do. The constraints he imposes -- sincerity is impossible for AI, consent requires informed understanding, discourse requires genuine interlocutors -- are real constraints, and respecting them produces a more honest system. The honest system says: "I generated this analysis; a human must validate it." The dishonest system says: "deliberation concluded that X."

### 5. The Monological Nature of AI-Generated "Deliberation"

Even in a multi-agent setup, all agents share the same underlying model, training data, and optimization objective. Their "disagreement" is a function of different prompts, not different worldviews. Habermas would recognize this as monological thinking distributed across multiple speakers -- the appearance of dialogue without its substance. Genuine discourse requires genuine otherness: participants who bring irreducibly different perspectives, experiences, and commitments. AI agents prompted to "argue for position X" and "argue for position Y" do not bring genuine otherness; they bring the same model's interpretation of what those positions entail.

**Mitigation:** Acknowledge the monological limitation and design around it. Multi-agent deliberation is useful not because it achieves genuine discourse but because it forces the system to *articulate* positions that a single agent might suppress or overlook. The value is in the articulation, not in the debate. Supplement automated deliberation with genuine discourse: bring the results to the human user, or to multiple human users with genuinely different perspectives. The automated system prepares the ground for discourse; it does not conduct it.

## Praxis Recommendations

1. **Separate validity claims in signal schema.** Restructure signals to distinguish `observation` (truth claim: what the sensor detected, with evidence), `significance` (normative claim: why this matters, what norm it violates), and `reliability` (sincerity analog: data source quality, confidence level, known false positive patterns). This enables targeted challenge during triage: a triager can accept the observation while questioning its significance, or accept the significance while questioning the reliability. *Cite: habermas/validity-claims.*

2. **Frame automation levels as informed consent instruments.** The automation level documentation should specify, for each feature at each level, exactly what the system will do, what resources it will consume, and what the user is consenting to. This is not just UX documentation -- it is a consent instrument in the discourse-ethical sense. A user who sets level 3 without understanding what level 3 entails has not given informed consent. The `init_prompts` in the feature manifest should present concrete examples, not abstract descriptions. *Cite: habermas/discourse-ethics, habermas/informed-consent.*

3. **Never auto-act on multi-agent deliberation results.** If multi-agent deliberation is implemented, cap its automation level at 2 (prompt). The system may generate and present deliberation results, but it must not act on them without human validation. The discourse-ethical deficit of automated deliberation -- no genuine sincerity, no genuine otherness -- means its results lack the legitimacy required for autonomous action. The human closes the discursive loop by evaluating, accepting, or rejecting the analysis. *Cite: habermas/ideal-speech, habermas/communicative-vs-strategic.*

4. **Label system outputs honestly.** When the system produces a recommendation, lesson, or deliberation result, label it as system-generated analysis, not as a conclusion of deliberation. The language matters: "the reflector identified a pattern suggesting X" (honest -- describes what the system did) vs. "reflection concluded that X" (dishonest -- attributes discursive agency to a pattern matcher). This prevents the system from colonizing the user's lifeworld with false authority. *Cite: habermas/colonization, habermas/communicative-vs-strategic.*

5. **Include a "silence audit" in deliberation design.** Any structured analysis -- whether single-agent reflection or multi-agent deliberation -- should conclude with an explicit statement of what perspectives were not considered, what stakeholders were not represented, and what validity claims were not examined. This is the inclusivity condition of the ideal speech situation translated into a checklist. Even if the audit cannot be exhaustive, making the attempt forces the system to acknowledge its own blind spots. *Cite: habermas/ideal-speech.*

6. **Treat cross-project lesson surfacing as a communicative act requiring validation.** When a lesson from Project A is surfaced in Project B, present it explicitly as a claim from another context: "In project X, developers found that Y. This may or may not apply here." Do not present it as universal advice. At automation levels 0-2, require explicit user acceptance before the lesson influences planning. At level 3, present the lesson but flag its foreign provenance in the plan. The Project B developer is a newly affected party who was not part of the original discourse. *Cite: habermas/discourse-ethics.*

7. **Design multi-agent deliberation for articulation, not consensus.** If multiple agents are used, their purpose should be to articulate a range of perspectives, not to reach agreement. The output should be a structured map of positions, arguments, and counter-arguments -- material for human judgment, not a synthetic conclusion. Do not include a "synthesizer" agent that produces a final recommendation; instead, present the positions side by side and let the human evaluate. This avoids the false legitimacy of automated consensus. *Cite: habermas/ideal-speech, habermas/communicative-vs-strategic.*

8. **Distinguish communicative from systemic parameters in configuration.** Some configuration parameters are legitimately systemic -- buffer sizes, timeout values, triage caps, context thresholds. These can be set algorithmically without user discourse. Others are normatively loaded -- what counts as a deviation, which signal types matter, how lessons are prioritized. These require communicative validation: the user should set them through informed decision, not accept defaults without understanding their implications. Mark configuration parameters as `systemic` or `normative` in the feature manifest to clarify which require user engagement. *Cite: habermas/lifeworld-system, habermas/colonization.*

## Citable Principles

- **habermas/communicative-vs-strategic**: Communicative action is oriented toward mutual understanding; strategic action is oriented toward predetermined outcomes. All automated system actions are inherently strategic. The system should never disguise strategic action as communicative -- when it produces recommendations, it should label them as system-generated analysis, not as conclusions of deliberation. Automated "deliberation" is a structured analysis technique, not genuine discourse.

- **habermas/validity-claims**: Every communicative act raises three validity claims: truth (factual accuracy), rightness (normative appropriateness), and sincerity (honest expression). Signals, lessons, and recommendations implicitly make all three. Making these claims explicit and separately challengeable improves the quality of the system's outputs and the user's ability to evaluate them.

- **habermas/ideal-speech**: Valid discourse presupposes no coercion, inclusivity of all affected parties, equality of participation, and sincerity. These conditions cannot be fully met in automated systems (sincerity is structurally impossible for AI), but the other three can be approximated structurally. Multi-agent deliberation should be designed to approximate conditions 1-3 while acknowledging the permanent deficit on condition 4.

- **habermas/discourse-ethics**: A norm is valid only if all affected parties could accept it in rational discourse. Automated actions are legitimate only within the scope of the user's informed consent (automation level). Actions that exceed the consented scope lack discursive legitimacy regardless of their practical benefit. Consent must be informed -- the user must understand what they are consenting to.

- **habermas/colonization**: System logic (efficiency, metrics, automation) must not displace communicative rationality in domains where understanding should govern. Health scores, signal counts, and automation statistics are instruments of discourse, not replacements for it. When metrics are surfaced, they must be accompanied by qualitative context that enables genuine understanding, not just numerical summary.

- **habermas/informed-consent**: Genuine consent requires understanding what one is consenting to. Automation level configuration is a consent instrument and must specify concrete consequences at each level for each feature. Abstract descriptions ("level 3 means auto") are insufficient. Users should be able to answer "what exactly will the system do without asking me?" before choosing a level.

- **habermas/lifeworld-system**: The lifeworld (shared background understanding) and the system (institutional coordination mechanisms) serve different functions and should not be conflated. CLAUDE.md and PROJECT.md are lifeworld documents -- they set interpretive context. config.json and automation parameters are system mechanisms -- they coordinate action. Changes to lifeworld documents are changes to shared understanding and deserve deliberative treatment, not routine patching.

- **habermas/monological-limitation**: Multi-agent AI deliberation is structurally monological -- all agents share the same model, training, and optimization objective. Their "disagreement" is prompted, not genuine. The value of multi-agent deliberation lies in articulating perspectives that a single agent might suppress, not in achieving genuine dialectical progress. Always supplement automated deliberation with human judgment to close the discursive gap.
