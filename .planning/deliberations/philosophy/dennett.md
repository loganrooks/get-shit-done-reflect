# Philosophical Deliberation: Intentional Systems Theory

**Tradition:** Philosophy of Mind / Cognitive Science
**Key thinkers:** Daniel Dennett (The Intentional Stance, 1987; Consciousness Explained, 1991; From Bacteria to Bach and Back, 2017)
**Created:** 2026-03-08
**Status:** Active

## Core Ideas

Daniel Dennett's work across five decades develops a distinctive approach to the philosophy of mind that is simultaneously deflationary and constructive. It is deflationary in that it refuses to treat consciousness, intentionality, and understanding as mysterious properties requiring special explanation. It is constructive in that it provides positive frameworks — the intentional stance, real patterns, competence without comprehension — that do genuine explanatory and predictive work. For the design of a self-improving system, Dennett's framework offers something none of the other philosophical traditions in this deliberation series can: a principled way to *dissolve* the question "does the system really understand?" rather than forcing an answer to it.

This matters for GSD Reflect because the system's central design tension — between phenomenological accounts that emphasize the irreducibility of human judgment and functionalist accounts that model cognition as information processing — threatens to paralyze design decisions. Every time the system is designed to "detect patterns," "learn lessons," or "make decisions," a philosophical objection looms: the system does not *really* detect, learn, or decide. Dennett's framework provides a way to use intentional vocabulary productively without making metaphysical commitments about the system's inner life.

### The Three Stances

Dennett distinguishes three predictive strategies — three ways of predicting a system's behavior — ordered by abstraction:

**The physical stance** predicts behavior from physical properties and laws. To predict where a baseball will land, use Newtonian mechanics. To predict when a capacitor will discharge, use circuit equations. The physical stance is maximally general (it works for everything) and maximally laborious (it requires knowing all the physical details).

**The design stance** predicts behavior from designed function, bypassing physical details. To predict what a calculator will output for 3+4, you do not need to trace electron flows through its circuits. You need to know it was designed to perform arithmetic. The design stance works when: (a) the system was designed, (b) you know the design, and (c) the system is functioning as designed. It fails when the system malfunctions — a broken calculator might output anything.

**The intentional stance** predicts behavior by attributing beliefs, desires, and rational agency. To predict a chess computer's next move, attribute to it the "belief" that its queen is threatened and the "desire" to protect it, then assume it will act "rationally" given these attributed states. The intentional stance works when: (a) the system is complex enough that the lower stances are impractical, (b) the system is well-designed enough to approximate rational agency, and (c) you can identify appropriate beliefs and desires to attribute.

The key insight is that these are *predictive strategies*, not metaphysical discoveries. Taking the intentional stance toward a chess computer does not commit you to the claim that the computer "really" has beliefs and desires. It commits you only to the claim that predicting its behavior *as if* it had beliefs and desires yields better predictions than the alternatives. The intentional stance is a tool, not a theory of consciousness.

For GSD Reflect, the three stances correspond to three ways of understanding the system:

- **Physical stance**: The system is a sequence of API calls to an LLM, returning token sequences that are parsed and stored. Accurate but useless for design.
- **Design stance**: The system is a pipeline with sensors, reflectors, and lesson engines designed to detect patterns and produce knowledge artifacts. This is how the system is currently understood — as a mechanism with designed functions.
- **Intentional stance**: The system "notices" patterns, "believes" certain interventions will help, "wants" to improve the workflow, and "decides" to propose changes. This vocabulary is predictively useful (it helps designers anticipate the system's behavior in novel situations) even if it is not literally true.

The current design operates entirely at the design stance: the system is a mechanism, its outputs are artifacts, its quality is measured by whether the mechanism functions as designed. Dennett's contribution is to ask: would adopting the intentional stance — designing AS IF the system has beliefs and goals — produce better design decisions than the design stance alone?

### The Intentional Stance in Detail

Dennett's central argument, developed across *The Intentional Stance* and *Brainstorms*, is that the question "does it REALLY have beliefs?" is malformed — or at least, less well-formed than it appears. Consider a thermostat. The thermostat "wants" to keep the room at 68°F and "believes" the current temperature is too high. This is the intentional stance applied to a trivially simple system. No one thinks the thermostat has phenomenal consciousness. But the intentional description is predictively useful: if you ask "what will the thermostat do next?" the answer "it will turn on the AC because it believes the room is too warm and wants it cooler" is more efficient than tracing the bimetallic strip's expansion coefficient.

Now move up the complexity ladder. A chess engine. A recommendation system. An LLM. At each step, the intentional stance becomes more useful (the system's behavior is harder to predict from the design stance alone) and more tempting to treat as literal (the system's outputs increasingly resemble the outputs of genuine believers). Dennett's claim is that there is no bright line — no point at which "as if" intentionality becomes "real" intentionality. The intentional stance either works or it does not, and its working is the only criterion available.

This is not eliminativism (the claim that beliefs do not exist). Dennett is clear that beliefs are real. But their reality is the reality of *patterns* — real regularities that are predictively useful — not the reality of *things* that can be found by opening up the system and looking inside. You cannot find a belief by examining neurons (or weights in a neural network). But you can find the *pattern* that the belief-attribution picks out: a reliable regularity in behavior that is best predicted by attributing that belief.

For GSD Reflect, this reframing is liberating. The question "does the system really detect patterns?" becomes: "is 'pattern detection' a good description of the regularity in the system's behavior?" If the system reliably produces outputs that correspond to genuine patterns in the signal data — if treating it as a pattern-detector yields better predictions about its outputs than treating it as a random text generator — then it *is* a pattern-detector in the only sense that matters for design. The metaphysical question about "real" detection dissolves.

### Real Patterns

Dennett's 1991 paper "Real Patterns" provides the ontological underpinning for the intentional stance. The argument proceeds by analogy with Conway's Game of Life. In the Game of Life, simple rules applied to a grid of cells produce complex emergent structures — gliders, oscillators, glider guns. These structures are *real patterns*: they pick out genuine regularities that are predictively useful and not reducible to descriptions at the cell level. You can predict that a glider will continue moving diagonally across the grid without tracking each cell's state. The pattern-level description is explanatorily autonomous — it captures a regularity that is real, even though it is not a "thing" at the physical level.

Dennett extends this to mental states. Beliefs, desires, and intentions are real patterns in behavior — regularities that are genuine, predictively useful, and not reducible to neuronal (or computational) descriptions. They are not mere fictions or useful pretenses. They are *real* — but real in the way that patterns are real, not in the way that particles are real.

Applied to GSD Reflect: the patterns that the reflection pipeline detects in the signal corpus may be *real patterns* in Dennett's sense. When the reflector identifies "fix-fix-fix chains correlate with missing test coverage," this may pick out a genuine regularity in the development process — a real pattern — even if the reflector does not "understand" the regularity in any phenomenological sense. The pattern is real if it has predictive power: does knowing about this pattern help predict future signal patterns? Does acting on it reduce future occurrences? If yes, the pattern is real, regardless of whether the system that detected it "comprehends" it.

This provides a criterion for evaluating reflection outputs that does not depend on resolving the consciousness question. A pattern is genuine (real) if it has predictive and practical utility. A pattern is spurious if it does not. This is an empirical question, testable through the system's own verification mechanisms (passive verification-by-absence, active outcome tracking). The reflector does not need to "understand" patterns to detect real ones — just as a telescope does not need to understand stars to reveal real ones.

### Competence Without Comprehension

In *From Bacteria to Bach and Back* (2017), Dennett develops his most direct contribution to the current AI debate. The core concept is captured in a slogan: "competence without comprehension." Natural selection produces organisms with remarkable competences — termites build elaborate mounds, beavers construct dams, cuckoos parasitize nests — without any comprehension of what they are doing or why. Evolution is the paradigm of competence without comprehension: it "discovers" elegant solutions to design problems without understanding the problems, the solutions, or the concept of discovery.

Dennett extends the principle beyond biology. Much of human cognition, he argues, is competence without comprehension. A native speaker of English can produce grammatically correct sentences without comprehending the rules of English grammar. A skilled carpenter can build a sturdy joint without comprehending the physics of stress distribution. Comprehension — the ability to explicitly represent and reason about what one is doing — is a late, rare, and partial achievement, layered on top of vast competences that operate without it.

LLMs are, arguably, the most dramatic example of competence without comprehension ever produced. GPT-4 can write working code, compose persuasive essays, solve logic puzzles, and generate novel analogies. It does so without (on most accounts) comprehending code, essays, logic, or analogy. It has extraordinary linguistic competence with (arguably) zero linguistic comprehension. Dennett himself has noted this parallel: LLMs are "zombie" competence engines — systems that do what comprehending agents do, without the comprehension.

For GSD Reflect, this concept directly addresses the design question. The system can competently detect patterns, generate hypotheses, and propose interventions — all without comprehending what patterns are, why they matter, or what "improvement" means. Design should *leverage the competence* (the system produces useful outputs) without *assuming the comprehension* (the system understands what it is producing or why it matters). This is not a deficiency to be fixed by better prompting or more sophisticated architecture. It is a fundamental characteristic of the system that design must accommodate.

Concretely: when the system proposes "CI should be a blocking gate," it is demonstrating competence (the proposal is reasonable given the signal data). It is not demonstrating comprehension (it does not understand what CI is, why blocking matters, or what the consequences of blocking would be for the developer's workflow). Governance mechanisms — the therefore test, deontic scorekeeping, developer engagement — are all responses to this gap between competence and comprehension. They ensure that competent outputs are evaluated by a comprehending agent before they become operational.

### Heterophenomenology

Dennett's method for studying consciousness — heterophenomenology — is directly applicable to the question of how to treat system reports. The method works as follows: take the subject's first-person reports seriously as *data* — as sincere expressions worth explaining — but do not take them as *incorrigible* (immune from error). When a subject says "I see red," the heterophenomenologist records this report and seeks to explain it, but does not assume that the subject's introspective report is a transparent window into their experience. The report is evidence, not revelation.

Applied to AI systems: when GSD Reflect reports "I detected a pattern of scope creep across phases 38-42," the heterophenomenological approach is to treat this as a functional report worth acting on — the system processed signals and produced a summary that may correspond to a genuine pattern. But the approach does not credit the report as infallible. The system might have "detected" a spurious pattern (correlation without causation). It might have "detected" a pattern that is an artifact of sensor design (the sensors are tuned to notice scope creep and under-tuned for other deviation types). It might have produced a report that sounds like pattern detection but is actually a restatement of the input signals in slightly different words.

Heterophenomenology provides a middle ground between two extremes: crediting system reports as genuine knowledge (the functionalist temptation) and dismissing them as meaningless text (the skeptic's temptation). The system's reports are sincere functional data — they reflect the system's processing of its inputs. They are worth engaging with. They are not authoritative.

This aligns naturally with the normative-status labeling recommended by the Sellars-Brandom framework: system outputs are "proposals" — functional reports that await evaluation by a comprehending agent. Heterophenomenology provides the epistemological justification for this design: we take the reports seriously enough to act on them (they might be right) but not so seriously that we skip evaluation (they might be wrong).

### Application and Displacement

Dennett might dissolve the phenomenology-vs-functionalism tension that runs through the deliberation series rather than forcing a resolution. The question "does the system really have practical intelligence?" is, in Dennett's framework, malformed. The well-formed question is: "does treating the system AS IF it has practical intelligence lead to better design than treating it as a pure mechanism?" This is an empirical, pragmatic question — not a metaphysical one.

Consider two design approaches:

**Design-stance approach (current):** The system is a mechanism. Its outputs are artifacts. Quality is measured by mechanism metrics (did the sensor fire? did the reflector run? did the lesson engine produce output?). The system has no intelligence, situated or otherwise. The developer supplies all judgment.

**Intentional-stance approach (proposed):** The system "notices" patterns, "believes" certain interventions will help, and "proposes" changes based on its "understanding" of the project. Quality is measured by the quality of the system's "proposals" — are its "beliefs" accurate? Are its "recommendations" helpful? Do its "predictions" come true? The developer evaluates the system's proposals as one evaluates a colleague's suggestions — with appropriate skepticism but genuine engagement.

The intentional-stance approach may produce better designs because it leads to asking better questions. Under the design stance, you ask: "is the mechanism functioning correctly?" (a binary question). Under the intentional stance, you ask: "are the system's proposals improving over time? Is it noticing the right things? Are its recommendations well-calibrated to this project?" (graduated questions that invite nuanced answers).

But the intentional stance carries risks that Dennett himself acknowledges. The predictive success of the intentional stance may encourage over-trust. If designers consistently treat the system AS IF it understands, they (and users) may begin to BELIEVE it understands. The "as if" qualifier erodes through habitual use. This is especially dangerous for a system that produces natural-language outputs — the very medium that humans associate with understanding. When the system writes "I recommend adding a CI check because the last five phases showed a pattern of bypassed failures," the natural reading is that the system *understands* what it is saying. Dennett's framework says: the intentional description is useful, but do not mistake the utility for understanding.

The design question becomes: build the system as if it has situated intelligence (give it the mechanisms of pattern detection, hypothesis generation, and proposal), but with governance constraints appropriate for a system whose intelligence is attributed, not verified. The therefore test, deontic scorekeeping, and developer-as-normative-gateway are all governance constraints designed for this intermediate status — a system that is treated as intentional for design purposes but not credited with comprehension for governance purposes.

Searle would object: syntax is not semantics, regardless of predictive utility. The Chinese Room argument insists that no amount of formal symbol manipulation constitutes understanding. But for *design purposes*, Searle's objection may be irrelevant. We are not designing for metaphysical truth. We are designing for outcomes. If the intentional stance produces better designs — more useful proposals, better-calibrated automation, more productive human-system interaction — then it is the right stance for design, regardless of whether the system "really" understands.

## Relevance to GSD Reflect

Dennett's framework addresses the deliberation series' central paralysis: the tension between designing the system as if it has intelligence and acknowledging that it probably does not. Every other framework has taken a position on one side or the other — Aristotle (it cannot have phronesis), Sellars (it is not in the space of reasons), the pragmatists (it does not matter as long as it works), the cybernetics tradition (second-order observation is what matters). Dennett offers a way through: use the intentional stance for design, the design stance for governance, and stop asking the metaphysical question about "real" understanding.

**The intentional stance as a design methodology.** Design the system as if it has beliefs (about patterns), desires (to improve the workflow), and rationality (it will act to achieve its goals given its beliefs). This leads to better design questions: What should the system believe? (What data should it attend to?) What should it desire? (What outcomes should it optimize for?) When does its rationality break down? (When do its proposals fail?) These questions are more productive than mechanism questions ("is the pipeline functioning?") because they invite graduated, context-sensitive answers.

**Competence without comprehension as the core design constraint.** The system has extraordinary competence (it can process signals, detect statistical regularities, generate natural-language proposals) without comprehension (it does not understand what signals mean, why patterns matter, or what "improvement" is). Every governance mechanism — the therefore test, commitment tracking, developer engagement — is a response to this gap. Design should leverage the competence (do not underestimate the system's ability to produce useful outputs) while constraining for the lack of comprehension (do not overestimate the system's ability to evaluate its own outputs).

**Real patterns as the criterion for reflection output quality.** The patterns detected by the reflector are genuine knowledge if and only if they are real patterns — regularities with predictive and practical utility. A pattern that predicts future signal behavior ("projects that skip CI tend to accumulate test rot") is a real pattern worth preserving as a lesson. A pattern that is an artifact of sensor design or statistical noise is not real, regardless of how confidently it is reported. The empirical test — does the pattern have predictive power? — is available through the system's own verification mechanisms and does not require resolving the consciousness question.

**Dissolving the phenomenology-functionalism tension for design purposes.** The deliberation series has generated productive philosophical tension between phenomenological accounts (Aristotle's phronesis, Gadamer's hermeneutic circle, Habermas's lifeworld) and functionalist accounts (cybernetics, Bayesian epistemology, error statistics). Dennett dissolves the tension: for design purposes, the question is not whether the system "really" perceives, understands, or judges. The question is whether treating it as if it does produces better designs. This is a pragmatic question that can be answered empirically — by building both designs and comparing outcomes — rather than a metaphysical question that paralyzes design decisions.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: The Intentional Stance Applied to System Design Decisions

Consider the design of the signal triage mechanism. Under the current design-stance approach, the question is: "what rules should the triage mechanism follow?" This leads to: if signal severity is high and confidence is strong, auto-triage as actionable; if severity is low and confidence is weak, auto-triage as noise; otherwise, prompt the developer.

Under the intentional-stance approach, the question becomes: "what should the system *believe* about each signal, and what should it *want* to do about it?" This leads to different and more productive questions:

- What should the system believe about a signal with high severity but weak confidence? (It should "believe" there might be a serious problem but "recognize" it is not sure.) This leads to a design where the output is: "Possible serious issue detected, but evidence is ambiguous. Confidence: 40%. Two alternative interpretations: [A] the pattern indicates genuine process failure; [B] the pattern is a sampling artifact from small phase count. Recommended: defer triage until more data is available."

- What should the system want to do when it detects a pattern it has seen before? (It should "want" to check whether the previous lesson worked.) This leads to a design where pattern recurrence triggers not just a new signal but a review of the prior lesson's effectiveness — did the previous intervention reduce the pattern? If not, the system should "doubt" the lesson and "propose" a revision.

- When should the system's rationality be expected to break down? (When the signal data is sparse, when the patterns cross multiple conceptual domains, when the project context has shifted significantly since the signals were collected.) This leads to a design where the system explicitly flags conditions under which its own proposals should be treated with extra skepticism.

None of these design insights require believing that the system "really" has beliefs and desires. They require only that the *designer* take the intentional stance toward the system to generate better design questions. The system itself remains a mechanism. But the mechanism is better designed because the designer asked intentional questions about it.

### Example 2: Competence Without Comprehension in Reflection Output

The v1.16 reflection report identified 13 patterns across 79 signals. Consider one: "Phase scope creep is the most frequent deviation, appearing in 29% of signals."

This output demonstrates competence: the system correctly counted signal types, identified the most frequent category, and reported it in clear prose. A human analyst examining the same data would likely reach the same conclusion. The system's *competence* in this task is genuine.

But the system lacks comprehension of what it reported. It does not understand:
- Why scope creep matters (it has consequences for timeline prediction, resource allocation, and developer cognitive load)
- Why scope creep might be acceptable in some phases (discovery-heavy phases) and problematic in others (execution-heavy phases)
- Whether the 29% figure is high or low relative to industry norms or this project's history
- Whether "scope creep" is the right category (some of what is labeled scope creep may be legitimate scope evolution)
- What should be done about it (the report describes but does not prescribe)

The gap between competence and comprehension is precisely the gap between the v1.16 reflection output (descriptive, pattern-counting) and what a genuine lesson would look like (prescriptive, context-sensitive, inferentially connected to action). The system competently counts and describes. It does not comprehendingly evaluate, contextualize, or recommend.

Dennett's framework says: leverage the competence. The system's ability to count patterns, detect statistical regularities, and summarize findings is genuinely useful. But do not design as if the comprehension is present. Do not take the system's description as an evaluation. Do not treat its count as a judgment about significance. Design the pipeline to add what the system's competence cannot provide: inferential connection (the therefore test), contextual evaluation (developer engagement), and predictive commitment (deontic scorekeeping). These governance mechanisms fill the comprehension gap without requiring the system to actually comprehend.

### Example 3: Real Patterns vs. Sensor Artifacts

Consider two patterns that the reflector might detect:

**Pattern A:** "Fix-fix-fix chains (3+ consecutive fix commits without test additions) correlate with phases that were planned without explicit test requirements."

**Pattern B:** "High-severity signals are more common in phases with more than 50 files modified."

Pattern A is likely a real pattern — it picks out a genuine regularity in the development process (inadequate test planning leads to iterative fixing) that has predictive power (future phases without test requirements can be expected to produce fix chains) and practical utility (adding test requirements to plans should reduce fix chains).

Pattern B may be a sensor artifact. High-severity signals may correlate with file count not because large phases are genuinely more problematic, but because the sensors are more likely to fire when more files are modified (more files = more opportunities for deviation detection). The pattern is real at the sensor level (sensors fire more in large phases) but not at the process level (large phases are not inherently more problematic). It is a pattern in the *observation instrument*, not in the *phenomenon being observed*.

Dennett's "real patterns" criterion helps distinguish these cases. A real pattern has predictive power *beyond the observation mechanism*. Pattern A predicts something about the development process (fix chains will occur when test planning is absent) that is testable by modifying the process (add test planning and check if fix chains decrease). Pattern B predicts only something about the observation mechanism (more files = more sensor firings) and does not predict anything about development quality that is independently verifiable.

The practical test: if you act on the pattern (change the process), does the outcome change? If adding test requirements to plans reduces fix chains, Pattern A is confirmed as real. If limiting phase size to 50 files does not reduce the actual incidence of problems (only the number of sensor firings), Pattern B is confirmed as a sensor artifact.

## Tensions and Limitations

### 1. The Chinese Room Objection

Searle's Chinese Room argument (1980) directly challenges the intentional stance's applicability to AI systems. Searle imagines a person in a room who follows syntactic rules to manipulate Chinese characters, producing outputs indistinguishable from a native speaker's, while understanding nothing. Searle's conclusion: syntax is not sufficient for semantics. No amount of formal symbol manipulation constitutes understanding.

If Searle is right, then the intentional stance applied to GSD Reflect is systematically misleading. The system does not "detect patterns" — it manipulates tokens. It does not "propose interventions" — it produces strings. The intentional vocabulary, however predictively useful, conceals a fundamental absence of understanding that matters for design.

Dennett's response to Searle is well-known: the entire room (person + rules + symbols) may "understand" Chinese even though no component does. Understanding is a property of the system, not its parts. And the criterion for understanding is behavioral — what the system can do — not introspective. But this response has never fully satisfied Searle's supporters, and the debate continues.

For design purposes, the tension is productive rather than paralyzing. Even if Searle is right that the system does not understand, the governance mechanisms recommended here (therefore test, commitment tracking, developer engagement) are appropriate regardless. If the system understands, these mechanisms verify its understanding. If the system does not understand, these mechanisms compensate for its lack of understanding. The design is robust to both positions.

**Mitigation:** Design governance mechanisms that are appropriate whether the system understands or not. Do not let the intentional stance's predictive success erode skepticism about the system's inner states. The intentional stance is a design tool, not an endorsement of machine understanding.

### 2. The Over-Attribution Risk

The intentional stance's greatest strength — predictive utility — is also its greatest danger. If designers consistently treat the system as an intentional agent, they may forget that this is a stance, not a fact. The "as if" qualifier erodes through habitual use. Users who interact with a system that "proposes" and "recommends" may come to believe it "understands" and "judges."

This is not a hypothetical risk. Research on human-AI interaction consistently shows that users attribute understanding, emotion, and judgment to systems that produce fluent natural language — even when told the system is a statistical model. Dennett himself has warned about this: the intentional stance is a tool for the stancer (the designer, the analyst), not a claim about the stancee (the system).

For GSD Reflect, over-attribution could lead to under-governance: if the developer trusts the system's "judgment," they may skip the evaluation step that is epistemologically essential. They may accept lessons without checking inferential connections, approve proposals without evaluating predictions, and allow autonomous action without tracking commitments. The result would be a system that acts with the authority of understanding and the reliability of competence-without-comprehension — a dangerous combination.

**Mitigation:** Build over-attribution resistance into the system's interface and language. Use third-person, mechanism-language in system outputs ("Pattern detected by reflector" rather than "I noticed a pattern"). Include explicit uncertainty quantification. Require developer acknowledgment (not just passive acceptance) for high-consequence proposals. Make the competence-comprehension gap visible in every interaction.

### 3. Competence Without Comprehension May Be Too Comfortable

"Competence without comprehension" provides a clean, comfortable framework: the system is competent, design leverages competence, governance compensates for absent comprehension. But this framework may discourage investigation into *what is actually happening* inside the system. If we accept that the system produces useful outputs without understanding them, we may stop asking *how* it produces those outputs and *why* they are useful — questions whose answers could improve the system's design or reveal systematic failure modes.

Dennett himself might be sympathetic to this concern. His framework is descriptive (how to think about the system) not prescriptive (how to stop investigating the system). But in practice, "competence without comprehension" can function as a conversation-stopper: "the system works, we don't know why, and it doesn't matter." This risks missing important insights about the system's failure modes — especially the failure modes that arise from the specific *kind* of competence LLMs have (pattern-matching over training distributions) as opposed to the *kind* of competence humans have (causal reasoning, contextual judgment, experiential knowledge).

**Mitigation:** Use "competence without comprehension" as a starting point, not an endpoint. The system has competence without comprehension — granted. Now investigate: what specific competences does the system have? Where do they come from? Under what conditions do they fail? What distinguishes the system's competence from human competence in the same domain? These questions are answerable and design-relevant, and "competence without comprehension" should not be used to avoid them.

### 4. Does the Intentional Stance Provide Design Guidance or Merely Relabel?

A critic might ask: does taking the intentional stance toward GSD Reflect actually change any design decisions, or does it merely redescribe the same decisions in intentional vocabulary? Instead of "the sensor should detect CI failures," we say "the system should believe that CI failures are occurring." Instead of "the lesson engine should produce actionable recommendations," we say "the system should want to improve the workflow." Is this a genuine shift in design thinking, or is it new labels on old bottles?

The answer depends on how seriously the stance is taken. If the intentional stance is used merely as a linguistic veneer — replacing mechanism vocabulary with intentional vocabulary without changing the questions asked or the designs produced — then it is indeed relabeling. But if the stance is used to generate genuinely new questions (What should the system believe that it currently does not? When should its rationality be expected to fail? What would make its proposals more "rational"?), then it produces designs that the mechanism vocabulary alone would not suggest.

The test is empirical: do designs produced under the intentional stance outperform designs produced under the design stance? This is answerable by building both and comparing, though the comparison is difficult because the stances lead to different metrics (mechanism metrics vs. proposal quality metrics).

**Mitigation:** Apply the intentional stance provisionally and evaluate whether it produces genuinely new design insights. If it does not — if every intentional-stance design question reduces to a design-stance question — abandon it as redundant. If it does — if intentional questions reveal design possibilities that mechanism questions miss — retain it as a genuine contribution. The stance must earn its keep through design utility, not philosophical elegance.

## Praxis Recommendations

1. **Adopt the intentional stance as a design methodology, not a metaphysical commitment.** When designing new features, ask intentional questions: What should the system believe about the current project state? What should it want to achieve? When should its rationality be expected to fail? These questions generate design insights that mechanism questions ("is the pipeline functioning?") miss. But treat the intentional vocabulary as a design tool, not a claim about the system's inner life. In documentation and governance, use the design stance: the system is a mechanism whose outputs are evaluated for quality. *Cite: dennett/intentional-stance, dennett/design-stance-dissolution.*

2. **Make the competence-comprehension gap an explicit design parameter.** Every feature that produces natural-language output should be designed with awareness that the system has competence (it can produce reasonable outputs) without comprehension (it does not understand what it produces). Governance mechanisms — the therefore test, deontic scorekeeping, developer engagement — are all responses to this gap. When adding new capabilities, ask: "if the system produces a competent but uncomprehending output here, what governance mechanism ensures it is evaluated before becoming operational?" *Cite: dennett/competence-without-comprehension.*

3. **Test detected patterns for reality using predictive utility.** When the reflector detects a pattern, apply the real-patterns criterion: does this pattern have predictive power beyond the observation mechanism? If acting on the pattern changes outcomes (adding test requirements reduces fix chains), the pattern is real. If acting on the pattern changes only sensor behavior (reducing phase size reduces sensor firings but not actual problems), the pattern is a sensor artifact. Build this distinction into the reflection pipeline: patterns that have not been tested for predictive utility should be labeled "candidate patterns," not "lessons." *Cite: dennett/real-patterns.*

4. **Build over-attribution resistance into system outputs.** Use third-person, mechanism-language in system reports: "Pattern detected by reflector" rather than "I noticed a pattern." "Artifact sensor flagged file-location discrepancy" rather than "I found a problem." Include explicit uncertainty quantification in every output. Never present a system output as a settled conclusion. These linguistic choices are not cosmetic — they resist the natural human tendency to attribute understanding to systems that produce fluent natural language. *Cite: dennett/over-attribution-risk, dennett/heterophenomenology.*

5. **Apply heterophenomenology to system reports: take seriously, don't take literally.** When the system reports "pattern detected: CI failures correlate with admin pushes," treat this as a functional report worth investigating — the system processed data and produced a summary that may correspond to reality. But do not treat it as infallible. The pattern may be spurious, artifactual, or mischaracterized. Evaluate the report's evidence, consider alternative explanations, and verify independently before acting. This is the epistemologically correct response to outputs from a system with competence but uncertain comprehension. *Cite: dennett/heterophenomenology.*

6. **Use Dennett's framework to dissolve design paralysis around the "does it really understand?" question.** When design discussions stall on the question of whether the system "really" detects patterns, "really" learns lessons, or "really" makes decisions, invoke the Dennettian dissolution: the question is malformed for design purposes. The well-formed question is: "does treating the system as if it detects/learns/decides produce better designs than treating it as a pure mechanism?" If yes, adopt the intentional stance for design. If no, use the design stance. In either case, the metaphysical question about "real" understanding is irrelevant to the design decision and should not be allowed to block progress. *Cite: dennett/design-stance-dissolution, dennett/intentional-stance.*

7. **Investigate the specific character of the system's competence.** "Competence without comprehension" is a starting point, not an endpoint. Investigate: What specific competences does the LLM bring to pattern detection? (Statistical regularity detection, cross-domain analogy, natural-language summarization.) Where do these competences come from? (Training on large corpora of text about software development.) Under what conditions do they fail? (Novel project types not represented in training data, subtle patterns that require causal reasoning, situations where the "obvious" pattern is a red herring.) This investigation improves design by calibrating expectations: leverage the competences that are genuine, and add governance mechanisms where the competences are known to fail. *Cite: dennett/competence-without-comprehension.*

## Citable Principles

- **dennett/intentional-stance**: Treating a system as if it has beliefs and desires is a predictive strategy. If it works — if attributing beliefs, desires, and rationality to the system yields better predictions and better designs than the alternatives — that is all there is to say about "real" intentionality. The intentional stance is a tool for the designer, not a claim about the system's consciousness.

- **dennett/competence-without-comprehension**: Systems can exhibit remarkable competence without any comprehension of what they are doing. LLMs produce competent language outputs without understanding language. Design should leverage competence (trust the system's outputs enough to evaluate them) without assuming comprehension (do not trust the system's outputs enough to act on them without evaluation). The competence-comprehension gap is a permanent design constraint, not a deficiency to be fixed.

- **dennett/real-patterns**: Intentional attributions and detected patterns pick out genuine regularities even if they do not correspond to discrete internal states. A pattern is "real" if it has predictive and practical utility — if acting on it changes outcomes. Patterns that are artifacts of sensor design or statistical noise are not real, regardless of how confidently they are reported. The test for reality is empirical: does the pattern predict?

- **dennett/design-stance-dissolution**: The question "does it really understand?" may be malformed for design purposes. The tractable question is: "does treating it as understanding produce better designs than treating it as a mechanism?" If yes, take the intentional stance for design. If no, take the design stance. In either case, the metaphysical question is irrelevant to the design decision and should not block progress.

- **dennett/heterophenomenology**: Take system reports seriously as functional data worth acting on, without claiming they reveal inner experience or genuine understanding. System outputs are sincere functional reports — they reflect the system's processing of its inputs. They are worth engaging with (they might be right) but not infallible (they might be wrong, artifactual, or misleading). The appropriate response is investigation, not credulity or dismissal.

- **dennett/over-attribution-risk**: The intentional stance's predictive success may encourage unwarranted trust in system "understanding." Habitual use of intentional vocabulary ("the system noticed," "the system recommends") can erode the "as if" qualifier, leading users to attribute genuine understanding to a system with competence but not comprehension. Over-attribution resistance — mechanism-language in outputs, explicit uncertainty, required evaluation — is an epistemological safeguard, not a UX preference.
