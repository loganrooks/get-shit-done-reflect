# Philosophical Deliberation: Inferentialism and the Space of Reasons

**Tradition:** Pittsburgh School / Analytic Philosophy
**Key thinkers:** Wilfrid Sellars (Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind, 1956; Science and Metaphysics, 1968), Robert Brandom (Making It Explicit, 1994; Articulating Reasons, 2000)
**Created:** 2026-03-08
**Status:** Active

## Core Ideas

The Pittsburgh School — named for the philosophical tradition centered at the University of Pittsburgh across two generations — develops a picture of knowledge and meaning that is fundamentally normative and inferential. Sellars, writing in the 1950s and 60s, attacked the empiricist foundations that most epistemology took for granted. Brandom, his student's student, built on Sellars's critique to produce a comprehensive theory of conceptual content as inferential role. Together, they provide the sharpest available tools for asking what it means for a system to *know* something, as opposed to merely storing information formatted to look like knowledge.

This matters for GSD Reflect because the system's central problem — 124 signals, 0 formal lessons, 0 remediated signals — is precisely a problem about the difference between producing outputs that resemble knowledge and actually participating in the practices that constitute knowledge. The reflection pipeline generates prose reports. Prose reports are not knowledge. The question is what would make them knowledge, and Sellars and Brandom provide a more precise answer than any other tradition considered so far.

### The Space of Reasons

Sellars's most influential contribution is the distinction between the "space of reasons" and the "space of causes" — between the domain of justifying and being able to justify what one says, and the domain of causal-nomological explanation. Being in the space of reasons — being able to give and ask for reasons — is what distinguishes genuinely cognitive states from mere reliable differential responsive dispositions. A thermometer reliably covaries with temperature. It does not *know* the temperature. A parrot trained to say "it's raining" when it rains does not *know* it's raining, because it cannot justify the claim, draw inferences from it, or respond to challenges against it. The parrot is causally reliable but normatively inert.

Sellars's formulation in *Empiricism and the Philosophy of Mind* (§36): "In characterizing an episode or a state as that of *knowing*, we are not giving an empirical description of that episode or state; we are placing it in the logical space of reasons, of justifying and being able to justify what one says." Knowledge is not a natural kind to be discovered by examining internal states. It is a normative status — something conferred by participation in a practice of giving and asking for reasons.

The question this forces on GSD Reflect is stark. When the reflection pipeline produces a report saying "pattern detected: CI failures correlate with admin pushes," is this a cognitive achievement — a piece of knowledge — or is it a reliable differential response, like a thermometer reading? The system detected a statistical regularity and formatted it as a natural-language assertion. But can it *justify* the claim? Can it respond to the challenge "maybe the correlation is spurious because both spiked during the migration week"? Can it draw inferences from the claim — "therefore, CI should be a blocking gate" — and commit to those inferences? If not, the output is in the space of causes (the LLM was caused to produce this text by its training and context), not in the space of reasons (the system justified this claim on the basis of evidence).

This does not mean the output is *useless*. A thermometer reading is useful. But it means the output is not *knowledge* in the full sense, and designing the system as if it were — giving its outputs titles like "lesson" and statuses like "high confidence" — is a misattribution of normative status. The system's outputs are better understood as *candidate assertions* that enter the space of reasons only when the developer engages with them: evaluates the evidence, considers alternatives, draws inferences, and commits to action.

### The Myth of the Given

Sellars's attack on "the given" — the idea that raw experience provides a self-justifying, pre-conceptual foundation for empirical knowledge — is among the most consequential arguments in twentieth-century epistemology. The Myth of the Given takes many forms, but the core error is always the same: treating some class of cognitive states as simultaneously (a) non-inferential (not derived from other beliefs or conceptual activities) and (b) epistemically efficacious (able to justify other beliefs). Sellars argues these two properties are incompatible. To justify a belief, a state must have conceptual content — it must be the kind of thing that can stand in inferential relations to other beliefs. But if it has conceptual content, it is not "given" — it is already shaped by the conceptual framework of the knower.

All observation is conceptually structured. There is no pre-theoretical perception of "raw data." What you observe depends on the concepts you bring to observation. A radiologist and a layperson see different things when they look at the same X-ray, not because the photons differ but because the conceptual frameworks differ. The radiologist's observation "pneumothorax in the left lung" is theory-laden: it presupposes anatomical concepts, radiological training, and a classificatory scheme. It is none the less an *observation* for being theory-laden. But it is not "given."

Applied to GSD Reflect: the system's signals are NOT raw observations of development activity. They are already interpreted through sensor schemas (what counts as a "deviation"?), severity heuristics (what counts as "high" severity?), confidence weights (what counts as "strong" evidence?), and the categories built into the sensor design itself (the decision to track "file churn" but not "decision reversals" is a conceptual choice that shapes what can be observed). The signal `sig-2026-03-02-ci-failures-ignored-throughout-v116` is not a neutral registration of facts. It is a conceptually structured observation that presupposes: (a) CI failures are the kind of thing that should be detected, (b) "ignored" is the right characterization (as opposed to "deferred" or "deprioritized"), (c) the 23-day timeframe is epistemically relevant, and (d) the pattern constitutes a *problem* rather than a *reasonable trade-off*.

None of this means the signal is wrong. It means the signal is theory-laden — shaped by the framework that produced it. Pretending the signals are neutral observations — treating them as "the data" from which reflection merely draws conclusions — is committing the Myth of the Given. It conceals the interpretive work already embedded in signal design and makes that interpretive work immune from scrutiny. A more epistemically honest system would make the theory-ladenness of its observations explicit: "This signal was generated by a sensor designed to detect X, using heuristics that assume Y, with severity calibrated by Z. Different sensor design choices would produce different observations."

### Inferentialism

Brandom develops Sellars's normative pragmatics into a comprehensive theory of meaning. The core thesis of *Making It Explicit* is that conceptual content is constituted by inferential relations — not by reference to objects or by verification conditions, but by the role a concept plays in a network of inferences. To grasp a concept is to know what follows from applying it (downstream commitments), what would be evidence for applying it (upstream entitlements), and what is incompatible with applying it (exclusion relations).

Consider the concept "load-bearing change." To grasp this concept is to know: (a) if a change is load-bearing, then it should be tested before deployment (downstream inference); (b) changes to shared infrastructure, APIs, and configuration files are typically load-bearing (upstream evidence); (c) a change cannot be both load-bearing and safe-to-ignore (exclusion). Someone who uses the phrase "load-bearing change" but cannot articulate any of these inferential connections does not grasp the concept — they are merely mouthing words.

Brandom's inferentialism provides a precise criterion for evaluating GSD Reflect's outputs. A "lesson" like "CI must be a blocking gate" has conceptual content only to the extent that it has inferential connections: what follows from it (THEREFORE add a CI-check step to the plan workflow, THEREFORE fail the phase if CI hasn't passed, THEREFORE escalate when CI is bypassed), what evidence supports it (five consecutive CI failures bypassed during v1.16, resulting in undetected test rot), and what it excludes (it is incompatible with treating CI as advisory-only). A lesson that is asserted without these inferential connections — that sits in the knowledge base as an isolated proposition — has no content in Brandom's sense. It is a string, not a concept.

This is more precise than Dewey's cash-value test because it is structural rather than merely consequentialist. We do not need to wait for the lesson to be "used" to evaluate whether it has content. We can examine its inferential articulation at the moment of creation. Does the lesson specify what follows from it? What supports it? What it rules out? If not, it is inferentially inert — conceptually empty — regardless of how confident the label says it is.

### Deontic Scorekeeping

Brandom's account of discursive practice centers on what he calls "deontic scorekeeping." Participants in a discourse keep track of each other's normative statuses — specifically, their *commitments* (what they have asserted and what follows from those assertions) and *entitlements* (what they are justified in asserting, given their other commitments and the evidence available). Assertion is not merely the utterance of a sentence. It is a normative act with consequences: asserting P commits you to the consequences of P and entitles your interlocutors to hold you to P. If you assert "this plan will complete in 3 phases" and it takes 7, your interlocutor is entitled to challenge your future estimates on the basis of this track record.

Deontic scorekeeping is Brandom's model of rational discourse as a social practice. Each participant maintains a "score" for the others — a running account of what they are committed to and entitled to — and the game of giving and asking for reasons consists in updating these scores through assertion, challenge, justification, and retraction.

Applied to the GSD Reflect governance question — who decides when the system may act autonomously — deontic scorekeeping provides a structural model. When the system proposes an intervention ("lesson detected: CI should be a blocking gate; recommended action: add CI-check to plan template"), it is making an assertion. This assertion creates commitments: the system is committed to the claim that CI blocking will improve outcomes, and if the claim proves wrong, the system's future proposals should be evaluated more skeptically. When the developer accepts the proposal and allows it to be implemented, the developer grants an entitlement: the system is now entitled to act on this class of proposal. When the outcome is tracked — did CI blocking actually improve outcomes? — the commitments are evaluated against results.

This gives a principled framework for automation governance that goes beyond fixed automation levels. The system earns entitlements through a track record of honored commitments. A system whose proposals consistently produce good outcomes has accumulated entitlements that justify greater autonomy. A system whose proposals frequently fail has commitments it cannot honor, and its entitlements should be reduced. This is not anthropomorphism — it is a structural model of trust based on normative accounting.

### The "Therefore" Test

Drawing directly from Brandom's inferentialism, we can derive a precise operational test for lesson quality: the "therefore" test. A lesson is genuine knowledge — has genuine conceptual content — only if it is inferentially connected to action. Specifically: can you state a "therefore" that follows from the lesson?

"CI must be a blocking gate" — THEREFORE add a CI-check step to the plan workflow. THEREFORE fail the phase if CI hasn't passed. THEREFORE escalate to the developer when CI is bypassed via admin push. The lesson passes the therefore test: it has explicit downstream inferential connections to concrete actions.

"Code quality is important" — THEREFORE... what? Nothing specific follows. The lesson is so general that no particular action is entailed. It is inferentially inert. It fails the therefore test.

"Fix-fix-fix chains indicate incomplete initial implementation" — THEREFORE require a root-cause analysis before the third fix commit in a sequence. THEREFORE flag plans that address symptoms without modifying the originally-deficient code. The lesson passes: specific actions follow from it.

The therefore test is more precise and more operationalizable than either Dewey's cash-value test (which requires waiting for outcomes) or Popper's falsifiability criterion (which asks what would refute the claim, not what action it entails). It is a *structural* test: we can mechanically check whether the output of the reflection pipeline includes an explicit inferential link from detected pattern to recommended action. A reflection report that says "we observed pattern X across signals Y and Z" but never says "THEREFORE..." has produced an observation without producing knowledge. It has described without inferring. And description without inference is precisely the gap in the current pipeline: 124 signals described, 0 inferences drawn that connect to action.

### Application and Displacement

Sellars and Brandom are talking about human linguistic practices — the practices of giving and asking for reasons as conducted by rational agents in a linguistic community. Whether an LLM "participates in the space of reasons" is a genuinely contested philosophical question that this deliberation cannot and should not attempt to resolve. When the system says "I detected pattern X because of signals Y and Z," is it giving reasons or producing outputs that are formatted like reasons? Sellars would insist on the distinction. The output has the *form* of reason-giving (it cites evidence, draws a conclusion, uses "because") without necessarily having the *normative force* of reason-giving (the system does not commit itself to the consequences of its assertion in the way that a rational agent does).

But this metaphysical question can be bracketed for design purposes. The Myth of the Given applies cleanly regardless of whether the system "really" thinks: the signals ARE theory-laden, shaped by sensor design, and pretending they are neutral observations is self-deception whether committed by a human or an AI system. The theory-ladenness of observation is a fact about the *observations*, not about the *observer*.

Similarly, inferentialism provides a precise design criterion without requiring resolution of the consciousness question. Regardless of whether the system genuinely reasons, its outputs can be evaluated for inferential articulation. Does the output specify what follows from it? What supports it? What it rules out? These are structural properties of the output text, evaluable by anyone (human or machine) who can parse inferential relations. The therefore test works whether the system "really" reasons or merely produces outputs that happen to have (or lack) inferential structure.

Deontic scorekeeping can be applied structurally to the governance question. When the system proposes an intervention, it takes on a commitment — not in the phenomenological sense (it does not *experience* commitment) but in the structural sense (its track record is updated, its future credibility is affected). When the developer accepts, they grant an entitlement — not because the system "deserves" trust in a moral sense, but because the governance framework tracks normative statuses functionally. This is what Brandom calls "AI scorekeeping" in a 2019 Locke Lecture — applying the structure of deontic scorekeeping without presupposing that the system is a full-fledged rational agent.

## Relevance to GSD Reflect

GSD Reflect's current pipeline failure — 124 signals, 0 lessons, 0 remediations — can be diagnosed with unusual precision through the Sellarsian-Brandomian framework. The pipeline produces prose reports that *describe* patterns in the signal data. But description is not inference. The reports sit in the space of causes (the LLM was caused to produce them) without entering the space of reasons (they do not commit to consequences, do not specify what follows, do not respond to challenges). They are thermometer readings formatted as knowledge claims.

The therefore test provides the most operationalizable design criterion to emerge from any philosophical framework considered in this deliberation series. It does not require resolving the question of whether the system "really" reasons. It does not require waiting for outcomes (like the cash-value test). It does not require specifying falsifiers (like Popper's criterion). It asks a single structural question of every pipeline output: does it include an explicit "therefore" connecting pattern to action? If not, the output is inferentially inert and should not be promoted to "lesson" status.

The Myth of the Given provides the clearest articulation of why treating signals as neutral data is epistemically dishonest. Every other framework acknowledges theory-ladenness in passing. Sellars makes it foundational: there is no epistemic contact with reality that is not already conceptually mediated. This means sensor design is not a neutral technical decision — it is an epistemological commitment that determines what the system can and cannot know.

Deontic scorekeeping provides a governance model that is neither anthropomorphic (treating the system as a rational agent deserving trust) nor purely mechanistic (treating the system as a rule-follower deserving no trust). It is structural: track commitments and entitlements, evaluate outcomes against commitments, adjust entitlements based on track record. This is the principled version of what the automation statistics (AUTO-06) are trying to approximate.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: The Therefore Test Applied to v1.16 Reflection Output

The v1.16 reflection produced a report identifying 13 patterns across 79 signals. Consider how the therefore test evaluates one of these patterns.

**Pattern identified:** "Scope creep is the dominant deviation type, appearing in 23 of 79 signals (29%)."

**The therefore test:** THEREFORE... what? The pattern is stated as a descriptive observation. It tells us that scope creep is common. But what follows from this? Several candidate inferences are available:

1. THEREFORE plans should include explicit scope boundaries that are machine-checkable (not just prose descriptions of what is in and out of scope).
2. THEREFORE the plan sensor should flag scope-adjacent work earlier (at the first out-of-scope file modification, not after a pattern accumulates).
3. THEREFORE phases should be smaller, reducing the scope available for creep.
4. THEREFORE scope creep should be reclassified — perhaps much of what is labeled "scope creep" is actually necessary discovery work that the planning phase failed to anticipate, in which case the problem is planning quality, not execution discipline.

None of these inferences appears in the reflection report. The report *describes* the pattern and *moves on*. It is, in Brandom's terms, an assertion without inferential articulation — a proposition placed in the discourse without any indication of what follows from it, what supports it beyond the count, or what it rules out. It has the grammatical form of knowledge ("scope creep is the dominant deviation type") without the inferential substance.

A reflection pipeline designed with the therefore test as a structural requirement would not emit this pattern without at least one explicit "therefore." The output schema would require: (a) pattern description, (b) at least one downstream inference (what action follows), (c) at least one upstream justification (what evidence, beyond signal count, supports the inference), and (d) at least one exclusion (what alternative interpretation is ruled out by this inference). This is not asking the LLM to "be smarter." It is structuring the output format so that inferentially inert descriptions are architecturally impossible — the schema rejects them.

### Example 2: Signals as Theory-Laden Observations

Consider two signals from the v1.16 corpus:

- `sig-2026-02-27-agent-protocol-not-in-npm-source`: the artifact sensor detected that agent-protocol.md existed in `.claude/agents/` but not in `agents/` (the npm source directory). Severity: high. Confidence: strong.

- The *absence* of any signal about the 23-day CI failure during the same period.

The first signal exists because a sensor was designed to detect file-location discrepancies between the npm source and install target directories. The sensor embodies a conceptual framework: "files should exist in both locations; discrepancy is a problem." This is a theory-laden observation — it presupposes that the dual-directory architecture is normatively correct and that violations are anomalous.

The second "signal" — the absence — is even more revealing. No CI sensor existed in v1.16. The system literally *could not observe* CI failures, no matter how severe or prolonged. This is not a failure of attention; it is a failure of conceptual framework. The system's observation vocabulary did not include the concept "CI failure." In Sellarsian terms, the system lacked the conceptual resources to make CI failures *observable*. They were not "given" data that the system happened to miss. They were *unobservable within the current framework* — as invisible to the system as ultraviolet light is to the human eye without instruments.

This is the Myth of the Given in action. If we treat the v1.16 signal corpus as "the data" — as a neutral record of what happened — we conclude that file-location discrepancies were a problem and CI failures were not (they do not appear in the data). But this conclusion is an artifact of sensor design, not a fact about the development process. The data is theory-laden by the sensors that produced it. Adding a CI sensor in v1.17 does not "discover" CI failures that were "there all along." It *constitutes* CI failures as observable phenomena by expanding the system's conceptual framework.

The practical upshot: when evaluating the signal corpus, always ask "what is this corpus *unable* to observe?" The gaps in the data are not random — they are systematic, determined by the conceptual commitments of the sensor architecture. Expanding what the system can know requires expanding its conceptual framework (adding sensors, categories, detection patterns), not just improving its processing of existing observations.

### Example 3: Deontic Scorekeeping in Automation Governance

Consider a concrete governance scenario. The system proposes: "Pattern detected: fix-fix-fix chains occur when initial implementation skips tests. Recommended action: add a pre-implementation test requirement to the plan template."

Under deontic scorekeeping, this proposal creates normative statuses:

**System's commitment:** By proposing this intervention, the system is committed to the claim that adding a pre-implementation test requirement will reduce fix-fix-fix chains. This commitment is now on the record. If the developer accepts and the intervention is implemented, the system's track record will be evaluated against this prediction.

**Developer's entitlement decision:** The developer can accept (granting the system entitlement to modify the plan template), reject (denying entitlement and recording the reason — perhaps "the fix chains were caused by unclear requirements, not missing tests"), or defer (neither granting nor denying, requesting more evidence).

**Outcome evaluation:** After 3 phases with the new requirement, check: did fix-fix-fix chains decrease? If yes, the commitment is honored — the system's credibility (entitlement score) increases. If no, the commitment is failed — the system's credibility decreases, and the lesson's confidence should be downgraded.

**Score update:** The system's deontic score now includes: 1 proposal made, 1 prediction recorded, outcome pending. Over time, this score accumulates. A system with 15 honored commitments out of 20 proposals has earned significant entitlement. A system with 3 honored commitments out of 20 has not. Automation levels could be dynamically linked to this score: entitlement for autonomous action (Level 3) requires a minimum commitment-honoring rate.

This model is more principled than fixed automation levels because it makes entitlement *earned* rather than *configured*. It is more principled than pure outcome tracking (AUTO-06) because it distinguishes between proposals that were accepted and succeeded (honored commitments), proposals that were accepted and failed (failed commitments), and proposals that were never tested (uncommitted). The governance question — should the system be allowed to act autonomously? — becomes an empirical question about the system's track record of commitment-honoring, not a configuration choice about risk tolerance.

## Tensions and Limitations

### 1. Does the LLM Participate in the Space of Reasons?

This is the foundational tension and it cannot be resolved within this deliberation. Sellars's space of reasons is defined by normative practices — giving and asking for reasons, committing to consequences, responding to challenges. An LLM produces text that has the *form* of these practices (it cites evidence, uses "because" and "therefore," responds to follow-up questions). Whether this constitutes *genuine participation* in the space of reasons or merely *simulation* of participation is a live question in the philosophy of AI.

Sellars would likely be skeptical. His criterion for being in the space of reasons is not behavioral similarity but normative engagement: can the system *commit* itself to the consequences of its assertions? When it says "CI should be a blocking gate," is it staking something on this claim? Can it be held accountable if the claim is wrong? In the full Sellarsian sense, probably not — the LLM does not have stakes, does not experience accountability, and does not maintain commitments across contexts in the way that a rational agent does.

But for design purposes, this skepticism can be productive rather than paralyzing. Design the system's outputs as *candidate entries into the space of reasons* — entries that achieve full normative status only when a rational agent (the developer) engages with them. This avoids both the error of treating system outputs as authoritative knowledge (over-attribution) and the error of treating them as meaningless noise (under-attribution). They are proposals that await normative uptake.

**Mitigation:** Label system outputs as "proposals" or "hypotheses" rather than "lessons" or "knowledge." Make the transition from proposal to knowledge contingent on developer engagement — evaluation, challenge, endorsement. The system produces candidates; the developer confers normative status.

### 2. Can Theory-Ladenness Be Made Visible to Itself?

If all observation is theory-laden, then the system's attempt to make its own theory-ladenness explicit is itself theory-laden. The meta-observation "this signal was shaped by sensor design assumptions" is produced by a meta-sensor with its own assumptions. This is a regress problem familiar from Sellars's own work: if justification requires conceptual mediation, what justifies the concepts?

Sellars's answer is holistic: concepts are justified by their place in a web of inferential relations, not by appeal to a foundation. But a holistic answer is difficult to implement in a system that must make discrete design decisions. Which aspects of theory-ladenness should the sensor document? How do we decide which conceptual commitments are worth making explicit and which can be left implicit? These decisions are themselves conceptual commitments — turtles all the way down.

**Mitigation (partial):** Accept that complete transparency about theory-ladenness is impossible. Aim for *one level of meta-reflection*: each sensor documents the conceptual framework it presupposes and the phenomena it cannot detect. Do not attempt to document the meta-framework that determines which aspects of the framework to document — that way lies paralysis. One level of reflection is an enormous improvement over zero.

### 3. Brandom's Framework Is Deeply Social

Deontic scorekeeping is a model of *social* normative practice — it describes how members of a linguistic community hold each other accountable through assertion, challenge, and justification. GSD Reflect is a single-user system. There is one developer and one AI system. Is deontic scorekeeping applicable when the "community" has only two members, one of whom may not be a genuine normative agent?

Brandom himself has addressed this in the context of AI: even a two-party interaction can instantiate deontic scorekeeping if the structural features are present (commitment tracking, entitlement management, accountability over time). The question is not the *size* of the community but whether the *practice* of commitment and entitlement is maintained. A single developer who tracks the system's proposals, evaluates outcomes, and adjusts autonomy based on results is engaged in deontic scorekeeping — keeping score of the system's commitments and entitlements.

But the asymmetry is real. The developer keeps score of the system. The system does not genuinely keep score of the developer (it does not track whether the developer's decisions were wise). This is a one-sided scorecard, which Brandom might argue is not fully deontic scorekeeping but a degenerate case of it. For design purposes, the one-sided version may be sufficient — we need governance of the system, not mutual accountability between equals.

**Mitigation:** Implement one-directional scorekeeping (tracking the system's commitments and honoring rate) without pretending to two-directional scorekeeping (mutual normative accountability). The governance model is asymmetric by design: the developer holds the system accountable, not vice versa.

### 4. The Therefore Test May Miss Practical Wisdom

The therefore test checks structural properties: does the output have inferential connections? But a lesson could pass the therefore test — have clear inferential connections to specific actions — and still be wrong for THIS situation. "CI must be a blocking gate, THEREFORE fail the phase if CI hasn't passed" has clear inferential structure. But if the CI environment is misconfigured and all tests are failing due to infrastructure issues, blocking on CI would halt all development for the wrong reason. The therefore test ensures inferential *articulation* but not inferential *wisdom*.

This is a variant of Aristotle's point about phronesis: no structural test can substitute for situational judgment. The therefore test is a necessary condition for knowledge (without inferential connections, there is no content) but not a sufficient condition (with inferential connections, the content might still be misapplied). The test catches inferentially inert outputs — which is the current problem — but cannot catch inferentially articulated outputs that are situationally inappropriate.

**Mitigation:** Use the therefore test as a *minimum quality threshold*, not as a certification of wisdom. Outputs that fail the test are rejected (no inferential content = no knowledge). Outputs that pass the test are promoted to candidate status, where situational evaluation by the developer determines applicability. The test is a filter, not a judge.

## Praxis Recommendations

1. **Implement the therefore test as a structural requirement of reflection output.** Every pattern assertion produced by the reflection pipeline must include at least one explicit downstream inference ("therefore, do X"), at least one upstream citation ("because signals Y and Z show..."), and at least one exclusion ("this rules out the interpretation that..."). Outputs lacking these components are observations, not lessons, and should be labeled accordingly. This is the single most actionable recommendation from the inferentialist framework and addresses the proximate cause of the 124-signals-0-lessons gap. *Cite: sellars-brandom/therefore-test, sellars-brandom/inferential-connection.*

2. **Make sensor theory-ladenness explicit in sensor specifications.** Each sensor design document should include a section titled "Conceptual Commitments" that states: what framework of interpretation does this sensor presuppose? What categories does it impose on observation? What phenomena are outside its detection scope? This makes the Myth of the Given operationally visible. When new signals seem surprising or contradictory, checking the sensor's conceptual commitments is the first diagnostic step — the surprise may be an artifact of the framework, not a feature of reality. *Cite: sellars-brandom/myth-of-the-given, sellars-brandom/theory-ladenness.*

3. **Label system outputs by normative status, not epistemic confidence.** Replace the current labels (`confidence: high/medium/low`) with normative-status labels: `proposal` (system-generated, awaiting developer engagement), `endorsed` (developer has evaluated and accepted), `committed` (action has been taken based on the proposal, outcomes pending), `validated` (outcomes confirm the proposal's prediction). This makes explicit that system outputs are *candidates for the space of reasons*, not *inhabitants of it*, until the developer confers normative status through engagement. *Cite: sellars-brandom/space-of-reasons.*

4. **Implement commitment tracking for system proposals.** When the system proposes an intervention, record: (a) the prediction ("this will reduce signal type X"), (b) the evaluation criterion ("check after N phases whether signal type X decreased"), and (c) the outcome (when known). Aggregate commitment-honoring rate as a governance metric. Systems with high honoring rates earn greater entitlement; systems with low rates have entitlement reduced. This is deontic scorekeeping applied to automation governance. *Cite: sellars-brandom/deontic-scorekeeping.*

5. **Conduct periodic sensor-framework audits.** Every N milestones (or when a surprising absence of signals is noted), explicitly ask: what can the current sensor architecture NOT observe? What conceptual categories are missing? The v1.16 CI blind spot is the paradigm case: the system could not observe CI failures because its conceptual framework did not include that category. Framework audits prevent the accumulation of systematic blind spots that are invisible precisely because they are *absences* in observation. *Cite: sellars-brandom/myth-of-the-given, sellars-brandom/theory-ladenness.*

6. **Design the reflection pipeline to produce inferentially articulated outputs, not prose summaries.** The current pipeline produces natural-language reports that describe patterns. Redesign the output schema to require structured inferential content: for each pattern, require fields for `therefore` (downstream inferences), `because` (upstream evidence), `rules_out` (excluded alternatives), and `challenged_by` (potential counter-evidence). This makes inferential articulation a structural property of the output format, not a hoped-for property of the LLM's generation. If the LLM cannot fill these fields for a given pattern, that pattern is not yet a lesson — it is an observation awaiting inferential development. *Cite: sellars-brandom/inferential-connection, sellars-brandom/therefore-test.*

7. **Treat the developer's engagement as the normative gateway.** No system output should acquire the status of "knowledge" or "lesson" without developer engagement. The pipeline can produce candidates. It can rank them by inferential articulation. It can present them with supporting evidence and downstream implications. But the transition from candidate to knowledge requires the developer to evaluate, challenge, or endorse — to perform the normative act that moves the proposition into the space of reasons. This is not a workflow bottleneck; it is an epistemological necessity. *Cite: sellars-brandom/space-of-reasons.*

## Citable Principles

- **sellars-brandom/space-of-reasons**: Genuine knowledge requires being able to give and ask for reasons, not merely producing reason-shaped outputs. A system output that asserts a pattern but cannot justify it, draw inferences from it, or respond to challenges against it is in the space of causes (produced by the system's mechanism) not the space of reasons (warranted by evidence and inference). Knowledge is a normative status conferred by participation in rational practice, not a label applied to confident outputs.

- **sellars-brandom/myth-of-the-given**: There is no pre-conceptual, self-justifying observation. All data is theory-laden by the framework that produces it. Signals are not neutral registrations of fact; they are conceptually structured observations shaped by sensor design, severity heuristics, and categorical choices. Treating the signal corpus as "the data" — as a neutral given from which conclusions are merely drawn — conceals the interpretive commitments embedded in sensor design and makes them immune from scrutiny.

- **sellars-brandom/inferential-connection**: Conceptual content is constituted by inferential relations. A claim with no inferential connections — that does not specify what follows from it, what supports it, or what it rules out — has no content in the inferentialist sense. It is a string, not a concept. Knowledge-base entries should be evaluated for inferential articulation, not just epistemic confidence.

- **sellars-brandom/therefore-test**: Knowledge must be inferentially connected to action. If no "therefore" follows from a pattern assertion — if the assertion sits as an isolated description with no downstream implications for what should be done — the output is an idle observation, not a lesson. The therefore test is a structural minimum for lesson quality: outputs that fail it are inferentially inert and should not be promoted to lesson status.

- **sellars-brandom/deontic-scorekeeping**: Proposals create commitments (the proposer is bound by the predicted consequences) and entitlements (the acceptor authorizes future action based on demonstrated reliability). Tracking these normative statuses — how many commitments have been honored, how many have failed — structures governance as an empirical question about track record rather than a configuration choice about risk tolerance.

- **sellars-brandom/theory-ladenness**: Sensor design determines what can be detected. The absence of a sensor is not the absence of a problem — it is the absence of the conceptual resources needed to make the problem observable. Expanding what the system can know requires expanding its conceptual framework (new sensors, new categories, new detection patterns), not just processing existing observations more thoroughly. Periodic framework audits should ask: what can the current architecture NOT see?
