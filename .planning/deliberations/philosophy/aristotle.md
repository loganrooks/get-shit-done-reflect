# Philosophical Deliberation: Aristotle's Practical Philosophy

**Tradition:** Aristotelian Practical Philosophy / Virtue Ethics
**Key thinkers:** Aristotle (Nicomachean Ethics, Politics, Rhetoric), Martha Nussbaum (The Fragility of Goodness), Alasdair MacIntyre (After Virtue)
**Created:** 2026-03-03
**Status:** Active

## Core Ideas

Aristotle's practical philosophy, developed primarily in the Nicomachean Ethics (especially Books III and VI), addresses a question that modern systems design rarely confronts directly: what does it mean to deliberate *well*, not just to deliberate? The answer turns on a set of distinctions -- between kinds of knowledge, between making and doing, between rule-following and judgment -- that bear directly on the design of a system that claims to improve its own deliberation over time.

### Phronesis (Practical Wisdom)

Aristotle identifies three intellectual virtues relevant to action: episteme (scientific knowledge of universal, necessary truths), techne (craft knowledge of how to produce things), and phronesis (practical wisdom about how to act well in particular situations). Phronesis is the most important for ethics and politics, and the hardest to formalize.

Phronesis is the capacity to deliberate well about "what is conducive to the good life in general" (NE VI.5, 1140a25-28). It is not knowledge of universals alone -- that would be episteme. It is not skill in producing artifacts -- that would be techne. Phronesis is the ability to perceive what a particular situation demands and to act accordingly. The phronimos (person of practical wisdom) does not apply a rule mechanically; they see *which* rule applies, *whether* it applies, and *how* it applies in this specific case. As Aristotle puts it: "Practical wisdom is not concerned with universals only; it must also recognize the particulars, for it is practical, and practice is concerned with particulars" (NE VI.7, 1141b14-16).

Nussbaum emphasizes that phronesis is essentially *non-algorithmic*. There is no decision procedure that, if followed correctly, guarantees a wise decision. Practical wisdom is precisely the capacity that remains necessary *after* all rules and procedures have been consulted. This is not irrationalism -- the phronimos reasons carefully -- but it is a rejection of the sufficiency of formal systems for practical judgment.

### Euboulia (Excellence in Deliberation)

Euboulia is a specific excellence of deliberation that Aristotle identifies in NE VI.9. Correct deliberation requires three conditions: the deliberation must be about the right end (not just any goal, but a genuinely good one), it must proceed in the right way (following appropriate methods, considering relevant evidence, not rushing or dawdling), and it must be completed at the right time (not premature and not belated). Crucially, euboulia concerns the *process* of deliberation, not merely its conclusion. A deliberation that reaches the right answer by lucky accident is not euboulia. A deliberation that follows the right process but is about a trivial or wrong-headed goal is also not euboulia.

Aristotle distinguishes euboulia from cleverness (deinotes). Cleverness is the ability to find means to any end, good or bad. Euboulia is cleverness directed toward genuinely good ends. A system can be clever -- optimizing for any metric it is given -- without possessing euboulia, because euboulia requires evaluating whether the end itself is worth pursuing.

### The Deliberative Syllogism

Aristotle models practical reasoning as a kind of syllogism (NE VII.3, 1147a25-31): a major premise stating a general rule ("plans that modify shared infrastructure should be sequenced, not parallelized"), a minor premise perceiving the particular situation ("this plan modifies the installer, which is shared infrastructure"), and a conclusion that is action ("therefore, this plan should not run in parallel with others touching the installer").

The critical insight is that the *minor premise* is the hard part. The major premise can be stated as a rule, stored as a lesson, or embedded in a workflow. But the minor premise requires *perception* -- correctly seeing what the situation IS. Is this plan modifying shared infrastructure, or is the overlap superficial? Is this signal indicating a genuine process failure, or a one-off anomaly? The deliberative syllogism fails not when the rules are wrong, but when the situation is misperceived.

MacIntyre extends this point: in complex practices, perceiving the situation correctly is itself a learned skill, developed through long experience within a tradition. A novice and an expert see different things when they look at the same situation, not because the novice lacks rules but because the novice lacks the perceptual capacity that comes from habituated practice.

### The Mean (Mesotes)

Virtue, for Aristotle, is a disposition to choose the mean between excess and deficiency (NE II.6, 1106b36-1107a2). Courage is the mean between cowardice (deficiency) and recklessness (excess). Generosity is the mean between miserliness and profligacy. Crucially, the mean is not arithmetic -- it is not always the midpoint. The mean is *relative to us*, determined by phronesis in the particular situation. What counts as courageous for an experienced soldier differs from what counts as courageous for a civilian. There is no formula for the mean; it requires judgment.

This has a direct structural implication: any system that offers a fixed scale of options (levels 0, 1, 2, 3) and asks the user to pick is offering a crude approximation of a judgment that should be context-sensitive. The "right" automation level is not a fixed number -- it is the mean between doing too little and doing too much for *this project, at this moment, in this situation*.

### Poiesis and Praxis

Aristotle distinguishes two kinds of rational activity: poiesis (making) and praxis (doing). Poiesis is production -- it aims at an artifact external to the activity. Building a house is poiesis; the house is the product, and the building is merely the means. Praxis is action whose end is internal to the activity -- acting well IS the end, not a means to some further product. Ethics and politics are praxis: the goal of ethical deliberation is not to produce a "decision artifact" but to act wisely.

Techne governs poiesis. Phronesis governs praxis. Confusing them -- treating practical action as if it were artifact production -- is a fundamental category error. A system that evaluates deliberation quality by counting artifacts produced (decisions made, documents written, files created) is measuring poiesis, not praxis. The quality of a deliberation cannot be assessed by examining its outputs alone; it depends on whether the deliberative process was conducted well, and this is a judgment that no output metric captures.

### Hexis (Disposition/Habit)

Virtue is not a feeling, not a faculty, and not a single action. It is a *hexis* -- a stable disposition developed through practice (NE II.1, 1103a14-18). "We become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, brave by doing brave acts." Hexis is not mere habit in the modern behaviorist sense (automatic response). It is an intelligent disposition: the phronimos acts from understanding, not from mechanical repetition. But it does require repetition -- virtue cannot be acquired from a single correct action or a single configuration file. It develops over time through iterated practice in diverse situations.

The implication for system design is that a system's "character" -- its patterns of response, its calibration, its accumulated dispositions -- is built through practice, not through initial setup. A freshly installed system has no hexis. It has rules but no judgment, configuration but no wisdom. Wisdom, to the extent a system can approximate it, accrues through the accumulation of signals, lessons, calibrated thresholds, and refined heuristics across many development cycles.

## Relevance to GSD Reflect

GSD Reflect is an attempt to formalize and automate aspects of practical reasoning about software development. Aristotle's framework reveals both the promise and the fundamental limits of this attempt.

**The central tension is this:** GSD Reflect's signal-to-lesson pipeline, its automation levels, its sensor architecture, its deliberation documents -- all of these are *techne*. They are craft knowledge about how to produce artifacts (signals, lessons, plans, summaries). But the *quality* of the system's operation is a question of *phronesis*: was the right signal triaged? Was the lesson genuinely warranted? Was the automation level appropriate for this project at this moment? Phronesis cannot be reduced to techne. The system can be technically excellent -- sensors firing, lessons accumulating, automation triggering -- and still lack practical wisdom, because practical wisdom is not a feature that can be implemented. It is a judgment that emerges (or fails to emerge) from the encounter between rules and particular situations.

**The biggest gap is the minor premise.** The system's sensors detect patterns (file churn, fix-fix-fix chains, CI failures). These are minor-premise perceptions: "this situation has property X." The reflector identifies recurrence and clusters signals into patterns. The lesson engine distills general rules. But the crucial judgment -- *what does this pattern mean for this project right now?* -- is precisely where phronesis is needed and where the system is weakest. A signal that "CI failed 5 consecutive times" admits many interpretations: the test environment is misconfigured, the tests are wrong, the code is broken, the CI pipeline is stale, the developer is bypassing CI intentionally for good reasons. The sensor can detect the fact. It cannot perceive the significance. The reflector can note the recurrence. It cannot judge whether the recurrence matters.

**Euboulia challenges the system's conception of deliberation quality.** The v1.17 deliberation system (M-C) aims to formalize deliberation. But if Aristotle is right, formalizing deliberation risks confusing the form of good deliberation with its substance. A deliberation document can follow all the structural requirements -- multiple perspectives, counter-evidence, assumptions surfaced, tensions noted -- and still fail as deliberation if it addresses the wrong question, proceeds at the wrong time, or considers perspectives that are formally diverse but substantively uniform. Euboulia is not a schema; it is an achievement.

**Automation levels are a crude but necessary approximation of the mean.** Aristotle would recognize the automation level system (0-3) as an attempt to locate the mean between excess and deficiency in system intervention. Level 0 (manual) is deficiency: the system withholds help when it could assist. Level 3 (auto) risks excess: the system acts when judgment should pause. But Aristotle would also note that a four-point scale is a blunt instrument for what should be a continuously variable, situation-sensitive judgment. The per-feature overrides and context-aware deferral (AUTO-04) are gestures toward context-sensitivity, but the real mean -- how much automation is appropriate *right now* for *this specific action* in *this specific project state* -- requires a kind of judgment the system cannot perform.

**The system optimizes for poiesis and has no framework for evaluating praxis.** Every metric the system tracks is a poiesis metric: plans completed, tasks done, files modified, tests passing, signals resolved. These measure production. But the system has no way to evaluate its own praxis: was the deliberation wise? Was the automation decision judicious? Was the signal triage perceptive? These are praxis questions, and they cannot be answered by counting artifacts. The system can tell you that it produced 7 lessons in v1.16. It cannot tell you whether those lessons were wise.

**Hexis explains system maturity better than configuration.** A newly initialized GSD project has `config.json` with default values. It has no signals, no lessons, no reflection history, no calibrated thresholds. Over successive development cycles, it accumulates: signals reveal recurring problems, lessons codify responses, automation levels are tuned to the developer's preferences, sensor thresholds are calibrated to the project's noise profile. This accumulation is hexis -- the development of stable dispositions through practice. The system's "character" at milestone 5 is fundamentally different from its character at milestone 1, not because it was configured differently but because it has *practiced* differently. This is why initial configuration matters less than the system's capacity to learn from practice.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Euboulia Applied to the CI Integration Deliberation

The v1.17 roadmap deliberation (`.planning/deliberations/v1.17-plus-roadmap-deliberation.md`) produced the decision to build a CI sensor, implement auto-triggering, and create an extensible sensor architecture. Euboulia asks not merely whether this conclusion was correct, but whether the deliberative process that produced it met Aristotle's three conditions.

**Right end (orthos skopos)?** The deliberation was prompted by signal `sig-2026-03-02-ci-failures-ignored-throughout-v116` -- five consecutive CI failures bypassed via admin push over 23 days. The end of the deliberation was to prevent this class of process failure from recurring. This is a genuinely good end, not merely a convenient one. It addresses a real problem with real consequences (the system's self-improvement mechanism failed to detect its own CI process failure). The end was not invented by the deliberation; it was forced by evidence. Euboulia is satisfied on this dimension.

**Right way (orthos tropos)?** Here the assessment is more mixed. The deliberation considered multiple architectural approaches (hardcoded CI sensor vs. extensible sensor architecture, boolean toggles vs. unified automation levels, manual vs. auto-triggered collection). Counter-positions were engaged: the deliberation notes that auto-triggering risks context exhaustion, that Level 3 automation could act when it should not, that the extensible architecture adds complexity. The philosophical foundations phase subjected the design to six epistemological frameworks, surfacing tensions and limitations.

But there is a gap. The deliberation considered perspectives *within the system's existing framework* -- different technical approaches to the same problem. It did not consider whether the problem might be *mis-framed*. The CI failures were treated as a detection problem (no sensor covers CI), leading to a detection solution (build a CI sensor). An Aristotelian would ask: is the problem really about detection, or about the team's relationship to CI? The developer bypassed CI for 23 days. A sensor that *detects* this bypass does not address *why* it happened. Perhaps the tests were perceived as irrelevant. Perhaps the CI environment was known to be misconfigured. Perhaps the developer judged (correctly or not) that the cost of fixing CI exceeded the benefit. These are phronesis questions about the *significance* of the CI failures, not techne questions about how to detect them.

The deliberation's minor premise -- "CI failures were ignored, therefore we need a CI failure sensor" -- skipped the interpretive step. A more complete minor-premise perception would be: "CI failures were ignored *because* the tests were environment-dependent and the developer judged fixing them as lower priority than shipping features." This reframing might lead to a different major premise: not "all CI failures should trigger signals" but "CI failures caused by environment misconfiguration should be distinguished from CI failures caused by code defects, and the two require different responses."

**Right time (en to deonti chrono)?** The deliberation occurred at the v1.16/v1.17 boundary -- after the CI failures had persisted for 23 days and after the milestone had shipped. This is late. Euboulia would demand earlier detection. But there is a subtler temporal question: was the deliberation premature *in its scope*? The v1.17 deliberation planned 8 phases across 49 requirements before any execution. Aristotle warned against excessive deliberation about things that are uncertain -- "we deliberate about things that are in our power and can be done" (NE III.3, 1112a30). Planning 49 requirements before executing any risks deliberating about futures that may not materialize as expected. The mean between premature action and excessive planning is itself a phronesis judgment.

**Euboulia verdict:** The deliberation scores well on right end, moderately on right way (good within its frame, but the frame itself was not questioned), and ambiguously on right time (timely relative to the milestone boundary, but late relative to the underlying problem, and possibly premature in scope). A fully eubouletic deliberation would have questioned whether CI failure detection is the right frame for the problem, not just whether the CI sensor is the right technical solution.

### Example 2: The Mean in Signal Collection Frequency

Signal collection frequency illustrates the mean between excess and deficiency with concrete operational consequences.

**Deficiency (too little collection):** Before v1.17, signal collection is purely manual. The developer invokes `/gsd:collect-signals` when they remember to, typically at phase boundaries or when they notice something went wrong. Result: signals from v1.16 show 79 signals with 62 untriaged. Many signals were detected weeks after the underlying problem. CI failures persisted for 23 days undetected. The system's "perception" was intermittent and biased toward consciously noticed problems. This is the vice of deficiency -- the system withholds its perceptual capacity when it could be exercised.

**Excess (too much collection):** Consider the extreme of auto-collecting after every file save, every commit, every tool invocation. The artifact sensor would run hundreds of times per phase. Each run consumes context. The reflector would be overwhelmed with micro-signals: "this commit message was terse," "this file was created without a plan reference," "this test was added after implementation." Most of these observations are individually true and collectively useless. The developer would face signal fatigue -- too many low-severity findings drowning the important ones. Context budget would be consumed by observation, leaving less for execution. This is the vice of excess -- the system intervenes when restraint is appropriate.

**The mean:** SIG-01 implements collection after every phase execution. This is a discrete, bounded cadence: one collection per major unit of work. It captures problems close to their origin without continuous surveillance. AUTO-04 adds context-aware deferral: if the context budget is above a threshold, auto-collection downgrades to nudge (suggesting collection rather than performing it). REFL-04 adds a session-scoped cooldown: reflection triggers at most once per session, preventing the reflector from consuming unbounded context.

But Aristotle would say this "mean" is still a rule, not a judgment. The *true* mean for signal collection is not "after every phase" -- it is "when collection would be valuable given the current state of the project." After a smooth, zero-deviation phase in a mature project, collection adds little. After a chaotic phase with multiple deviations and a fix-fix-fix chain, collection is urgent. The right frequency depends on the situation, and a fixed cadence approximates the mean without achieving it.

A more phronesis-informed design would modulate collection frequency based on indicators of turbulence: high file churn, many commits, long phase duration, deviation from plan scope. When these indicators are high, collection is more valuable. When they are low, collection can be deferred. The per-feature override system partially enables this (the developer can set `signal_collection` to a different level), but the judgment of *when* to adjust remains with the developer.

**The Aristotelian lesson:** The mean is not a fixed point on a scale. It is a continuously varying target that requires perception of particulars. Any fixed rule ("collect after every phase," "collect every N hours," "collect when deviation exceeds threshold") is an approximation that will sometimes be too much and sometimes too little. The system should be designed to make adjustment easy and its consequences visible, rather than to find the "optimal" fixed frequency -- because no fixed frequency is optimal across all situations.

## Tensions and Limitations

### 1. Can AI Possess Phronesis?

This is the fundamental objection to applying Aristotle's framework to an automated system. Phronesis, for Aristotle, is an intellectual virtue of a *person* -- a rational agent with desires, experiences, a life history, and a stake in the outcome. Phronesis requires not just rule-following but *perception of particulars*, and this perception is informed by the phronimos's character, experience, and emotional attunement. Nussbaum argues that emotions are constitutive of practical wisdom, not obstacles to it: fear helps the courageous person perceive danger; compassion helps the just person perceive suffering.

An AI system has none of this. GSD Reflect does not perceive situations; it pattern-matches text. It does not judge significance; it applies heuristics. It does not have a stake in the outcome; it has no outcomes that matter to it. When the reflector identifies a "pattern" across signals, this is not perception in Aristotle's sense -- it is syntactic analysis that produces results structurally similar to perception. The similarity is useful for design purposes, but it is not identity.

The honest Aristotelian assessment is that GSD Reflect cannot possess phronesis and should not claim to. What it can do is *support the developer's phronesis* by presenting information in forms that aid human perception and judgment. The system is a tool -- techne, not phronesis. Its quality is measured by how well it supports the phronimos (the developer), not by whether it can substitute for one. This is a genuine limitation, not a problem to be solved by better algorithms. Phronesis may be the kind of thing that requires being a living agent embedded in a practice, and no amount of engineering can bridge that gap.

**Mitigation (partial):** Design the system as an *assistant to phronesis*, not a replacement for it. Concretely: present multiple interpretations rather than single verdicts. Surface uncertainty rather than hiding it. Make the system's reasoning transparent so the developer can evaluate it. Never auto-act on high-stakes decisions (a reason to be cautious with Level 3 automation for consequential actions). The system's techne should make the developer's phronesis more effective, not less necessary.

### 2. Particular Judgment vs. Generalizable Rules

Aristotle insists that practical wisdom deals with *particulars* -- this situation, this agent, this moment. But a software system necessarily operates through *general rules*: if pattern X, then signal type Y; if signal count > N, then trigger reflection; if automation level >= 2, then prompt. Every rule generalizes away the particular. The system cannot treat each situation as unique because it has no mechanism for perceiving uniqueness -- it perceives only features that its sensors are designed to detect.

This tension between the particular and the general is not just a technical limitation. It reflects a deep philosophical disagreement between Aristotle's particularism and the universalism that software systems embody. Every schema, every enum, every threshold is a universalizing act -- it says "all instances of this type should be treated identically." Aristotle says they should not: the mean for courage depends on the particular person in the particular situation.

The tension is irresolvable in principle but manageable in practice. The system should be *more* particularist than it currently is -- more sensitive to project-specific context, development-phase-specific conditions, and recent history -- without pretending to achieve the full particularity that phronesis demands. Per-feature overrides, context-aware deferral, and configurable thresholds are steps toward particularity. They are not phronesis, but they are better than rigid universalism.

**Mitigation:** Increase the system's sensitivity to context. When surfacing a lesson, include the conditions under which it was learned and let the developer judge applicability. When triggering automation, include the reasoning ("triggering because signal density is above threshold in the last 3 phases") so the developer can evaluate whether the general rule applies to this particular case. The system should explain its reasoning, not just its conclusions.

### 3. Virtue Ethics for a System Without Character

Aristotle's ethical framework presupposes a *character* -- a stable set of dispositions developed through habituated practice within a community. Character is not programmed; it grows. It is tested by novel situations that rules cannot anticipate. It is shaped by the agent's history of choices, each of which reinforces or weakens existing dispositions.

GSD Reflect has something analogous to character -- its accumulated lessons, calibrated thresholds, and tuned automation levels constitute a set of stable dispositions. But the analogy breaks down in important ways. The system's "dispositions" are stored data, not embodied habits. They can be deleted, reset, or overridden at will. The system does not *resist* changes to its character the way a person does -- it has no attachment to its own dispositions. And it has no internal experience of acting from character; it simply applies rules derived from past data.

MacIntyre would add a deeper objection: virtue is intelligible only within a *practice* -- a cooperative human activity with internal goods and standards of excellence that evolve historically. Software development is arguably such a practice, but the AI system is not a *practitioner* in MacIntyre's sense. It does not pursue the internal goods of software development (elegance, reliability, maintainability as experienced by the developer). It pursues metrics that approximate those goods. The difference between pursuing a good and pursuing a metric that correlates with it is precisely the difference between praxis and techne.

**Mitigation:** Treat the system's accumulated state (KB, signal history, calibrated settings) as *institutional memory*, not as *character*. Institutional memory is valuable -- it preserves past learning and informs future action. But it is not character, and pretending otherwise risks the kind of anthropomorphization that leads to misplaced trust in automated judgment. The system should present its history as evidence, not as wisdom.

### 4. The Formalization Paradox

There is a recursive irony in writing a formal document about the limits of formalization. This deliberation document follows a schema (Core Ideas, Concept Mapping, Praxis Recommendations, Citable Principles) to argue that practical wisdom cannot be captured in schemas. The `/gsd:deliberate` skill, when formalized, will structure deliberation to argue that deliberation resists structuring.

Aristotle would recognize this as a version of the problem he himself faced: he wrote systematic treatises about the limits of systematic knowledge. His response was that *some* formalization is appropriate -- it is good to articulate general principles -- as long as we remember that the principles are starting points for judgment, not substitutes for it. "The agents themselves must in each case consider what is appropriate to the occasion" (NE II.2, 1104a8-10).

The praxis recommendation is not to abandon formalization but to hold it with appropriate modesty: every schema, every checklist, every automation rule is a *beginning* of practical reasoning, not its *end*. The system should make clear, at every point where formalized rules produce a recommendation, that the recommendation is a starting point for judgment, not a verdict.

## Praxis Recommendations

1. **Design automation as an assistant to phronesis, not a replacement for it.** At every point where the system produces a recommendation (signal triage, lesson surfacing, automation trigger), present the reasoning alongside the conclusion. Include the evidence, the alternative interpretations, and the uncertainty. The developer's phronesis -- their judgment of what this situation demands -- is the irreducible element that no automation can supply. The system's value is measured by how well it supports this judgment, not by how much it supplants it. *Cite: aristotle/phronesis, aristotle/techne-phronesis-distinction.*

2. **Evaluate deliberations for euboulia, not just completeness.** When the deliberation system (M-C) is formalized, include lightweight euboulia heuristics: Does this deliberation address a problem evidenced by signals or concrete experience (right end)? Were counter-positions genuinely engaged -- not just listed but substantively addressed (right way)? Was this deliberation triggered by evidence rather than by anxiety or routine (right time)? These heuristics cannot guarantee euboulia, but they can flag its absence. *Cite: aristotle/euboulia.*

3. **Invest in minor-premise quality in signal interpretation.** The system's sensors detect facts; the critical step is interpreting what those facts *mean* in this particular context. When a sensor emits a signal, it should generate multiple candidate interpretations rather than a single classification. The reflector should weigh these interpretations against project context, recent history, and developer annotations. The goal is to improve the system's *perception* of particulars, which is where the deliberative syllogism most often fails. *Cite: aristotle/deliberative-syllogism, aristotle/phronesis.*

4. **Track the consequences of automation level choices to approximate the mean.** The mean between automation excess and deficiency cannot be determined a priori. It must be discovered through practice and feedback. Track what happens after each automation level is applied: did Level 3 produce context exhaustion or signal fatigue (excess)? Did Level 1 allow problems to persist undetected (deficiency)? Use this evidence to adjust the system's default recommendations over time. This is not phronesis -- it is empirical approximation of what phronesis would recommend. *Cite: aristotle/mesotes, aristotle/hexis.*

5. **Distinguish poiesis metrics from praxis evaluation.** The system currently measures only poiesis: plans completed, files modified, tests passing, signals resolved. Introduce qualitative praxis markers: when a lesson is surfaced and the developer acts on it, capture whether the lesson was *appropriate* to the situation (not just whether it was surfaced). When an automation trigger fires, capture whether the trigger was *judicious* (not just whether it fired). These annotations are the beginning of a praxis evaluation that goes beyond artifact counting. *Cite: aristotle/poiesis-praxis.*

6. **Model system maturity as hexis, not as configuration.** A freshly installed system and a system that has processed 200 signals, distilled 40 lessons, and calibrated its automation over 5 milestones have the same configuration schema but radically different dispositions. Make this maturity visible: track development cycles completed, lessons applied and validated, automation levels adjusted from evidence, sensor thresholds calibrated. Present maturity as a project property that develops through practice, reinforcing the understanding that the system's value grows with use. *Cite: aristotle/hexis.*

7. **Make formalized rules transparent about their limits.** Every automated rule (sensor threshold, triage heuristic, automation trigger) should be presented as a *starting point for judgment*, not a verdict. When the system recommends an action based on a rule, include the rule's rationale and the conditions under which the rule might not apply. This supports the developer's phronesis by making the system's reasoning available for evaluation rather than hiding it behind a recommendation. *Cite: aristotle/phronesis, aristotle/formalization-limits.*

8. **Accept the techne-phronesis boundary as a permanent design constraint.** The system is techne. The developer supplies phronesis. This is not a deficiency to be overcome by better AI but a structural feature of the relationship between tools and practitioners. Design decisions should be evaluated against this boundary: does this feature help the developer reason about the situation (supporting phronesis), or does it try to reason on the developer's behalf (substituting techne for phronesis)? The former is appropriate; the latter risks misplaced confidence in formalized judgment. *Cite: aristotle/techne-phronesis-distinction.*

## Citable Principles

- **aristotle/phronesis**: Practical wisdom is the capacity to perceive what a particular situation demands and to act accordingly. It cannot be reduced to rules, procedures, or algorithms. A system that follows rules perfectly may still lack practical wisdom if it cannot perceive when the rules do not apply. Phronesis is the judgment that remains necessary after all formalization has been applied.

- **aristotle/euboulia**: Excellence in deliberation requires three conditions: the deliberation addresses a genuinely good end (not just any goal), proceeds in the right way (genuine engagement with counter-positions, not performative diversity), and occurs at the right time (neither premature nor belated). A deliberation that reaches the right conclusion by accident does not exhibit euboulia. The process matters, not just the outcome.

- **aristotle/deliberative-syllogism**: Practical reasoning has the structure: general rule (major premise) + perception of this situation (minor premise) = action (conclusion). The minor premise -- perceiving what the situation IS -- is the critical and hardest step. Systems fail at practical reasoning not because they lack rules but because they misperceive situations. Investing in minor-premise quality (richer context, multiple interpretations, explicit uncertainty) improves practical reasoning more than adding more rules.

- **aristotle/mesotes**: The right course of action is a mean between excess and deficiency, determined relative to the particular situation. There is no formula for the mean -- it requires judgment. Any fixed scale (automation levels 0-3, severity scales, threshold values) is an approximation of a judgment that should be continuously variable. The approximation is useful but should be held with appropriate humility.

- **aristotle/poiesis-praxis**: Making (poiesis) aims at an external product and is governed by techne. Doing (praxis) aims at acting well and is governed by phronesis. Evaluating a system solely by its outputs (artifacts produced, tasks completed, metrics met) measures poiesis. Evaluating whether the system *acted well* -- whether its recommendations were wise, its timing judicious, its interpretations perceptive -- requires praxis evaluation, which cannot be reduced to output metrics.

- **aristotle/hexis**: Practical excellence is a stable disposition developed through repeated practice in diverse situations, not a one-time configuration or a checklist. A system's "character" -- its accumulated lessons, calibrated thresholds, tuned automation, and refined heuristics -- develops over time through iterated engagement with real problems. Initial configuration matters less than the capacity to learn from practice.

- **aristotle/techne-phronesis-distinction**: Techne (craft knowledge) and phronesis (practical wisdom) are fundamentally different intellectual virtues. Techne can be formalized, taught, and automated. Phronesis cannot -- it requires perception of particulars, emotional attunement, and a stake in the outcome. A system is techne; a developer exercises phronesis. Designing a system to support phronesis is appropriate. Designing a system to replace phronesis is a category error.

- **aristotle/formalization-limits**: Formalization (schemas, rules, workflows, checklists) is a necessary starting point for practical reasoning, not its completion. Aristotle formalized practical reasoning precisely to show that practical reasoning exceeds formalization. Every rule should be presented as a beginning of judgment, not a verdict. A system that treats its own formalizations as sufficient has confused techne with phronesis.
