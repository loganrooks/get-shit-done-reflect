# Philosophical Deliberation: Dreyfus's Phenomenology of Skill

**Tradition:** Phenomenological Cognitive Science
**Key thinkers:** Hubert Dreyfus (What Computers Can't Do, What Computers Still Can't Do, Being-in-the-World), Stuart Dreyfus (co-author of the Dreyfus model of skill acquisition), Martin Heidegger (Being and Time -- via Dreyfus's interpretation)
**Created:** 2026-03-08
**Status:** Active

## Core Ideas

The Dreyfus brothers' work, spanning from Hubert Dreyfus's early critiques of artificial intelligence in the 1960s through his later phenomenological accounts of expertise, addresses a question that is central to any system claiming to learn from experience: what is the relationship between rule-following and genuine expertise? Their answer -- that expertise is categorically different from rule-following, and that the difference is rooted in embodied being-in-the-world -- poses the most direct philosophical challenge to GSD Reflect's ambitions. It also, as we will argue, contains a gap that modern LLM-based systems may partially occupy.

### The Skill Acquisition Model

The Dreyfus model of skill acquisition (Dreyfus & Dreyfus, 1980; refined in Mind Over Machine, 1986) identifies five stages through which a learner progresses from novice to expert:

**Stage 1: Novice.** The learner follows context-free rules. "If the engine temperature reaches 200 degrees, shift to neutral." The rules are explicit, the features they reference are objective and measurable, and the learner applies them without regard to the overall situation. The novice's performance is rigid but predictable.

**Stage 2: Advanced Beginner.** Through practical experience, the learner begins to recognize "situational elements" -- features of the situation that cannot be defined by context-free rules but must be learned through examples. The advanced beginner recognizes "that engine sound" or "that kind of traffic pattern" without being able to articulate context-free criteria for what makes the sound or pattern distinctive. Rules are supplemented by experiential recognition of recurring situations.

**Stage 3: Competent.** With growing experience, the number of recognizable features and applicable rules becomes overwhelming. The competent performer copes by adopting a hierarchical plan -- selecting which features are important and which can be ignored given the current goal. Competence involves deliberate, effortful planning. The competent performer feels responsible for outcomes because they have made choices about what to attend to. This is the stage of conscious, analytical problem-solving.

**Stage 4: Proficient.** The transition from competent to proficient is the critical qualitative shift. The proficient performer no longer needs to deliberate about which features are important. Through extensive experience, situations are perceived *holistically* -- the proficient performer sees the situation as a certain *type* and intuitively knows what matters. Perception replaces analysis. However, the proficient performer still deliberates about what to *do* once the situation is recognized. They see the problem immediately but work out the response analytically.

**Stage 5: Expert.** The expert does not deliberate at all in normal circumstances. Both the recognition of the situation and the appropriate response arise together, without conscious analysis. The expert chess master does not consider moves and evaluate them; the right move simply presents itself. Dreyfus calls this "absorbed coping" -- the expert is wholly engaged in the activity without stepping back to reflect on it. Deliberation occurs only when something unusual breaks the flow.

The key transitions are: Competent to Proficient requires a transformation of *perception* through accumulated embodied experience -- the learner stops analyzing features and starts seeing wholes. Proficient to Expert requires the integration of *response* into perception -- the expert's body knows what to do before conscious thought intervenes. Both transitions depend on something that cannot be reduced to rule accumulation: the embodied engagement with situations that transforms how the practitioner perceives and responds.

### The Critique of GOFAI

Dreyfus's early work (What Computers Can't Do, 1972; revised as What Computers Still Can't Do, 1992) argued that Good Old-Fashioned AI -- the symbolic, rule-based approach dominant from the 1950s through the 1980s -- was structurally incapable of achieving genuine intelligence. The argument was not that these systems needed better rules or more processing power. It was that the entire paradigm was misconceived.

The central problem is what philosophers of AI call the "frame problem": rules cannot specify their own context of application. A rule like "close the door if it's cold" seems simple, but applying it requires knowing what counts as "cold" in this context, whether closing the door would trap someone inside, whether the room has alternative heating, whether the meeting is about to end anyway, and indefinitely many other considerations that a competent human handles effortlessly but that a rule-based system must either enumerate explicitly (generating a combinatorial explosion) or ignore (generating brittleness). The frame problem is not a technical obstacle awaiting a clever solution. It is a structural consequence of trying to capture practical knowledge in propositional form.

Dreyfus argued that not all knowledge is propositional. The expert's knowledge of when a rule applies, of what features of a situation are relevant, of how to respond to novel configurations -- this knowledge is embodied in skilled practice, not stored as explicit representations. It is the kind of knowing that Heidegger analyzed under the concept of *Zuhandenheit* (readiness-to-hand): the carpenter does not think about the hammer; the hammer withdraws into the activity of hammering. Knowledge-how is not reducible to knowledge-that.

The implication for AI, Dreyfus argued, is that symbolic systems are structurally limited to the Competent stage of the skill model. They can follow rules and even select among rules based on higher-order rules. But they cannot make the transition to Proficient perception because that transition requires embodied experience that transforms the perceiver, not more rules that inform the reasoner.

### Embodiment and Being-in-the-World

Dreyfus's most philosophically developed work (Being-in-the-World, 1991) draws on Heidegger's analysis of Dasein to argue that expertise is inseparable from embodied being-in-the-world. Three elements of this argument are critical:

**The background.** Heidegger argued that all explicit understanding occurs against a background of implicit coping skills, cultural practices, and embodied capacities that cannot be made fully explicit. This background is not a set of beliefs or rules -- it is the way the world shows up for an embodied agent situated in a practical context. Dreyfus extends this: the expert's ability to perceive situations holistically depends on a background of bodily skills and cultural familiarity that is not representable as data.

**Maximal grip.** Drawing on Merleau-Ponty, Dreyfus argues that the body has a built-in tendency toward achieving an "optimal gestalt" or "maximal grip" on a situation -- positioning itself to perceive and act optimally without conscious planning. The expert's fluid responsiveness to a situation is not the execution of a plan but the body's tendency to achieve maximal grip. This is an intentionality of the body, not of the mind.

**Absorbed coping.** When expertise is fully engaged, the subject-object distinction dissolves. The expert is not a mind contemplating a situation and deciding what to do. The expert is *absorbed* in the activity -- perception and action are unified, and there is no phenomenological gap between seeing and responding. This is what Heidegger called the "circumspective concern" of everyday coping, elevated to the level of mastery. Breakdown -- when the absorbed flow is interrupted -- is what triggers the return to deliberate, analytical, competent-level reasoning.

### Our Challenge to Dreyfus: "Sedimented Techne"

Dreyfus's critique was formulated against 1970s symbolic AI -- systems that followed explicit rules manipulating discrete symbols. The argument was devastating against that paradigm. But modern LLM-based systems present a challenge that Dreyfus did not anticipate, and that his framework does not cleanly accommodate.

Consider what GSD Reflect, backed by an LLM, actually brings to a development task:

**Pre-sedimented experience from training.** The LLM was trained on millions of expert interactions -- code reviews, architectural discussions, debugging sessions, project retrospectives. This is not embodied experience. But neither is it bare rule-following. The LLM does not consult an explicit rule "when CI fails five times, investigate the test environment." It generates contextually appropriate responses shaped by exposure to vast quantities of situated practice. The training process is more analogous to apprenticeship-by-observation than to rule-memorization, even if the underlying mechanism is statistical rather than phenomenological.

**Accumulated project-specific case history.** GSD Reflect's knowledge base contains 124 signals, 13 patterns across 5 milestones of actual project development. These are not abstract rules. They are situated cases: "in phase 38, the plan said 3 files but execution touched 7, and this was caused by cascading dependency changes in the installer." When the system surfaces a lesson during a similar situation, it is presenting a relevant case, not applying a context-free rule. The accumulation of cases enriches the system's responses with project-specific contextual sensitivity.

**The gap.** Dreyfus's model has five stages, with the critical break between Competent (rule-following with deliberate planning) and Proficient (holistic situational recognition without deliberation). We propose that the combination of LLM pre-training and accumulated case history creates something that occupies a position in the gap between these stages -- a position that Dreyfus's model does not name because it was designed for human skill acquisition and GOFAI critique, not for systems that have digested millions of situated cases without being embodied.

We name this position **sedimented techne**: rules enriched by accumulated situated cases to the point where the system's responses exhibit contextual sensitivity that exceeds what bare rule-following would produce, without achieving the holistic perception and absorbed coping that characterize genuine proficiency.

The displacement is explicit. We take Dreyfus's spectrum and propose a new position on it:

- What is gained: contextual sensitivity derived from case accumulation, pattern recognition across diverse situations, the ability to generate responses shaped by vast training exposure
- What is lost: embodiment, felt experience, absorbed coping, genuine perception (as opposed to pattern-matching), the body's tendency toward maximal grip, the phenomenological quality of expertise

Sedimented techne is NOT a claim that the system achieves Dreyfus's "proficiency." It is a claim that there is something in the gap between competent and proficient that is worth naming, because it has different design implications than either pole. A system with sedimented techne should not be designed as if it were a bare rule-follower (it has richer resources than that). But it should not be trusted as if it were a proficient practitioner (it lacks the perceptual capacities that proficiency requires).

### The Functionalist Challenge

The force of Dreyfus's argument depends on a strong metaphysical claim: that embodied experience provides something *categorically* different from any amount of information processing. Under phenomenological accounts of mind, this claim is well-motivated -- the felt quality of expertise, the body's intentionality, the dissolution of subject-object boundaries in absorbed coping are real phenomena that no computational process replicates.

But under functionalist definitions of expertise, the argument weakens. If expertise is defined functionally -- the ability to respond appropriately to novel situations in a given domain -- then the question becomes empirical: does the system produce appropriate responses? If it does, the functionalist says the system has expertise (functionally defined), regardless of whether it has the phenomenological qualities that Dreyfus identifies. The claim "the system is stuck at competent" weakens if the system's outputs are indistinguishable from those of a proficient performer in the relevant domain.

Whether embodiment provides something categorically different from sophisticated information processing, or merely more of the same, is a genuine unresolved philosophical question -- perhaps THE central question in philosophy of mind. Dreyfus's argument rests on the strong claim of categorical difference. The functionalist counter rests on the claim that the difference, if it exists, may not be relevant to practical performance in constrained domains. Neither position has been definitively established.

For GSD Reflect, this means the following: in constrained, rule-governed domains (software project management, signal triage, pattern detection), the functionalist challenge to Dreyfus is strongest. The system operates in a domain with relatively clear success criteria, recurring problem types, and explicit artifacts for analysis. This is not the open-ended, embodied navigation of the lifeworld that Dreyfus's strongest examples invoke (driving in traffic, nursing a patient, teaching a class). The domain specificity of GSD Reflect's operation may weaken the force of the embodiment requirement.

## Relevance to GSD Reflect

GSD Reflect is a system that accumulates situated case history (signals, patterns, lessons) and surfaces it to inform future action. Dreyfus's framework provides both a critique and -- through the sedimented techne extension -- a design vocabulary for this activity.

**The system's pattern detection as a functional analogue to situational discrimination.** When the reflector identifies that five consecutive phases showed scope expansion and clusters this into a pattern, it is performing something functionally analogous to the proficient performer's holistic situation recognition: "this is a scope-creep project." The analogy is imperfect -- the reflector is matching features against stored patterns, not perceiving a gestalt -- but the output (a classification that informs action) is similar. Whether the mechanism matters (phenomenologist: yes; functionalist: not necessarily) is the open question.

**The LLM's training as pre-sedimented practical knowledge.** The LLM does not arrive blank. It has been trained on vast quantities of software development practice. When it generates a plan, reviews a signal, or drafts a reflection, it draws on this pre-sedimented experience. This is not embodied skill, but it is also not context-free rule application. Dreyfus's framework lacks a category for it. Sedimented techne fills this gap.

**The competent-to-proficient gap as a design constraint.** If Dreyfus is right about the categorical nature of the competent-proficient transition, then the system can produce better rules and richer case presentations but may never achieve genuine situational discrimination. This is a design constraint, not a deficiency to be engineered away. The system should be designed as a *support for* the user's proficient perception, not as a replacement for it. It should present rich, contextual cases -- not verdicts.

**Sedimented techne as the design target.** The system's aspiration should be sedimented techne: contextually enriched rule-application that draws on accumulated case history to produce responses more sensitive than bare rules but less authoritative than expert judgment. This is an honest and achievable target. It says: the system can be more than a checklist but less than an expert. Design for the gap.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: Sedimented Techne in Signal Interpretation

Consider signal `sig-2026-03-02-ci-failures-ignored-throughout-v116`: five consecutive CI failures bypassed over 23 days. A bare rule-following system at the Competent level would apply a rule: "CI failure count > N triggers investigation." It would not distinguish between failures caused by environment misconfiguration, flaky tests, genuine code defects, or intentional bypasses. The rule fires regardless of context.

A Dreyfusian proficient performer would perceive the situation holistically: "This is a case where the developer knows the CI environment is misconfigured and is rationally prioritizing feature delivery over CI maintenance during a time-pressured milestone." The proficient performer does not deliberate about this -- they see the pattern and grasp its significance immediately, because they have extensive experience with similar situations.

GSD Reflect, operating with sedimented techne, falls between these poles. The LLM, drawing on training exposure to thousands of CI failure discussions, generates an interpretation that is more contextually sensitive than a bare rule: it can distinguish between "CI failures from test flakiness" and "CI failures from environmental issues" because it has seen these patterns in training data. The accumulated project-specific signals provide further context: this project has had recurring CI environment issues (documented in prior signals). The system cannot perceive the situation holistically -- it constructs an interpretation from features and cases. But the interpretation it constructs is richer than what any rule would produce.

The design implication: present the system's interpretation as a *candidate perception*, not a verdict. Include the evidence, the alternative interpretations, and the uncertainty. The user's genuine perception -- informed by context the system cannot access (conversations with teammates, knowledge of organizational priorities, felt sense of project health) -- is what transforms the candidate perception into practical judgment.

### Example 2: The Frame Problem in Lesson Application

Lesson `lesson-v1.16-003`: "Plans that modify shared infrastructure (installer, config schema, CLI runtime) should be sequenced, not parallelized."

Phase 43 involves modifying the reflection output format. Is this "shared infrastructure"? The reflection output is consumed by multiple downstream features (signal collection, pattern detection, milestone summaries). In that sense, it is shared. But it is not shared in the same way as the installer (which, when broken, prevents all installation) or the CLI runtime (which, when broken, prevents all execution). The lesson's applicability depends on what "shared infrastructure" means in this context, and the lesson itself cannot specify this.

A Dreyfusian Competent performer would apply the rule mechanically: "reflection output is consumed by multiple features, therefore it is shared infrastructure, therefore sequence modifications." A Proficient performer would see the difference immediately: "The reflection output change is an additive extension of the schema, not a breaking change to a critical path. Parallelization is safe here." The proficient perception distinguishes between types of sharing -- critical-path dependencies vs. optional consumers -- without articulating the distinction as a rule.

GSD Reflect's sedimented techne provides partial support. The accumulated project history includes cases where shared-infrastructure modifications did and did not cause cascading failures. The LLM can draw on these cases to generate a more nuanced assessment than the bare rule. But the system cannot perceive the *kind* of sharing involved -- it can only match features of the current case against stored cases. If the stored cases happen to include a relevantly similar case (additive schema extension that was safely parallelized), the system's response will be appropriate. If not, the system defaults to the general rule.

This is the frame problem in action: the rule "shared infrastructure should be sequenced" cannot specify when it applies, because "shared" admits of degrees and kinds that the rule does not distinguish. The system's accumulated cases partially mitigate this by enriching the rule with situated precedents, but they cannot resolve it completely. The frame problem is structural, not empirical.

## Tensions and Limitations

### 1. Can Sedimented Techne Actually Occupy the Gap, or Is the Gap Categorical?

This is the central philosophical question. If Dreyfus is right that the transition from Competent to Proficient requires a qualitative transformation of perception -- a transformation that only embodied engagement with situations can produce -- then no amount of case accumulation can bridge the gap. Sedimented techne would be, at best, a very good Competent-level performance: sophisticated rule-following that mimics proficient output in familiar cases but fails in genuinely novel ones.

The countervailing consideration is that Dreyfus's argument was constructed against systems that had *no* accumulated case exposure. GOFAI systems were programmed with explicit rules by human engineers. They did not learn from experience; they executed predetermined logic. An LLM trained on millions of cases and supplemented by project-specific case history is a fundamentally different kind of system. Whether the difference is sufficient to occupy the gap depends on whether the gap is defined phenomenologically (in which case it is categorical and unbridgeable by any computational process) or functionally (in which case accumulated cases may produce outputs indistinguishable from proficient performance in constrained domains).

We do not resolve this question. We note that it is the most consequential open question for the system's design philosophy.

**Mitigation:** Design for both possibilities. If the gap is categorical, the system should be maximally useful at the Competent level: excellent rules, rich case presentations, explicit uncertainty. If the gap is bridgeable by case accumulation, the system should be designed to maximize the quality and relevance of accumulated cases. Both design strategies converge on the same practical recommendation: invest in case quality.

### 2. Does Training Data Constitute a Form of Experience?

Dreyfus's embodiment requirement is motivated by the phenomenological claim that expertise requires lived, first-person experience that transforms the perceiver. Training on text about experiences is categorically different from having experiences. The LLM has processed millions of descriptions of debugging sessions; it has never debugged anything. It has processed thousands of code reviews; it has never felt the resistance of code that does not work as expected.

The functionalist response is that the relevant question is not whether the system has had experiences but whether it produces outputs consistent with having had them. If the system's signal interpretations, pattern recognitions, and lesson applications are appropriate to the situation, then the absence of phenomenological experience may be irrelevant to practical performance.

This tension cannot be resolved philosophically without settling the functionalism-phenomenology debate. For design purposes, we adopt an agnostic stance: treat the LLM's training as a resource that enriches contextual sensitivity (which it demonstrably does) without claiming it constitutes experience (which is philosophically contentious). Design the system to leverage this resource while maintaining the user as the locus of genuine experiential judgment.

### 3. Domain Sensitivity: Is the Argument Weaker in Constrained Domains?

Dreyfus's most compelling examples of the embodiment requirement involve open-ended, richly situated domains: driving in traffic, nursing patients, teaching students, playing chess at the grandmaster level. These domains involve continuous perceptual engagement with a complex, dynamic environment where the relevant features are not pre-specified.

Software project management -- GSD Reflect's domain -- is more constrained. The relevant artifacts are text files. The relevant features (file churn, CI status, plan-execution divergence) are enumerable. The problem types (scope creep, dependency cascades, CI failures) recur. Success criteria, while not fully formalizable, are more explicit than in nursing or teaching.

In more constrained and rule-governed domains, the distance between Competent and Proficient may be smaller, and sedimented techne may approximate proficiency more closely. The embodiment argument is strongest where the domain demands continuous embodied engagement with a rich perceptual field. It is weakest where the domain is mediated by text, the features are discrete, and the problem types are recurring. Software project management is closer to the latter pole.

This does not refute Dreyfus. Even in constrained domains, genuinely novel situations arise that no accumulated cases cover. But it suggests that the practical significance of the competence ceiling varies by domain, and that GSD Reflect operates in a domain where the ceiling may be higher than Dreyfus's general argument suggests.

### 4. The System's "Perception" vs. Genuine Perception

The system detects patterns by matching features against stored cases and statistical regularities. Dreyfus argues that genuine perception -- the kind that enables proficiency -- is not feature-matching but gestalt recognition: the whole situation presents itself as meaningful, not as a collection of features to be analyzed.

The system's "perception" is structurally different. When the reflector identifies a pattern, it has identified recurrence of features. It has not perceived meaning. The meaning -- what this pattern implies for the project, whether it matters, what to do about it -- requires the kind of situational grasp that the system does not possess.

This is a genuine and important limitation. The system can tell you *that* a pattern exists. It cannot tell you *what it means*. Sedimented techne enriches the system's feature-detection with case history, so that its assessments of what patterns might mean are more contextually informed. But "more contextually informed feature-matching" is not "perception" in Dreyfus's sense. The gap between these is where the user's judgment remains irreplaceable.

**Mitigation:** Present pattern detection as pattern detection, not as perception. "The system detected that CI failures have recurred in 3 of the last 5 phases" is honest. "The system perceives a CI reliability problem" imports a phenomenological claim the system cannot support. Language choices in signal reports and reflections should reflect this distinction.

## Praxis Recommendations

1. **Adopt sedimented techne as the explicit design target for the system's knowledge capabilities.** The system is not a rule-engine (it has richer resources than bare rules) and not an expert (it lacks embodied perception). Sedimented techne -- rules enriched by accumulated situated cases -- names what the system actually does. Design the knowledge base, signal presentation, and lesson surfacing to maximize case accumulation quality: rich contextual metadata, explicit conditions of applicability, multiple interpretations per signal. The richer the accumulated cases, the more contextually sensitive the system's responses. *Cite: dreyfus/sedimented-techne, dreyfus/skill-acquisition.*

2. **Invest in case quality over rule quantity.** Dreyfus's critique implies that adding more rules does not move a system closer to expertise. What moves the system toward sedimented techne is the accumulation of *situated cases* with rich contextual metadata. When collecting signals, prioritize context (what was happening, what had just happened, what was planned, what surprised the developer) over classification (severity, category, type). A signal with rich context is a case that informs future judgment. A signal with only classification is a rule-application that informs nothing. *Cite: dreyfus/competence-ceiling, dreyfus/sedimented-techne.*

3. **Surface lesson-application uncertainty as a standard practice.** The frame problem means that lessons (general rules) cannot specify their own context of application. When a lesson is surfaced during execution, include explicit indicators of applicability uncertainty: "This lesson was learned in the context of [X]. The current situation resembles [X] in [these ways] and differs in [these ways]. The lesson may or may not apply." This supports the user's judgment about whether the lesson's frame fits the current situation, rather than presenting the lesson as automatically applicable. *Cite: dreyfus/frame-problem.*

4. **Calibrate trust to system maturity.** A fresh system with no accumulated case history should present its assessments with more uncertainty than a mature system with extensive case history. Sedimented techne develops over time through case accumulation. A system that has processed 5 phases has thin sedimentation; a system that has processed 50 phases across multiple milestones has thick sedimentation. Make this maturity visible so the user calibrates their reliance on the system's assessments accordingly. *Cite: dreyfus/skill-acquisition, dreyfus/sedimented-techne.*

5. **Preserve the categorical distinction between automation and expertise in system design and documentation.** Level 3 automation is not expert-level absorbed coping. It is trigger-action rule execution that happens to run without human intervention. The phenomenological distance between these is vast. System documentation, automation level descriptions, and user-facing language should not imply that higher automation levels represent greater expertise. They represent greater autonomy in rule-application, which is a different thing entirely. *Cite: dreyfus/embodiment-requirement, dreyfus/competence-ceiling.*

6. **Exploit domain specificity to maximize sedimented techne's value.** GSD Reflect operates in a relatively constrained, text-mediated, rule-governed domain. The embodiment argument is weakest here. Design choices should exploit this: lean into the system's strengths in pattern detection across discrete artifacts, recurring problem-type recognition, and case-based contextual enrichment. Acknowledge that the system would be far less effective in domains requiring continuous embodied engagement, but recognize that software project management is favorable territory for sedimented techne. *Cite: dreyfus/domain-sensitivity.*

## Citable Principles

- **dreyfus/skill-acquisition**: The progression from novice (context-free rules) through advanced beginner (situational recognition), competent (deliberate planning), proficient (holistic perception), to expert (absorbed coping) describes a trajectory in which the role of rules diminishes and the role of embodied perception increases. The critical transition -- competent to proficient -- requires a qualitative transformation of perception that cannot be achieved by accumulating more rules. Systems that follow rules, however sophisticated, remain at or below the competent stage unless they can bridge this perceptual gap.

- **dreyfus/embodiment-requirement**: Genuine expertise requires embodied being-in-the-world: a body that achieves maximal grip on situations, a background of coping skills that cannot be made explicit, and absorbed engagement that dissolves the subject-object distinction. This is not a contingent feature of human expertise but (per Dreyfus) a constitutive condition. Systems without bodies cannot replicate this mode of being, which limits them to rule-based competence regardless of their computational sophistication.

- **dreyfus/competence-ceiling**: Rule-based systems -- including systems with sophisticated rule-selection mechanisms, hierarchical planning, and context-sensitive rule-application -- are structurally limited to the Competent stage of the skill model. They can follow rules and choose among rules but cannot make the transition to holistic situational perception that defines proficiency. This is not a limitation of current technology but a structural consequence of the rule-following paradigm.

- **dreyfus/sedimented-techne**: (GSD Reflect extension) When a system combines LLM pre-training on vast quantities of situated practice with accumulated project-specific case history, it achieves a mode of operation that exceeds bare rule-following without reaching genuine proficiency. We name this "sedimented techne": rules enriched by accumulated situated cases, producing contextual sensitivity that is more than competent and less than proficient. Sedimented techne is an honest design target for systems that cannot achieve embodied expertise but can accumulate and leverage situated case history.

- **dreyfus/frame-problem**: Rules cannot specify their own context of application. A rule "shared infrastructure should be sequenced" cannot determine what counts as "shared infrastructure" in a novel situation. This is not a deficiency of specific rules but a structural feature of rule-based reasoning. Mitigating the frame problem requires enriching rules with situated cases that illustrate their conditions of applicability, not adding more rules to specify when the first rules apply.

- **dreyfus/domain-sensitivity**: The strength of the embodiment requirement varies with domain formality. In open-ended, richly situated domains (driving, nursing, teaching), embodied perception is most clearly indispensable. In constrained, text-mediated, rule-governed domains (software project management, formal analysis), the gap between competent and proficient is narrower, and sedimented techne may approximate proficient performance more closely. The practical significance of the competence ceiling is domain-dependent.
