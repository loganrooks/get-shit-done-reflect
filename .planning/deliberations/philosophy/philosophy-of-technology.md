# Philosophical Deliberation: French Philosophy of Technology

**Tradition:** French Philosophy of Technology / Technics
**Key thinkers:** Gilbert Simondon (On the Mode of Existence of Technical Objects, 1958; Individuation in Light of Notions of Form and Information, 2005), Bernard Stiegler (Technics and Time, vol. 1-3, 1994-2001; The Age of Disruption, 2016)
**Created:** 2026-03-08
**Status:** Active

## Core Ideas

The French philosophy of technology tradition, centered on Simondon and Stiegler, offers something that Anglophone philosophy of technology largely lacks: a serious ontology of technical objects themselves. Rather than asking "is technology good or bad?" or "how should we regulate technology?", Simondon and Stiegler ask "what ARE technical objects?" and "what is the relationship between technical memory and human thought?" These questions bear directly on the design of a system like GSD Reflect, which is itself a technical object attempting to evolve through self-observation, and which functions as an externalized memory system for workflow experience.

### Simondon: Technical Individuation

#### Technical Individuation

Technical objects are not static artifacts; they evolve. Simondon traces how technical objects move from "abstract" to "concrete" through a process he calls individuation. An abstract technical object has parts designed independently, not mutually adapted -- like early engines with separate cooling, combustion, and lubrication systems, each solving its own problem without regard for the others. A concrete technical object has parts that are mutually adapted into an integrated whole -- like modern engines where the cylinder block simultaneously serves cooling, structural, and combustion functions. This evolution from abstract to concrete is "individuation" -- the object becomes more itself, more internally coherent, through iterated encounters with use.

Individuation is not a metaphor. Simondon means that technical objects have a genuine mode of existence -- a way of being that deserves philosophical attention on its own terms, not merely as a derivative of human purposes. The engine is not just a tool for transportation; it is an evolving system with its own internal logic, its own trajectory of development, its own "problems" that it resolves through successive redesign. Understanding a technical object requires understanding its genesis -- how it arrived at its current form through a history of problem-solving encounters between design and use.

#### The Associated Milieu

A technical object does not evolve in isolation. It requires an "associated milieu" -- the environment that the object both shapes and is shaped by. The Guimbal turbine, Simondon's paradigmatic example, requires the river: the river's flow drives the turbine, and the turbine's structure channels the river's flow. The turbine's oil cooling system uses the surrounding water, making the environment a functional component of the machine itself. Without an associated milieu, the technical object cannot individuate -- it remains abstract, its parts disconnected from the conditions of use.

The associated milieu is NOT the operating environment in general. It is the specific medium through which the object's internal organization and external function are coupled. The milieu is not merely context; it is a constitutive relation. The turbine does not merely sit IN the river; the river-turbine system is a functional unity in which the boundary between object and environment is partly dissolved. This coupling is what allows the object to evolve: problems encountered at the object-milieu interface drive redesign, and redesign alters the interface, generating new problems and new solutions.

#### Concretization

The process of becoming more concrete is "concretization." In a concrete technical object, each component serves multiple functions, creating synergies. The genius of concretization is that it does not just combine parts -- it finds arrangements where the interaction between parts produces new functional properties that no part had alone. The air-cooled engine's fins simultaneously dissipate heat, provide structural rigidity, and increase surface area for heat exchange. No single function dictated the fin design; the design emerged from the convergence of multiple functional requirements.

Concretization cannot be fully planned in advance. It emerges through iterated design-use-redesign cycles because the synergies between components are often discovered, not predicted. A designer may intend a component to serve one function and discover, through use, that it serves others. This discovery feeds back into the next design iteration, and the object becomes more concrete -- more internally coherent, more functionally integrated. The trajectory of concretization is from plurality (many independent parts) toward unity (mutually adapted components forming a functional whole).

#### Technical Culture and Alienation

Simondon argues that modern culture is "alienated" from its technical objects because it treats them either as mere instruments (tools for human purposes, reducible to their utility) or as autonomous threats (technology "out of control," hostile to human values). Both attitudes miss what technical objects ARE: evolving entities with their own mode of existence that deserves understanding, not just use or fear.

The instrumental view reduces the technical object to a means-end relation: the hammer is "for" driving nails. This misses the hammer's technical reality -- its weight distribution, material properties, the way its form has evolved through centuries of use. The autonomy-threat view projects human agency onto technical objects, treating them as quasi-subjects with intentions and powers. This misses the fact that technical objects evolve not through autonomous will but through the coupled system of object, milieu, and human practice.

Understanding technology, for Simondon, requires a "technical culture" -- the capacity to enter into the logic of technical objects, to understand their internal organization, their genesis, and their trajectory of development. Without technical culture, we are either enslaved to our tools (using them without understanding them) or alienated from them (fearing what we do not understand). The philosopher of technology, like the engineer, must be able to think with and through the technical object, not merely about it.

### Stiegler: Technics and Time

#### Tertiary Retention

Building on Husserl's phenomenology of time-consciousness, Stiegler distinguishes three kinds of retention. Primary retention is the present perception as it unfolds -- what I am hearing now in a melody, where each note is retained as the next arrives, giving the melody its continuity. Secondary retention is memory and imagination -- what I remember hearing when I recall the melody later. Tertiary retention is externalized technical memory -- the recording, the text, the database, the score. These are material supports for memory that exist independently of the biological subject.

Stiegler's key insight is that tertiary retention is not merely a supplement to biological memory. It shapes and reorganizes primary and secondary retention. We perceive and remember differently because of our technical memory systems. Writing did not just record what we already knew -- it changed how we think. The alphabet enabled new forms of logical analysis, abstract reasoning, and historical consciousness that were unavailable to purely oral cultures. The database does not just store what we observed -- it reorganizes what we observe and how we observe it.

This is not technological determinism. Stiegler does not claim that technology dictates thought. He claims that technical memory systems are constitutive conditions of thought -- not sufficient causes, but necessary conditions that shape the space of what is thinkable. A culture without writing can think, but it cannot think in the ways that writing enables. A developer without a knowledge base can learn from experience, but they cannot learn in the ways that externalized, searchable, cross-referenced experience enables.

#### Grammatization

Grammatization is the process by which continuous human activities are discretized and externalized into reproducible forms. Speech is a continuous flow of sound; writing grammatizes it into discrete letters, words, sentences. Gesture is a continuous flow of bodily movement; industrial machinery grammatizes it into discrete, repeatable operations. Knowledge is a continuous flow of experience, judgment, and intuition; databases and algorithms grammatize it into discrete entries, categories, and rules.

Each step of grammatization both enables new capabilities and risks a loss. Writing enables long-distance communication, legal contracts, scientific accumulation -- but it also, as Plato warned in the Phaedrus, risks undermining living memory. The student who relies on notes may lose the capacity to remember without them. Industrial machinery enables mass production -- but it also separates the worker from the rhythm and judgment of craft, reducing skilled gesture to mechanical repetition. Knowledge databases enable pattern detection across cases -- but they also risk replacing the felt quality of experience with structured data points that have lost their situational texture.

Grammatization is not inherently good or bad. It is a structural process with structural consequences. The question is not whether to grammatize (this is largely unavoidable in any complex technical system) but how to grammatize in ways that preserve what matters and acknowledge what is lost. Every discretization is a decision about what counts as a unit, and that decision shapes all subsequent operations on those units.

#### Proletarianization

When knowledge or skill is externalized into a machine, the human who previously exercised that skill may lose it. Stiegler uses "proletarianization" in an expanded sense beyond its Marxist origin: not just the industrial worker's loss of craft knowledge to the factory system, but any loss of savoir-faire (know-how) or savoir-vivre (knowledge of how to live) through externalization into technical systems.

The artisan who used judgment is replaced by the factory worker who follows the machine's rhythm. The navigator who read stars and currents is replaced by the GPS user who follows turn-by-turn directions. Stiegler extends this from industrial and navigational labor to cognitive labor: when knowledge systems "think for us" -- when algorithms recommend, curate, decide -- we may lose the capacity to think for ourselves. Not because the algorithms are wrong, but because the exercise of judgment, like any capacity, atrophies when it is not practiced.

This is not technophobia. Stiegler is not arguing against externalization. He is identifying a structural risk inherent in grammatization: every externalization that relieves the human of a cognitive task also removes an occasion for the exercise of the capacity that performed that task. The question is whether the externalization empowers (gives new capabilities, frees attention for higher-order judgment) or proletarianizes (removes existing capacities without replacing them with anything, leaving the human dependent on a system they do not understand).

#### Pharmacology

Stiegler uses the Greek pharmakon (both remedy and poison) to characterize technology. Every technical system is simultaneously curative and toxic. Writing both enables culture and undermines memory (Plato's Phaedrus). The printing press both democratizes knowledge and enables propaganda. Social media both connects communities and fragments attention. A knowledge base both preserves experience and risks replacing the judgment that produced it.

The pharmacological perspective refuses the question "is this technology good or bad?" The answer is always "both." The design question is not how to build a purely curative technology (this is impossible) but how to maximize the curative and minimize the toxic. This requires awareness of the specific toxicities of each technical system -- not generic warnings about "technology" in the abstract, but precise identification of how THIS system, in THIS configuration, for THIS purpose, simultaneously heals and harms.

Pharmacology is not relativism. Stiegler does not claim that all technical configurations are equally curative and toxic. Some designs manage the pharmakon better than others. The question is always about the ratio and the conditions: under what circumstances does this system's curative potential dominate? Under what circumstances does its toxicity dominate? What design choices shift the ratio?

## Relevance to GSD Reflect

GSD Reflect is a technical object in Simondon's sense -- an evolving system moving from abstract (independent components: signal collector, reflector, knowledge base, automation engine) toward concrete (components mutually adapted through use). It is also a tertiary retention system in Stiegler's sense -- an externalized memory that shapes how its user perceives and remembers workflow experience. Both thinkers offer frameworks for understanding the system's current crisis: 124 signals, 0 formal lessons, 0 remediated signals. The pipeline produces prose reports that change nothing.

**Simondon's diagnosis:** The system is still abstract. Its components -- signal detection, reflection, knowledge base, automation -- were designed independently, each solving its own problem without mutual adaptation. Signal detection produces signals that the reflector cannot effectively consume. The reflector produces reports that the knowledge base cannot integrate. The knowledge base stores lessons that automation cannot apply. Each component works in isolation; the system as a whole does not function as an integrated unit. This is the hallmark of the abstract technical object: parts that do not serve multiple functions, that do not create synergies, that do not form a functional whole.

More critically, the system lacks an associated milieu. Self-referential reflection -- signals about its own signals, reflection on its own reflection process -- is reflection without a milieu. The system examining its own artifacts is not encountering anything external. There is no river for this turbine. The associated milieu for GSD Reflect would be the user's actual practice: the projects worked on, the problems encountered, the decisions made under uncertainty. Without coupling to this milieu, the system cannot individuate. It spins in place, producing artifacts (signals, reports) that have no external reference point against which to test and refine themselves.

**Stiegler's diagnosis:** The knowledge base IS tertiary retention -- externalized memory of workflow experience. Signal detection is grammatization: continuous workflow experience is discretized into signal entries with severity, lifecycle state, and confidence. Something is inevitably lost in this discretization -- the felt quality of "this phase was chaotic" becomes a structured data point with fields that may or may not capture what mattered about the chaos. The 124 unprocessed signals are grammatized experience that has been externalized but not integrated back into practice. They sit in the database like unread notes -- technically preserved, practically inert.

The proletarianization risk is real but deferred: if the system were to auto-patch its own behavior based on signals (which it currently cannot do), the user would lose understanding of WHY things work the way they do. They would become dependent on a system they do not fully understand. The current failure -- no lessons, no remediation -- is paradoxically protective against proletarianization. The system fails to externalize judgment because it fails to produce actionable lessons. If it succeeded, the question would become: does the user still understand their own practice, or have they outsourced that understanding to the system?

The pharmacological analysis is the most immediately applicable. The knowledge base is simultaneously remedy (preserves experience across sessions, surfaces patterns the user would miss, provides institutional memory for a single-developer project) and poison (may produce false confidence in formalized knowledge, may replace situated judgment with database lookups, may generate signal fatigue that obscures rather than illuminates). The current state -- 124 signals producing nothing -- is the poison dominating: the system consumes attention and context to produce signals that change nothing, while giving the impression of self-improvement ("look, we're collecting signals!"). The curative potential is latent but unrealized.

**Both thinkers together:** The system is not a static tool but an evolving technical object with its own mode of existence. Its current failure is not a bug to be fixed with better code but a phase in its individuation -- the abstract phase, where components have not yet achieved mutual adaptation. The path forward requires both concretization (making components serve multiple functions, creating synergies between signal detection and lesson production and automation) and milieu-coupling (grounding the system's self-observation in encounters with actual development practice, not just its own artifacts).

## Tensions and Limitations

### 1. Simondon's Physical Machines vs. Software

Simondon's paradigmatic examples are physical machines: engines, turbines, electronic tubes. These objects individuate under material constraints -- thermodynamic limits, mechanical stress, fluid dynamics. Software has fundamentally different constraints: it can be infinitely copied, versioned, rolled back, and forked. There is no material resistance, no physical degradation, no thermodynamic efficiency to optimize.

This matters because concretization in physical machines is driven partly by material constraints: the need to manage heat, stress, and friction forces components into mutual adaptation. Software components can remain abstract indefinitely -- they can be loosely coupled, independently deployed, separately versioned -- without any physical pressure toward integration. The "associated milieu" for software is more diffuse than for a turbine: it includes user practice, codebase state, project type, team dynamics, and the broader ecosystem of tools and conventions. None of these exert the focused, unambiguous pressure that a river exerts on a turbine.

**What survives the displacement:** The insight that technical objects evolve from abstract to concrete through iterated encounters with use is genuine and arguably MORE important for software (which can avoid concretization indefinitely) than for physical machines (which material constraints push toward concretization whether the designer intends it or not). The "associated milieu" concept is harder to apply but not inapplicable: software that is never used in real projects, never tested against real problems, never adjusted to real user needs, remains abstract in exactly Simondon's sense.

**What does not survive:** The specific mechanism of concretization -- components forced into mutual adaptation by material constraints -- does not transfer directly. Software concretization, if it occurs, is driven by design decisions and user feedback, not by physics. This makes it more contingent, more dependent on the designer's judgment, and more likely to stall.

### 2. Stiegler's Civilizational Critique vs. a Single-User Tool

Stiegler's pharmacology addresses SOCIETY -- digital platforms, industrial capitalism, civilizational deskilling. He writes about Facebook, Google, automated trading, and the destruction of savoir-vivre on a global scale. GSD Reflect is one person and a workflow tool. The political-economic critique does not scale down without major loss.

Stiegler's proletarianization thesis depends on a political economy: workers lose skills because capital extracts and externalizes their knowledge for profit. There is no analogous dynamic in a single developer using a self-improvement tool. The developer is both the "worker" and the "owner" of the knowledge externalization. They choose to externalize; they can choose to stop. The power asymmetry that makes industrial proletarianization pernicious is absent.

**What survives the displacement:** The structural insight that externalized memory shapes the externalizer. Even in a single-user context, a developer who relies on a knowledge base for workflow decisions is developing different capacities than one who relies on personal memory and judgment alone. The pharmakon principle -- that every technical intervention is simultaneously curative and toxic -- applies regardless of scale. A knowledge base that replaces judgment is toxic even for a single user, even when that user chose to build it.

**What does not survive:** The civilizational urgency of Stiegler's analysis. He is arguing about the fate of democratic society, the destruction of intergenerational knowledge transmission, the reduction of citizens to consumers. Our application is about whether a PhD student's workflow tool produces actionable lessons. The stakes are incommensurable. Applying Stiegler's vocabulary to GSD Reflect risks inflating modest design questions into civilizational crises, which distorts both the design questions and Stiegler's critique.

### 3. Is Self-Referential Reflection Always Degenerative?

The claim that self-referential reflection lacks a milieu is the strongest Simondonian criticism of the current system. But it may be too strong. CI test results ARE encounters with external reality, not pure self-reference. When the system detects that tests failed, it is detecting something about the code's relationship to its specification, not just about its own signal-detection process. When the system detects that a plan deviated from scope, it is detecting something about the relationship between intention and execution, which involves external factors (requirements changing, unforeseen complexity, time pressure).

The question is whether these encounters with external reality are sufficiently "milieu-like" to drive individuation, or whether they are so mediated by the system's own categories that they amount to self-reference in disguise. A sensor that detects CI failures according to a predefined pattern is not encountering the CI failure directly -- it is encountering a representation of the failure that its own categories have produced. The milieu is always already interpreted.

**Partial resolution:** Self-referential reflection is degenerative when the system's categories are never challenged by what they observe. If signals always confirm the categories (every observation fits neatly into a predefined type and severity), the system is not learning -- it is classifying. Productive self-reflection requires that observations sometimes resist classification, forcing the system to revise its categories. This is the encounter with the milieu: not the observation itself, but the friction between the observation and the existing framework.

### 4. Can Proletarianization Be Prevented by Design?

Stiegler is ambiguous on whether proletarianization is an inherent consequence of grammatization or a contingent consequence of how grammatization is implemented. If every externalization of knowledge removes an occasion for the exercise of that knowledge, then any knowledge base, no matter how well designed, proletarianizes its user to some degree. The developer who consults the KB for signal interpretation is not exercising the judgment that would develop if they interpreted the signal without the KB.

But there is a counter-argument: externalization can also ENABLE new forms of judgment that would not exist without it. A developer who can see patterns across 100 signals (something no human memory could hold) is exercising a NEW kind of judgment that the externalization made possible. The question is not just "what capacity is lost?" but "what capacity is gained?" If the gained capacity is more valuable than the lost one, the externalization is net-curative even if some proletarianization occurs.

**Design implication:** The honest translation for design is: externalize memory (case history, pattern detection across cases -- this is curative), do not externalize judgment (decisions about what to do, automated remediation without user understanding -- this risks proletarianization). The user should be able to choose not to engage with the case history and still develop their own practical sense. The system should make the user's judgment MORE effective, not less necessary.

### 5. Understanding a System That Is Its Own Logic

Simondon's "technical culture" requires entering into the logic of the technical object -- understanding it from the inside, not just consuming its outputs. But GSD Reflect IS its own logic. It is a system for understanding and improving software development, applied to the development of itself. What does it mean for a system to "enter into its own logic"?

This is not quite the same as self-reference. Self-reference is a formal relation (the system refers to itself). Technical culture is an epistemic relation (understanding the object's internal organization, genesis, and trajectory). The system can refer to itself without understanding itself -- it can produce signals about its own process without grasping the significance of those signals. Understanding, for Simondon, requires grasping the object's mode of existence, its trajectory of individuation, its relationship to its milieu. This is precisely what the philosophy deliberations attempt: understanding the system as a technical object, not just operating it.

**Implication:** The philosophy deliberation series is, in Simondonian terms, an exercise in technical culture -- entering into the logic of the system to understand its mode of existence. This understanding cannot be performed by the system itself (the system cannot grasp its own mode of existence from the inside). It requires a perspective that stands both within and outside the system -- which is what the user-as-philosopher provides.

### 6. Pharmacological Maximization: Principled or Contextual?

If every technical intervention is simultaneously curative and toxic, is there a principled way to maximize cure and minimize toxicity, or is it always contextual? Stiegler gestures toward "long circuits" (institutions, education, intergenerational transmission) as curative and "short circuits" (instant gratification, algorithmic optimization, attention capture) as toxic. But this mapping may not transfer to a workflow tool where both "long" and "short" circuits are in play simultaneously.

A principled answer might be: interventions that increase the user's capacity for judgment are curative; interventions that decrease it are toxic. But measuring "capacity for judgment" is itself a judgment call that cannot be automated. The system cannot measure whether its user is becoming more or less capable of practical reasoning. It can only offer features and observe usage patterns, which are weak proxies.

**Practical resolution:** Accept that pharmacological management is always contextual, never algorithmic. Design features with explicit awareness of their dual nature, and give the user the tools to adjust the ratio. A user who finds signals helpful should see more of them; a user who finds signals noisy should see fewer. The system should not decide the pharmacological ratio -- it should make it adjustable.

## Praxis Recommendations

1. **Pursue concretization by designing multi-functional components.** The system's current architecture is abstract in Simondon's sense: signal detection, reflection, KB storage, and automation each solve independent problems. Concretization requires that components serve multiple functions simultaneously. A signal should be structured to inform the user, feed the reflector, and update the KB in a single pass. A lesson should serve both human understanding and machine-actionable remediation. Each step toward multi-functionality is a step from abstract to concrete, and from a pipeline that produces artifacts to a system that functions as an integrated whole. *Cite: simondon/concretization, simondon/individuation.*

2. **Open the system to its associated milieu by coupling reflection to user practice.** Self-referential reflection -- the system observing its own artifacts -- is reflection without milieu. The associated milieu for GSD Reflect is the user's actual development practice: the projects worked on, the problems encountered, the decisions made under pressure. Design mechanisms for the user to annotate signals with practice-level context ("this signal matters because...", "this signal is noise because..."), assess outcomes ("this lesson helped / did not help"), and provide the external reference points that the system cannot generate for itself. Without milieu-coupling, the system cannot individuate -- it will continue producing signals that refer only to other signals. *Cite: simondon/associated-milieu, simondon/individuation.*

3. **Audit grammatization losses in the signal schema.** Signal detection is grammatization: continuous experience is discretized into fields (severity, type, confidence, lifecycle state). Every schema embodies a theory about what matters. Periodically ask: does the signal schema capture what the user actually needs to remember about a problematic phase, or does it force experience into categories that lose the essential texture? If the user's felt experience of "this phase was chaotic and I'm not sure why" cannot be expressed in the schema, the grammatization is losing something important. Consider adding a free-text "interpretation" field alongside structured fields -- preserving both the grammatized form (for machine processing) and the ungrammatized residue (for human understanding). *Cite: stiegler/grammatization.*

4. **Externalize memory, not judgment, to prevent proletarianization.** The knowledge base should make past experience available (curative) without making decisions on the user's behalf (proletarianizing). When the system surfaces a pattern or recommends a change, present the case history -- the signals, their context, the outcomes -- and let the user judge what to do. Auto-remediation that patches behavior without user understanding is the toxicity pole of the pharmakon. The user should always be able to develop their own practical sense independently of the system, and should never find that the system has made decisions they cannot explain. *Cite: stiegler/proletarianization, stiegler/pharmacology.*

5. **Design pharmacologically: for each feature, identify cure and toxicity explicitly.** The knowledge base preserves experience (cure) and may replace judgment (toxicity). Signal detection surfaces patterns (cure) and produces fatigue (toxicity). Automation saves effort (cure) and removes occasions for learning (toxicity). For each feature, document both poles and design to maximize the curative-to-toxic ratio. This is not a one-time analysis but an ongoing practice: as the system evolves, the pharmacological balance shifts. A feature that is curative at low signal volume may become toxic at high volume. *Cite: stiegler/pharmacology.*

6. **Track the system's individuation trajectory explicitly.** The system is evolving from abstract to concrete across milestones. Make this trajectory visible: which components have become mutually adapted? Where do functional synergies exist? Where do components remain independently designed? This tracking serves as an operational model of the system's technical maturity -- not a configuration parameter but a developmental assessment. It also provides the system's designers (including the user) with the vocabulary to distinguish genuine concretization (components achieving mutual adaptation through use) from cosmetic integration (components connected by interfaces but not functionally unified). *Cite: simondon/individuation, simondon/concretization.*

7. **Treat self-referential reflection as productive only when categories are challenged.** Self-observation that always confirms existing categories is not learning -- it is classification. The system's reflection should be designed to surface cases where signals resist the existing schema, where observations do not fit predefined types, where patterns cut across categories. These friction points are the encounters with the milieu that drive individuation. If every signal fits neatly into the schema, the schema is either perfectly adequate (unlikely) or insufficiently sensitive to detect what it cannot categorize (more likely). *Cite: simondon/associated-milieu, simondon/technical-culture.*

8. **Design tertiary retention to enrich, not replace, the user's perception and memory.** The knowledge base shapes how the user perceives their workflow (Stiegler's point about tertiary retention reorganizing primary and secondary retention). Design this influence deliberately: surface relevant past experience at moments of decision, provide cross-session pattern visibility that no human memory could maintain, present historical context for current signals. But also: make it easy for the user to ignore the system and rely on their own memory and judgment. The system's tertiary retention should be a resource the user draws on by choice, not a filter they cannot opt out of. *Cite: stiegler/tertiary-retention.*

## Citable Principles

- **simondon/individuation**: Technical objects evolve from abstract (components designed independently, not mutually adapted) to concrete (components mutually adapted into an integrated whole) through iterated encounters with use. Self-improvement requires engagement with real problems and real practice, not self-referential analysis of internal artifacts. A system whose components remain independently designed -- where signal detection, reflection, and remediation each solve their own problem without functional integration -- has not yet individuated.

- **simondon/associated-milieu**: Evolution requires encounter with an environment the object both shapes and is shaped by. The associated milieu is not the operating context in general but the specific medium through which internal organization and external function are coupled. Reflection without a milieu -- observation that never encounters anything external to the system's own categories -- degenerates. For a workflow system, the associated milieu is the user's actual practice: projects, problems, decisions, outcomes.

- **simondon/concretization**: Mature technical objects have components that serve multiple functions, creating synergies that could not be planned in advance but emerge through iterated design-use-redesign cycles. A system where each component serves exactly one function (signals for detection, reports for reading, lessons for storage) is abstract. Concretization means finding designs where the interaction between components produces functional properties that no component had alone.

- **simondon/technical-culture**: Understanding technology requires entering into its logic -- grasping its internal organization, genesis, and trajectory of development -- not just consuming its outputs or fearing its autonomy. A user who operates a workflow system without understanding its mode of existence is alienated from it. A system that operates on its user's practice without the user understanding how is equally alienated. Technical culture is the condition for productive human-system cooperation.

- **stiegler/tertiary-retention**: Externalized memory (knowledge bases, signal stores, databases) is not neutral storage. It shapes and reorganizes how we perceive and remember. The knowledge base does not just record what happened -- it changes what the user notices next time and how they interpret it. Design externalized memory with awareness of this constitutive role: it is not a passive archive but an active influence on perception and judgment.

- **stiegler/grammatization**: Discretizing continuous experience into formal categories (signals with severity, type, confidence) inevitably loses something. Every schema embodies a theory of what matters, and everything outside the schema becomes invisible to the system. Design should be aware of what is lost in grammatization: the felt quality of experience, the situational texture, the ambiguity that structured fields cannot capture. Preserve space for the ungrammatized alongside the grammatized.

- **stiegler/proletarianization**: Externalizing knowledge into a system risks the user losing the capacity that produced the knowledge. The developer who relies on the KB for interpretation may lose the ability to interpret without it. This is not an argument against externalization -- it is an argument for externalizing memory (case history, pattern detection) rather than judgment (decisions, remediations). The user should be able to develop practical sense independently of the system.

- **stiegler/pharmacology**: Every technical system is simultaneously curative (preserves experience, surfaces patterns, saves effort) and toxic (replaces judgment, produces fatigue, removes occasions for learning). Design cannot eliminate toxicity -- it can only manage the ratio. For each feature, identify both the curative potential and the specific toxicity, and design to maximize the former while making the latter visible and adjustable. A system that claims to be purely beneficial is hiding its toxicity, not eliminating it.
