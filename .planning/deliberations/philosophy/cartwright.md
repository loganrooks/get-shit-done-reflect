# Philosophical Deliberation: Cartwright's Philosophy of Scientific Models

**Tradition:** Philosophy of Science / Anti-Fundamentalism
**Key thinkers:** Nancy Cartwright (How the Laws of Physics Lie, 1983; The Dappled World, 1999; Nature's Capacities and Their Measurement, 1989)
**Created:** 2026-03-08
**Status:** Active

## Core Ideas

Cartwright's philosophy of science challenges the assumption that the world is governed by universal, fundamental laws from which all phenomena can, in principle, be derived. Her central contention -- developed across three major books spanning sixteen years -- is that the "fundamental laws" of physics do not describe the real world. They describe *idealized models*: carefully constructed, deliberately simplified, shielded-from-interference arrangements that work under controlled conditions. Outside those conditions, the laws do not hold. This is not a claim about human ignorance (we just haven't found the right fundamental law yet); it is a claim about the world. The world is *dappled* -- governed not by a few universal laws but by a patchwork of local models, each with its own domain of application. Applied to GSD Reflect, Cartwright's framework provides rigorous analytic grounding for treating lessons as local capacity claims rather than universal laws, and for requiring explicit scope conditions on every piece of practical knowledge the system produces.

### How the Laws of Physics Lie

Cartwright's first and most provocative thesis (developed in *How the Laws of Physics Lie*) is that the fundamental laws of physics -- the equations that physicists prize as the deepest truths about nature -- are not literally true of the world. Coulomb's law accurately describes the force between two point charges in a vacuum with no other forces present. But real charges exist in materials, under gravitational influence, surrounded by other charges, buffeted by thermal fluctuations. The actual force between two real charges does not follow Coulomb's law. It follows a messy composite of many interacting factors that cannot be derived from Coulomb's law alone.

The standard response is that Coulomb's law describes a *component* of the total force -- the electrostatic contribution. Other laws describe other components (gravitational, magnetic, etc.), and the total force is the vector sum of all components. Cartwright's reply is that this "component force" interpretation makes the law unfalsifiable and explanatorily inert. We never observe component forces in isolation. The "component" is a theoretical posit introduced precisely to save the universality of the law. What we observe are *resultant behaviors* -- the actual trajectory of the actual charge in its actual environment. And these resultant behaviors are described not by fundamental laws but by specific models built for specific situations.

The key claim is about the direction of explanation: it is not that fundamental laws explain phenomena and models are convenient approximations. Rather, models do the explanatory work, and fundamental laws are *ingredients* in models -- tools used in model construction, not descriptions of reality. Cartwright does not deny that Coulomb's law is useful. She denies that it is *true* -- literally, of the real world. It is true of the model. The model, when carefully constructed to match a specific situation, is what explains the phenomenon.

### The Dappled World

In *The Dappled World*, Cartwright develops the ontological implications of her earlier work. If fundamental laws do not describe the real world, what does? Her answer: the world is a patchwork -- a "dappled" mosaic -- of local orders. Different regions of nature are governed by different models, each with its own domain of validity. There is no "theory of everything" that unifies them, not because we have not found it yet, but because the world is not the kind of thing that admits a unified theory.

This is a strong metaphysical thesis, and Cartwright is careful to distinguish it from mere epistemic humility. She is not saying "the world might be unified but we cannot know." She is saying: the evidence we have supports a dappled metaphysics over a unified one. The success of physics is best explained not by the truth of fundamental laws but by the carefully controlled conditions of laboratories -- *nomological machines* that produce regular behavior by shielding systems from the interference that makes the real world dappled.

The dappled world thesis has an important corollary: *generalization is not free*. Taking a result from one domain and applying it to another is not a deduction from universal laws; it is an empirical bet that the conditions of the new domain are sufficiently similar to the old one. Sometimes the bet pays off; sometimes it does not. The burden of proof is on the generalizer, not on the skeptic. If you claim that a principle that worked in domain A will work in domain B, you need positive evidence that the relevant conditions are shared, not just an absence of evidence to the contrary.

### Capacities

In *Nature's Capacities and Their Measurement*, Cartwright introduces "capacities" as a more honest ontological category than "laws." Aspirin has the *capacity* to relieve headaches. This capacity is real -- it is grounded in aspirin's chemical properties. But the capacity does not manifest uniformly. Whether aspirin relieves *this* headache depends on many contextual factors: the cause of the headache, the patient's metabolism, interactions with other medications, the dosage, the timing. The capacity is a *tendency* that manifests differently in different contexts, not a *law* that produces uniform effects everywhere.

Capacities are the ontological ground floor for Cartwright. Laws (universal regularities) are abstractions from capacities. They hold when a nomological machine is set up to isolate the capacity from interfering factors. Outside the machine, capacities interact, interfere, enhance, and cancel each other in ways that no single law captures. The world is governed by capacities, not laws -- and capacities are irreducibly context-dependent.

The capacity framework has an important epistemological implication: *measuring* a capacity requires controlling for interfering factors (setting up a nomological machine). If you want to know whether aspirin relieves headaches, you run a controlled trial that isolates aspirin's effect from confounders. If you want to know whether a workflow lesson improves development outcomes, you need analogous controls -- and in software development, such controls are rarely available.

### Nomological Machines

A "nomological machine" is Cartwright's term for a fixed arrangement of components, shielded from outside interference, that produces regular behavior. The laboratory is the paradigmatic nomological machine: by controlling temperature, pressure, materials, and measurement instruments, the physicist creates conditions under which Coulomb's law holds. The regularity is real -- within the machine. Outside the machine, the world is too messy, too interactive, too dappled for the law to hold.

Nomological machines are not limited to laboratories. A factory assembly line is a nomological machine (the arrangement produces regular output when shielded from interference). A legal system is a nomological machine (the rules produce regular outcomes when the institutional framework holds). A software project with a specific CI pipeline, branching strategy, testing framework, and team composition is a nomological machine.

The critical insight is that the *regularity depends on the machine*. Change the components, remove the shielding, introduce new interference -- and the regularity breaks down. A lesson learned in one nomological machine (one project with one CI pipeline) does not necessarily hold in a different machine (a different project with a different setup). The lesson describes a capacity that manifested under specific conditions. Whether it manifests in other conditions is an empirical question, not a deductive certainty.

## Relevance to GSD Reflect

GSD Reflect's lesson system treats lessons as if they were laws: universal principles that hold across all projects and all conditions. "CI must be a blocking gate." "Plans that modify shared infrastructure should be sequenced." "Every fix commit must reference the signal it addresses." These are stated as unconditional imperatives -- do X, always. Cartwright's framework reveals them as capacity claims: they describe tendencies that manifested under specific conditions and may or may not manifest under different conditions.

**Lessons as capacity claims, not laws.** A lesson like "CI must be a blocking gate" is, in Cartwright's terms, a claim about a capacity: CI pipelines have the capacity to prevent broken code from reaching production. This capacity is real -- it is grounded in how CI systems work. But whether it manifests depends on conditions: Does the project have meaningful tests? Is the CI environment stable? Does the team push to main or use feature branches? Are there time pressures that make blocking gates counterproductive? The lesson, stated as a universal law, is true of the *nomological machine* in which it was learned (a specific project where CI failures indicated real code defects and blocking them prevented production incidents). It is not necessarily true of a different project where CI failures are predominantly environment misconfigurations and blocking them prevents all progress.

This is not an argument against lessons. Capacity claims are valuable -- knowing that CI has the capacity to prevent broken code is important even if the capacity does not manifest uniformly. But the claim should be stated as a capacity with scope conditions, not as a law without them. "CI should block merges when: (a) the test suite is maintained and tests real functionality, (b) CI failures are predominantly caused by code defects rather than environment issues, (c) the blocking does not create a bottleneck that incentivizes bypassing." This scoped version is more honest, more useful, and more likely to be applied correctly in new contexts.

**Scope conditions as a design requirement.** The current lesson format does not require scope conditions. A lesson is a statement of what to do, derived from a pattern of signals. Cartwright's framework says this is insufficient. Every lesson is an abstraction from a specific nomological machine -- the project configuration, team composition, development phase, and tooling state that produced the signals from which the lesson was derived. Without explicit scope conditions, the lesson is an unsupported generalization: it claims to hold universally when it was learned locally.

The design implication is concrete: lessons should carry structured scope conditions -- the project characteristics under which the lesson was learned and is expected to hold. When the system surfaces a lesson for a new project, it should check whether the current project matches the scope conditions. If it does not match, the lesson should be presented with reduced confidence or a warning that conditions differ. This is not overcaution; it is epistemic honesty about the domain of validity of local knowledge.

**The dappled world of software development.** Is software development "dappled" in Cartwright's sense? Are there really different local orders that resist unification, or is there a set of universal best practices (version control, testing, CI, code review) that hold everywhere? The answer is probably both: certain extremely general capacities (version control prevents information loss) hold across almost all conditions, while more specific claims (how to structure tests, when to enforce CI, how to manage dependencies) are genuinely context-dependent. The system should distinguish between these levels -- near-universal capacities vs. context-dependent ones -- and handle them differently.

**Projects as nomological machines.** Each project constitutes a specific arrangement of components (language, framework, team, CI pipeline, branching strategy, release cadence, dependency management) that produces regular behavior under controlled conditions. Lessons learned in this machine describe the regularities of *this* machine. Transferring them to another machine requires checking whether the relevant components and shielding conditions are shared. A lesson about CI blocking gates learned in a monorepo with 2,000 tests and a stable CI environment does not automatically transfer to a microservice with 20 tests and a flaky CI runner. The components are different; the machine is different; the regularity may not hold.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1: "CI Must Be a Blocking Gate" as a Capacity Claim

The signal `sig-2026-03-02-ci-failures-ignored-throughout-v116` records that CI failed 5 consecutive times across 23 days during v1.16, and these failures were bypassed via admin merge. The natural lesson: "CI must be a blocking gate -- failures should prevent merges."

Stated as a universal law, this is plausible. Stated as a capacity claim with scope conditions, it becomes more honest:

**Capacity:** CI pipelines have the capacity to prevent broken code from reaching the default branch by blocking merges when tests fail.

**Scope conditions under which this capacity is productive:**
- The test suite tests actual functionality (not just boilerplate or environment setup)
- CI failures are predominantly caused by code defects, not environment instability
- The team has the ability to fix CI failures promptly (not blocked by infrastructure constraints)
- Blocking does not create a merge bottleneck that incentivizes workarounds (force-merging, disabling tests)

**Interfering capacities:**
- Flaky tests have the capacity to create false-positive blocks, incentivizing CI bypass
- Strict blocking in a solo-developer project may create ceremony without safety gain
- Environment-dependent tests have the capacity to fail for reasons unrelated to code quality

**The v1.16 case:** The CI failures during v1.16 were bypassed because the test environment was misconfigured. The developer judged (possibly correctly) that the failures did not indicate code defects. A universal "block on CI failure" rule would have prevented *all* progress for 23 days until the environment was fixed. The scoped version would say: "CI blocking is productive when failures indicate code defects. These failures indicated environment misconfigurations. The capacity to prevent broken code was not the relevant capacity here. The relevant capacity was CI's capacity to signal environment degradation -- a different capacity requiring a different response (fix the environment, not block merges)."

Cartwright would note that this is exactly the difference between a law and a capacity. A law says: "if CI fails, block." A capacity says: "CI failure has the capacity to indicate problems, but what kind of problem determines what kind of response." The law is simple and universal; the capacity is nuanced and context-dependent. The world of software development is dappled: different failures require different responses, and no single rule covers all cases.

### Example 2: Cross-Project Lesson Transfer as an Empirical Bet

Suppose GSD Reflect learns a lesson in Project A (a TypeScript monorepo with 2,000 tests and a 3-person team): "Fix commits that do not reference their originating signal indicate an incomplete feedback loop." This lesson is derived from a pattern where fixes were applied without tracing them back to signals, leading to recurrence of the same problems.

Now the system is installed on Project B (a Python CLI tool, solo developer, 40 tests). Should the lesson be surfaced?

Cartwright's framework says: transferring the lesson from A to B is an empirical bet, not a deduction. The lesson describes a regularity that held in the nomological machine of Project A. Project B is a different machine. The bet is that the relevant components are shared -- specifically, that both projects have a feedback loop where fix-signal traceability prevents recurrence. But the conditions differ: a solo developer may hold the feedback loop in memory (no need for formal traceability), a small project may have few enough signals that recurrence is immediately noticed, and the overhead of referencing signals in every fix commit may exceed its benefit in a 40-test CLI tool.

The system should not silently apply the lesson as if it were a law. It should present it as a capacity claim with a condition mismatch: "This lesson was learned in a monorepo with a 3-person team and 2,000 tests. Current project is a solo CLI tool with 40 tests. The conditions that made fix-signal traceability valuable (multiple developers, many tests, signals too numerous to track mentally) may not hold here. Apply with reduced confidence."

This is not overcaution. It is what Cartwright's framework demands: generalization is an empirical claim, not a logical entailment. The burden of proof is on the claim that conditions match, not on the skeptic who doubts the transfer.

## Tensions and Limitations

### 1. How Specific Do Scope Conditions Need to Be?

This is the practical tension that will arise immediately in implementation. Over-specifying scope conditions makes lessons inapplicable: if a lesson requires matching on CI provider, test framework, language version, team size, repository structure, branching strategy, and release cadence, it will rarely match any new project exactly. Under-specifying makes them vacuously universal -- no better than the unscoped lessons the system currently produces.

Cartwright's own practice suggests a middle path: specify the conditions that are *causally relevant* to the capacity's manifestation, not all conditions that happened to be present. "CI blocking is productive when tests are reliable" is a causally relevant condition. "CI blocking was learned in a TypeScript project" is incidental (the language is not causally relevant to whether blocking prevents defects). But determining which conditions are causally relevant requires the kind of judgment -- Aristotle's phronesis -- that resists full formalization.

**Mitigation:** Start with broad scope conditions (project type, team size, CI configuration) and refine based on experience. Track cases where a lesson was surfaced and found inapplicable, and use these cases to identify the conditions that should have been specified. Scope conditions are not static metadata; they develop through the system's experience of application and misapplication.

### 2. Is Software Engineering Really Dappled?

Some patterns in software engineering appear remarkably universal: version control is better than no version control, testing catches bugs, code review catches oversights, CI provides fast feedback. If these are genuine universal laws (not just near-universal capacities), then Cartwright's dappled-world thesis is too strong for this domain. Perhaps software engineering has a "fundamental physics" of universally valid principles plus a "dappled" layer of context-dependent practices.

Cartwright might respond that even these apparently universal patterns are capacity claims that fail under certain conditions: version control is counterproductive for ephemeral one-off scripts; testing catches bugs only when tests are written to test the right things; code review adds latency that may not be worth it for trivial changes; CI provides fast feedback only when the pipeline is fast. The universality is approximate, not absolute -- which is Cartwright's point about physics as well.

**Mitigation:** Distinguish between levels of generality in lessons. Some lessons are near-universal capacities (version control prevents information loss); others are highly context-dependent (how to structure feature branches). The system should handle these differently -- near-universal capacities need minimal scope conditions; context-dependent lessons need extensive ones. The classification itself is a judgment call, but making it explicit is better than treating all lessons as equally universal or equally local.

### 3. Normative vs. Descriptive Application

Cartwright's framework is about the *descriptive* adequacy of scientific laws: do they accurately describe the world? Lessons in GSD Reflect are *normative*: they prescribe how development should be done. Applying a descriptive framework (capacities, scope conditions) to normative claims (best practices, workflow rules) requires a translation.

The translation is: normative claims have descriptive presuppositions. "CI should block merges" presupposes the descriptive claim "CI blocking prevents broken code." If the descriptive presupposition fails (CI blocking does NOT prevent broken code in this context because failures are environment-related), the normative claim loses its force. Cartwright's framework applies to the descriptive presupposition, which in turn affects the normative claim.

But normative claims also have value presuppositions: "preventing broken code is important," "merge speed matters less than code quality." These are not capacity claims and are not addressed by Cartwright's framework. A lesson might have correct scope conditions (the capacity does manifest here) but wrong value presuppositions (preventing broken code is less important than shipping quickly in this context). Cartwright cannot help with this dimension.

**Mitigation:** Apply Cartwright's framework to the descriptive presuppositions of lessons, not to their normative content directly. Scope conditions specify when the capacity manifests. Whether the capacity *should* be exercised (the normative question) depends on values that are outside Cartwright's framework. The system should present both: "this capacity manifests under these conditions" (Cartwright) and "exercising this capacity serves these values" (a separate question).

### 4. Cross-Project Knowledge Transfer Under Dappled-World Assumptions

If every project is a different nomological machine, and lessons hold only within their originating machine, then cross-project knowledge transfer becomes difficult to justify. The whole point of accumulating lessons is to reuse them across projects. Cartwright's framework suggests this reuse is epistemically risky -- an empirical bet that conditions match, not a deductive certainty.

This tension is real but manageable. Cartwright does not deny that capacities transfer -- aspirin's capacity to relieve headaches is real across many patients. She insists that the *manifestation* of the capacity depends on conditions. Similarly, the capacity of CI to catch defects is real across many projects. Whether it manifests productively depends on test quality, CI stability, and team practices. Cross-project transfer is legitimate when condition-matching is performed; it is illegitimate when lessons are applied blindly as universal laws.

**Mitigation:** Frame cross-project knowledge transfer as *capacity transfer* with explicit condition-matching, not as *law application*. The system transfers the capacity claim ("CI blocking can prevent broken code") along with its scope conditions ("when tests are reliable, failures indicate code defects, and blocking does not create bottlenecks"). The receiving project checks conditions. If conditions match, the lesson applies with high confidence. If conditions partially match, the lesson applies with reduced confidence and a warning. If conditions do not match, the lesson is archived but not surfaced.

## Praxis Recommendations

1. **Add structured scope conditions to the lesson schema.** Every lesson generated by the system should carry explicit scope conditions: the project characteristics under which the lesson was learned and is expected to hold. At minimum: project type (solo/team, monorepo/single-package), CI/CD configuration (present/absent, blocking/advisory), test maturity (high/medium/low coverage, reliable/flaky), development phase (early/mature). Scope conditions are metadata, not optional commentary. *Cite: cartwright/scope-conditions, cartwright/nomological-machine.*

2. **Present lessons as capacity claims, not as unconditional directives.** Reframe lesson language from "always do X" to "X tends to produce Y under conditions A, B, C." This is not hedging -- it is epistemic honesty about the domain of validity of local knowledge. The system should present the capacity, the scope conditions, and the known interfering factors. The developer decides whether the current situation matches. *Cite: cartwright/capacities-not-laws, cartwright/idealization-risk.*

3. **Implement condition-matching for cross-project lesson surfacing.** When the system surfaces a lesson for a new project, compare the current project's characteristics to the lesson's scope conditions. If conditions match, surface with full confidence. If conditions partially match, surface with a condition-mismatch warning. If conditions do not match, do not surface. This prevents the system from applying lessons as universal laws when they are local capacity claims. *Cite: cartwright/local-models, cartwright/scope-conditions.*

4. **Track lesson application outcomes to refine scope conditions.** When a lesson is surfaced and applied, track the outcome: did the capacity manifest as expected? If not, why? Use these outcomes to refine scope conditions over time. A lesson whose scope conditions consistently fail to predict successful application needs broader conditions; a lesson that fails in unexpected contexts needs additional conditions. Scope conditions are empirical claims, testable by application. *Cite: cartwright/capacities-not-laws, cartwright/nomological-machine.*

5. **Distinguish near-universal capacities from context-dependent ones.** Some lessons are nearly universal (version control prevents information loss); others are highly context-dependent (how to structure CI gates). Classify lessons by generality level and handle them differently. Near-universal capacities need minimal scope conditions and can be surfaced broadly. Context-dependent capacities need extensive scope conditions and should be surfaced only when conditions match. *Cite: cartwright/dappled-world, cartwright/local-models.*

6. **Record the nomological machine that produced each lesson.** When a lesson is generated, record the key characteristics of the project configuration (the "machine") that produced the signals from which the lesson was derived. This provenance metadata enables condition-matching and prevents lessons from being treated as context-free universals. The machine is part of the lesson; abstracting it away creates an idealization that may not fit any real project. *Cite: cartwright/nomological-machine, cartwright/idealization-risk.*

7. **Model interactions between lessons.** Lessons from different domains (CI, scope management, dependency hygiene) may interact in non-linear ways. Enforcing strict CI gates (one lesson) while allowing experimental branches (another lesson) creates a tension that neither lesson addresses individually. When surfacing multiple lessons, check for known interactions and flag potential conflicts. Cartwright's "component forces" critique warns against assuming that independently valid capacities combine cleanly. *Cite: cartwright/capacities-not-laws, cartwright/dappled-world.*

## Citable Principles

- **cartwright/capacities-not-laws**: Practical knowledge describes capacities (context-dependent tendencies), not universal laws. A lesson like "CI should block merges" describes a capacity that manifests under specific conditions -- reliable tests, code-defect-driven failures, teams that can fix failures promptly. Outside those conditions, the capacity may not manifest, or may manifest counterproductively. Stating lessons as capacities with conditions is more honest and more useful than stating them as unconditional laws.

- **cartwright/scope-conditions**: Every lesson requires explicit specification of the conditions under which it holds. A lesson without scope conditions is an unsupported generalization -- an idealization abstracted from its originating context that claims universal validity it has not earned. Scope conditions include project type, CI configuration, team composition, test maturity, and development phase. They are metadata, not optional commentary.

- **cartwright/dappled-world**: Different projects have genuinely different dynamics. A monorepo with 50 contributors operates under different principles than a solo CLI tool. There is no universal set of workflow laws that governs all development. Some capacities are near-universal; others are highly context-dependent. The system should distinguish between these and handle them differently, rather than treating all lessons as equally universal.

- **cartwright/nomological-machine**: The project conditions under which a lesson was learned constitute its domain of validity. The specific configuration of CI pipeline, test framework, team size, branching strategy, and release cadence forms a "machine" that produces regular behavior. Outside this machine -- in a project with different components and different shielding conditions -- the regularity may not hold. Lessons carry their machines with them; abstracting the machine away creates an idealization.

- **cartwright/local-models**: Knowledge is organized as local models with bounded applicability, not as a unified theory of software development. Cross-project knowledge transfer requires checking whether the conditions of the new project match the conditions under which the knowledge was produced. Transfer is an empirical bet, not a deductive certainty. The burden of proof is on the claim that conditions match.

- **cartwright/idealization-risk**: Abstracting a lesson from its originating context creates an idealization that may not describe any real project. Every lesson is abstracted from a specific nomological machine; the abstraction removes conditions that may have been causally relevant. The risk is not that idealizations are useless (they are essential for knowledge transfer) but that they are mistaken for literal descriptions of reality. Lessons should carry their ceteris paribus conditions explicitly.
