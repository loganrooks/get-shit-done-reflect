# Requirements Review: v1.20 GSD Reflect

**Reviewer:** GPT 5.4 xhigh
**Date:** 2026-04-08
**Documents reviewed:** REQUIREMENTS.md, MILESTONE-CONTEXT.md, SUMMARY.md

## Executive Summary

The v1.20 requirements are unusually well-motivated at the individual requirement level: most items cite concrete failures, signals, or research gaps rather than aspirational feature ideas. The main weakness is not lack of evidence but lack of shaping: too many items are written as unconditional v1.20 requirements even where SUMMARY.md treats them as differentiators, gated work, or likely deferrals.

Micro-traceability is strong, but macro-traceability is weak. Each requirement has motivation, yet the milestone still has 46/46 requirements unmapped to phases, several cross-category dependencies are implicit rather than explicit, and the specification is missing companion requirements for migration, runtime prerequisites, and verification of the new infrastructure.

## Category-by-Category Review

### KB Infrastructure

**Completeness:** Strong on schema, indexing, and CLI capabilities. Incomplete on migration, backward compatibility, and runtime prerequisites. The research summary explicitly raises migration of existing signal files and `source` field ambiguity, but no KB requirement covers that work.

**Specificity:** Mixed. `KB-01`, `KB-02`, `KB-03`, and `KB-07` are requirement-shaped. `KB-04`, `KB-05`, `KB-06`, and `KB-08` bundle multiple concerns and leave "done" ambiguous, especially around atomicity, query surface, and agent integration boundaries.

**Traceability:** Mostly strong. The weaker item is `KB-08`: its motivation is architectural preference, not a sharply stated failure mode. The Traceability section in REQUIREMENTS.md also leaves KB work unmapped to phases, so end-to-end traceability is still absent.

**Dependencies:** Underspecified. `KB-07` depends on `KB-01`, `KB-05`, and `KB-06`. `KB-08` depends on `KB-04` and a stable query interface. `SPIKE-07` depends on `KB-03`. None of those dependencies are encoded in the requirement text.

**Feasibility:** Reasonable for the index itself, less so for safe rollout. `KB-05` assumes reliable dual-write semantics without specifying partial-failure recovery. The summary also identifies Node `>=22.5.0` as a hidden prerequisite for `node:sqlite`, but no KB requirement captures that.

**Scope appropriateness:** Overcommitted. `KB-06` makes the full `query/stats/health/transition/link/search/rebuild` surface mandatory even though SUMMARY.md treats some of those capabilities as differentiators rather than table stakes.

**Design constraint compliance:** Mostly aligned with "files remain source of truth" and "don't merge signal and issue concepts prematurely." The risk is `KB-02`: disposition begins to pre-shape the future signal/issue/opportunity ontology before that ontology is designed.

**Over-formalization risk:** Moderate. A richer lifecycle is useful, but `KB-01` can still encode a closure bias if long-lived diagnostic signals are expected to progress toward remediation by default.

**Cross-requirement tensions:** `KB-08` hardcodes SQLite query usage in agents before the open CLI-vs-MCP question is fully settled. `KB-01`'s remediation-oriented lifecycle also sits uneasily beside the audit finding that some signals should remain live diagnostic instruments rather than closure candidates.

**Issues found:**
- `KB-06` collapses MVP and follow-on scope into one mandatory requirement, which conflicts with SUMMARY.md's table-stakes vs differentiator split.
- `KB-08` makes local SQLite access the agent retrieval mechanism before the cross-machine access question is resolved.
- No KB requirement covers migration/backfill for existing signal files, `source` field normalization, or safe rollout of schema changes.
- `KB-05` states a dual-write invariant but does not define failure semantics, repair semantics, or the acceptance test that proves the invariant.

### Measurement & Telemetry

**Completeness:** The telemetry category covers extraction, baseline capture, validation, and enrichment. It does not cover prediction capture, intervention attribution mechanics, or what happens if token validation fails and session-meta cannot be trusted as a primary source.

**Specificity:** `TEL-02` is clear. `TEL-01` is acceptable but output contracts are unspecified. `TEL-03` lacks pass criteria, sample size, and fallback behavior. `TEL-04` says the join should exist, but not what minimum coverage or data quality is required.

**Traceability:** Strong. The motivations are concrete and unusually good for infrastructure requirements.

**Dependencies:** This is the most important missing dependency chain in the whole document. `TEL-02` depends on `TEL-01` and `TEL-03`, and in practice it blocks large parts of `GATE-*`, `SENS-*`, and `SPIKE-*`. That blocking relationship is described in SUMMARY.md but not formalized in REQUIREMENTS.md.

**Feasibility:** Extraction is feasible; trustworthy interpretation is less certain. `TEL-03` may materially delay the milestone if token counts are unreliable. `TEL-04` adds analysis complexity before the predictive value of the metrics is known.

**Scope appropriateness:** Mostly appropriate, but `TEL-04` may be too far downstream for a baseline-first milestone. Raw join capability is in scope; requiring enrichment value from it may be premature.

**Design constraint compliance:** Good on avoiding pricing assumptions. Weaker on multi-dimensional predictions: MILESTONE-CONTEXT.md says predictions should be functional, structural, interactional, temporal, and risk-oriented, but no telemetry requirement captures that intervention-prediction contract.

**Over-formalization risk:** Low to moderate. Baseline discipline is warranted, but if the requirement is interpreted as "no other work may proceed until telemetry is perfect," it can freeze the milestone.

**Cross-requirement tensions:** `TEL-02` says baseline must exist before structural interventions ship, while `GATE-01`, `GATE-02`, and `GATE-03` are framed as urgent recurrent fixes. That sequencing tension needs explicit governance.

**Issues found:**
- `TEL-03` is not fully testable because it does not define how many sessions to compare or what discrepancy threshold counts as "validated."
- `TEL-02` is a milestone-wide gate in practice, but the rest of the spec does not treat it as one.
- `TEL-04` requires enrichment before the spec defines which metrics are actually predictive or how to interpret the joined data.
- No telemetry requirement captures the "every intervention should come with predictions" principle from MILESTONE-CONTEXT.md.

### Structural Enforcement

**Completeness:** Good coverage of the recurrent audit failures. Missing explicit override policy, CI failure fallback behavior, and a sharper boundary between structural gating and deferred automation.

**Specificity:** Uneven. `GATE-02`, `GATE-04`, `GATE-05`, and `GATE-08` are concrete. `GATE-01` does not define "CI verification." `GATE-03` does not define how runtime code is detected. `GATE-07` is not yet requirement-shaped because its triggering conditions are heuristic labels rather than measurable thresholds.

**Traceability:** Strong overall. These are among the best-motivated requirements in the set.

**Dependencies:** `GATE-06` and `GATE-07` depend on `XRT-01`. `GATE-01` depends on reliable PR/CI state detection. `GATE-03` depends on a file-classification rule that the spec does not currently define.

**Feasibility:** Mostly feasible. The highest-risk item is `GATE-07`, which requires reliable session-level heuristics and could become noisy or gameable. `GATE-06` is also more complex than it reads because Codex lacks hooks.

**Scope appropriateness:** `GATE-01` through `GATE-06` are appropriate v1.20 infrastructure corrections. `GATE-07` may be too ambitious for this milestone unless it is narrowed. It looks closer to partially automated incident detection, which the spec otherwise treats cautiously.

**Design constraint compliance:** Strong on "advisory text is not enough." Weaker on cross-runtime behavior because the degradation path is outsourced to `XRT-01` instead of being embedded here.

**Over-formalization risk:** Moderate. The structural gates are intentional, but `GATE-07` risks producing low-quality compulsory self-signals, and `GATE-06` can become rote checklist behavior if not carefully placed.

**Cross-requirement tensions:** `GATE-01` requires PR creation and CI before phase advancement, while `GATE-03` allows docs-only quick tasks direct to main. The spec does not say whether those docs-only quick tasks are exempt only from branch/PR flow during execution or also from advancement gating afterward.

**Issues found:**
- `GATE-01` needs a precise definition of "CI verification" and an explicit policy for unavailable or flaky CI.
- `GATE-03` needs an objective runtime-code classification rule or it will be inconsistently enforced.
- `GATE-07` is under-specified and edges toward the deferred automation agenda rather than a bounded v1.20 correction.
- `GATE-06` and `GATE-07` rely on cross-runtime degradation behavior that is not referenced from within the requirements themselves.

### Sensor Pipeline

**Completeness:** Good on log and patch sensors. Incomplete on calibration, fallback behavior, and compatibility management for private runtime internals.

**Specificity:** Better than average. `SENS-02`, `SENS-03`, `SENS-04`, and `SENS-05` are concrete. `SENS-01` describes a process but not acceptance criteria. `SENS-06` is concrete but overlaps with cross-runtime parity requirements.

**Traceability:** Strong. The motivations clearly trace to audit findings and known drift problems.

**Dependencies:** `SENS-01` depends on stable collect-signals integration and likely on KB lifecycle support. `SENS-03` depends on Codex `state_5.sqlite` remaining queryable in the expected shape. `SENS-06` overlaps materially with `XRT-03`, and `SENS-04` overlaps with `XRT-02`.

**Feasibility:** Moderate. The patch sensor is tractable. The Codex adapter is riskier because it depends on runtime internals that GSDR does not control. Without capability checks, this can become fragile across Codex versions.

**Scope appropriateness:** Mostly appropriate, but the spec overstates certainty on Codex internals. `SENS-03` should be framed with version-checked capability detection or fallback behavior rather than hard dependence.

**Design constraint compliance:** Mixed. The category is aligned with cross-runtime parity goals, but it does not explicitly guard against hardcoded machine paths or unstable runtime-specific assumptions.

**Over-formalization risk:** Low to moderate. The bigger risk is brittleness, not bureaucracy. The taxonomy in `SENS-05` could still become too rigid if categories are treated as closed too early.

**Cross-requirement tensions:** `SENS-06` and `XRT-03` are effectively the same requirement from two angles. Keeping both as mandatory risks double-counting one deliverable.

**Issues found:**
- `SENS-03` relies on private Codex runtime state without a companion requirement for version/capability detection.
- `SENS-06` duplicates `XRT-03`, and `SENS-04` partially duplicates `XRT-02`.
- `SENS-01` defines a processing pattern but not the observable success criteria for the sensor.
- No sensor requirement defines fallback behavior when only partial runtime data is available.

### Spike Methodology

**Completeness:** Richest category in the document. Also the most vulnerable to scope creep. The category covers design review, evidence interpretation, outcome types, auxiliary assumptions, metric limitations, sample design, qualification, adherence, findings review, programme infrastructure, and philosophical framing. What it does not cover is how cross-model review is dispatched when the preferred second model is unavailable.

**Specificity:** Mixed. `SPIKE-01` through `SPIKE-09` are mostly requirement-shaped, though some are still broad. `SPIKE-10` and especially `SPIKE-11` are too large and too interpretive to function as clean v1.20 requirements.

**Traceability:** Strong. The research base is credible and repeatedly cited. The problem is not lack of grounding; it is translation from theory to requirement shape.

**Dependencies:** `SPIKE-07` depends on `KB-03`. `SPIKE-09` depends on `SPIKE-01`, `SPIKE-02`, and `SPIKE-08`. `SPIKE-10` depends on a scope decision the documents do not resolve. `SPIKE-11` depends on methodological design work that MILESTONE-CONTEXT.md still treats as an open question.

**Feasibility:** Feasible in parts, risky in aggregate. `SPIKE-01`, `SPIKE-02`, and `SPIKE-03` look like solid v1.20 targets. `SPIKE-09` is more conditional. `SPIKE-10` and `SPIKE-11` are likely harder than they appear because they require stable workflow architecture, not just template edits.

**Scope appropriateness:** This category is the clearest case of mandatory-scope inflation. SUMMARY.md explicitly treats spike programme infrastructure as a likely v1.21 deferral or late optional scaffolding, yet REQUIREMENTS.md makes `SPIKE-10` mandatory. `SPIKE-11` reads more like a design constraint for the whole spike overhaul than a single requirement.

**Design constraint compliance:** Intellectually aligned, but operationally risky. The milestone warns against rigid procedures, yet the cumulative effect of `SPIKE-01`, `SPIKE-08`, `SPIKE-09`, `SPIKE-10`, and `SPIKE-11` could produce exactly that.

**Over-formalization risk:** High. This is the category where the spec is closest to turning productive experimental work into ceremony. The fact that `SPIKE-11` explicitly warns against rigidity is evidence that the risk is already visible.

**Cross-requirement tensions:** `SPIKE-10` conflicts with the summary's defer/gate posture. `SPIKE-11` is in tension with `WF-01` and with the milestone's general caution that productive informal practices should not be suffocated by protocol.

**Issues found:**
- `SPIKE-10` should not remain an unconditional v1.20 requirement unless the roadmap explicitly chooses that scope expansion.
- `SPIKE-11` is not verifiable enough to serve as a deliverable requirement; it is better treated as a governing design constraint with concrete subrequirements under it.
- `SPIKE-09` should be conditional on `SPIKE-01` proving useful; otherwise the spec stacks two reviewer agents before validating the first.
- No spike requirement defines fallback behavior when cross-model independence is desired but not operationally available.

### Workflow Gaps

**Completeness:** Incomplete. The strongest missing item is milestone-level discussion/steering. MILESTONE-CONTEXT.md explicitly states that no formal `/gsdr:discuss-milestone` workflow exists, yet that gap is not represented here.

**Specificity:** `WF-02` and `WF-03` are specific enough. `WF-01` is detailed, but the requirement mixes desired flexibility with procedural load in a way that could work against itself.

**Traceability:** Strong. Each item maps to a concrete workflow gap.

**Dependencies:** `WF-01` depends on cross-model dispatch capability and on the broader anti-over-formalization discipline established in the spike work. `WF-03` needs clear boundaries relative to existing research-phase/spike flows.

**Feasibility:** `WF-02` is feasible and high value. `WF-03` is feasible if sharply differentiated from spike work. `WF-01` is feasible, but the "committed audit spec before launch" requirement may increase friction enough to reduce usage.

**Scope appropriateness:** Good overall, except that the category is missing the milestone-steering gap that generated the current MILESTONE-CONTEXT artifact in the first place.

**Design constraint compliance:** `WF-01` attempts to comply with "don't over-formalize cross-model review," but its own pre-launch committed spec requirement may violate that principle.

**Over-formalization risk:** High for `WF-01`, low otherwise.

**Cross-requirement tensions:** `WF-01` says the command must remain opt-in and flexible, but "committed audit spec before launch" pushes it toward heavier ceremony than the positive pattern may tolerate.

**Issues found:**
- The workflow category is missing a requirement for a formal milestone-level discuss/steering workflow even though the gap is explicitly documented.
- `WF-01` contains an internal tension between flexibility and mandatory up-front artifact creation.
- `WF-03` needs a clearer boundary relative to existing research-phase and spike workflows or it will duplicate them.

### Cross-Runtime Parity

**Completeness:** Good on degradation intent and distribution checks. Incomplete on capability detection, runtime/version prerequisites, and executable parity verification beyond install-time checks.

**Specificity:** `XRT-03` is concrete. `XRT-02` needs a definition of compatibility. `XRT-01` is process-specific rather than behavior-specific, which is acceptable only if the spec explicitly distinguishes governance requirements from shipped product behavior.

**Traceability:** Strong.

**Dependencies:** `XRT-01` is an upstream dependency for `GATE-06`, `GATE-07`, and any hook-dependent feature. `XRT-03` overlaps with `SENS-06`. `XRT-02` relies on patch comparison semantics that are not specified elsewhere.

**Feasibility:** Achievable, but less straightforward than the wording suggests. Patch compatibility is not binary when runtimes intentionally diverge. Capability checks are required if `SENS-03` and hook-dependent flows are going to be safe.

**Scope appropriateness:** Appropriate, but incomplete. Given the reliance on `node:sqlite` and Codex runtime internals, runtime prerequisite verification should be in scope, not implicit.

**Design constraint compliance:** Aligned with the milestone's parity concerns. Weaker on "don't hardcode single-machine paths" because no parity requirement explicitly calls for path-agnostic runtime discovery.

**Over-formalization risk:** Low.

**Cross-requirement tensions:** `XRT-03` duplicates `SENS-06`. `XRT-01` requires phase CONTEXT documentation before implementation, but there is no milestone-level discuss workflow to standardize that practice above the phase layer.

**Issues found:**
- `XRT-01` is a governance/process requirement and should be labeled as such; otherwise it sits awkwardly beside product behavior requirements.
- `XRT-02` needs a sharper definition of compatibility and what evidence proves a patch is safe to apply.
- `XRT-03` overlaps substantially with `SENS-06`.
- No cross-runtime requirement captures Node/CLI version prerequisites or runtime capability detection.

### Parallel Execution

**Completeness:** The category addresses the visible `STATE.md` merge-conflict pain, but not the harder problem of semantic dependency conflicts. It also omits migration from the current single-file state model.

**Specificity:** `PAR-01` and `PAR-02` are concrete. `PAR-03` is too vague: "non-overlapping file scopes" is not a sufficient operational criterion, and "coordinated merge" is undefined.

**Traceability:** Good for the pain being addressed, but less convincing for the full scope being mandated.

**Dependencies:** `PAR-02` depends on `PAR-01`. `PAR-03` depends on both, plus dependency analysis beyond file overlap. SUMMARY.md also treats this as separately gated work, but REQUIREMENTS.md does not encode that gate.

**Feasibility:** The state-file split is feasible. Safe parallel phase execution is materially harder than the requirement suggests because interface-level conflicts can occur without file overlap.

**Scope appropriateness:** Overcommitted. SUMMARY.md treats parallel execution as late, gated, and only justified once it is a regular workflow. REQUIREMENTS.md instead presents all three `PAR-*` items as unconditional v1.20 scope.

**Design constraint compliance:** Neutral to positive.

**Over-formalization risk:** Low. The risk here is false safety rather than bureaucracy.

**Cross-requirement tensions:** `PAR-03` assumes non-overlapping file scopes are enough, while the rest of the system increasingly relies on structural gates and shared workflow invariants that can still conflict across worktrees.

**Issues found:**
- `PAR-03` needs stronger eligibility criteria than non-overlapping file scopes.
- The category is missing a migration/compatibility requirement for moving from `STATE.md` to per-worktree state files.
- Parallel execution is presented as mandatory v1.20 scope even though SUMMARY.md frames it as separately gated and potentially deferable.

## Cross-Cutting Findings

- The specification has strong evidence discipline at the requirement level and weak dependency discipline at the milestone level.
- The largest systematic issue is mandatory-scope inflation: REQUIREMENTS.md turns several "should-have," "late-phase," or "roadmap decision needed" items from SUMMARY.md into unconditional v1.20 commitments.
- Process/governance requirements and product behavior requirements are intermixed without labeling. `XRT-01` is the clearest example, but similar issues appear in `GATE-05` and parts of the spike stack.
- Migration and compatibility work are treated as implementation details when they are actually milestone-critical requirements. This affects KB schema evolution, spike artifact evolution, runtime prerequisites, and `STATE.md` replacement.
- The Traceability section at the end of REQUIREMENTS.md is effectively empty. That does not negate the per-requirement motivations, but it does mean the document is not yet ready for reliable roadmap handoff.

## Gaps Identified

- **Missing category: Migration and compatibility.** The spec needs explicit requirements for signal schema migration, spike artifact compatibility, `STATE.md` migration, and rollback/rebuild safety.
- **Missing category: Runtime prerequisites and capability detection.** The summary identifies Node `>=22.5.0`, Codex hook absence, and reliance on `state_5.sqlite`, but no requirement captures prerequisite verification.
- **Missing category: Verification/invariant testing for the new infrastructure.** Dual-write safety, lifecycle automation, telemetry validation, and parity checks all need explicit acceptance requirements, not just implementation intent.
- **Missing workflow requirement:** A formal milestone-level discuss/steering workflow or equivalent artifact generator is absent even though MILESTONE-CONTEXT.md documents that gap directly.
- **Missing parallel-execution guard:** There is no requirement for semantic dependency/conflict analysis before allowing phases to run in parallel.

## Recommendations

1. Re-tier the spec so REQUIREMENTS.md matches SUMMARY.md. At minimum, make `SPIKE-10`, `PAR-03`, and the full current form of `KB-06` explicitly conditional, deferred, or separately gated rather than unconditional v1.20 commitments.
2. Add a migration/compatibility requirement set. It should cover existing signal-file migration, `source` field normalization, spike artifact/template migration, and `STATE.md` to per-worktree-state transition.
3. Add a runtime-prerequisite requirement set. It should verify Node/CLI versions, detect hook availability, and gate any use of Codex `state_5.sqlite` behind capability checks.
4. Turn `TEL-02` into an explicit cross-category gate: baseline capture cannot be considered satisfied until `TEL-03` passes and `.planning/baseline.json` is committed, and structural changes should not merge ahead of that point without an explicit exception.
5. Narrow `GATE-07` to a smaller v1.20 target or defer it. A bounded destructive-event prompt is requirement-shaped; a multi-heuristic self-signal detector is not yet.
6. Split `SPIKE-11` into a short design-constraints section plus a few concrete deliverables. As written, it is too interpretive to verify cleanly.
7. Add `WF-04`: a milestone-level discuss/steering workflow that produces a structured context artifact, since the current milestone had to invent that informally.
8. Remove duplication between `SENS-06` and `XRT-03`, and clarify the boundary between `SENS-04` and `XRT-02`.
9. Strengthen `PAR-03` so eligibility for parallel execution requires dependency analysis, not just non-overlapping file scopes.
10. Replace or supplement the empty Traceability table with requirement-to-phase mapping before roadmap execution begins.

## Tensions and Risks

- `TEL-02` versus `GATE-01`/`GATE-02`/`GATE-03`: the spec wants urgent structural fixes and uncontaminated baseline measurement. Without an explicit exception policy, roadmap pressure will pick one and silently violate the other.
- `SPIKE-01`/`SPIKE-08`/`SPIKE-09`/`SPIKE-11` versus the anti-over-formalization thesis: the cumulative spike stack risks producing methodology theater rather than better experiments.
- `WF-01` versus itself: opt-in, flexible cross-model review is the stated goal, but a committed audit spec before launch may reduce the very usage pattern the requirement wants to preserve.
- `SENS-06` versus `XRT-03`: duplicated deliverables create false breadth and muddy acceptance criteria.
- `KB-01` versus the audit finding that some signals should remain live diagnostics: lifecycle support is needed, but the lifecycle must not implicitly force every signal toward closure.
- `PAR-03` versus actual system coupling: file-level non-overlap is not enough to guarantee safe parallel phase execution in a workflow harness with shared gates, shared runtime assumptions, and shared verification surfaces.
