## DESIGN.md Audit

### What Worked
- All three designs stated a clear question, hypothesis, and success criteria
- Experiment plans were concrete and executable
- Research mode was correctly identified (no unnecessary BUILD/RUN phases)
- Spike A correctly identified evidence sources (session-meta + JSONL)

### Issues

**I-1: Designs are too thin for serious spikes.** All three DESIGN.md files are ~30-40 lines. For research-mode data analysis on existing files, this is adequate. For genuinely experimental or frontier work, DESIGN.md should include:
- **Scope boundaries** — what is explicitly OUT of scope for this investigation
- **Failure modes** — what would make the findings misleading (e.g., "if session-meta files were generated by different Claude Code versions with different schemas, our cross-session comparisons may be invalid")
- **Alternative hypotheses** — what else could explain the expected patterns (e.g., "friction ↔ interruption correlation could be driven by session length as a confound, not a causal relationship")
- **What inconclusive looks like** — how to distinguish "no signal" from "not enough data"

**I-2: No design review before execution.** The orchestrator embedded design content in agent prompts rather than writing DESIGN.md files first and having the spike-runner read them. The agents wrote their own DESIGN.md as step 1 of execution. This means no orchestrator review occurred between design and execution — the design phase collapsed into the execution phase.

**I-3: Success criteria are too binary.** "At least 2 of 4 correlations show meaningful patterns" is pass/fail. A richer success criterion would describe what levels of finding are possible: strong signal (include with full weight), weak signal (include with caveats), no signal (exclude), or surprising counter-signal (investigate further). The actual findings were more nuanced than the binary criteria allowed.

### Spike A-Specific Design Issues

**I-1a: No scope boundaries.** Spike A didn't state what it WON'T investigate. It actually discovered JSONL unreliability (streaming duplication, /continue inheritance) — an important finding that was outside the original question but emerged because the scope was undefined. Serendipitous here, but undirected scope in a more complex spike could waste effort.

**I-1b: No alternative hypotheses.** The design only considers "post-cache residuals." Doesn't pre-register: truncated data, version-dependent schema changes, sampling vs aggregation, or the /continue inheritance issue it actually discovered. All three of the divergence causes it found (batch generation, streaming duplication, /continue inheritance) were emergent, not designed-for.

**I-1c: Success criteria didn't match actual finding space.** ">10% difference = unreliable" is binary. The actual finding was tripartite: input is unreliable as workload BUT reliable as cache-miss indicator; output IS reliable; JSONL is ALSO unreliable for different reasons. A richer success criterion would have described this possibility space.

**Positive note:** Spike A significantly exceeded its design. It found 3 causes of divergence, discovered batch generation timestamps (meta files created in bulk, not per-session), proposed a 4-level metric hierarchy, and identified /continue inheritance as a JSONL contamination vector. The thin design didn't prevent good work — but it means the good work was emergent rather than directed.

## DECISION.md Audit

### What Worked
- All three decisions included detailed findings tables with sample sizes
- Outlier analysis was present and insightful (Spike A: 3 causes of divergence, batch timestamps; Spike C: high-error fully-achieved sessions; Spike E: 19,996-minute artifact)
- Counterintuitive findings were explained rather than ignored (helpfulness ↔ errors inverse, input_tokens as useful cache-miss indicator despite being "unreliable")
- Confidence was calibrated with basis (HIGH for specific findings, MEDIUM for thresholds)
- Implications sections were concrete and actionable
- Spike A's metric hierarchy (output_tokens > assistant_message_count > input_tokens > JSONL) is a genuinely useful framework

### Issues

**I-4: Decision framing is too closure-oriented.** All three DECISION.md files frame findings as "we decided to include X in the baseline." Per user guidance (2026-04-09): spikes should open design space, not close it. Findings should be landscape characterizations that expand understanding, not verdicts that foreclose research.

Example — Spike C says: "Chosen approach: Include facets data in baseline metrics with appropriate weighting."
Should say something like: "Landscape finding: Facets data contains validated signal in friction and session_type dimensions, but outcome and helpfulness are holistic judgments orthogonal to technical metrics. This opens the question of what 'session quality' actually means and whether we need separate quality constructs for technical quality vs goal achievement vs user experience."

Example — Spike A says: "Chosen approach: Use output_tokens as the primary token signal."
Should say something like: "Landscape finding: session-meta token fields measure two fundamentally different things. input_tokens tracks cache-miss residuals (API billing artifact), output_tokens tracks generation work (behavioral signal). This opens: can input_tokens be repurposed as a cache-health indicator? What does it mean that true cost accounting requires JSONL with dedup — is there a middle path?"

**I-5: No "Opened Territory" section.** No DECISION.md documents the new questions that emerged. All three spikes opened significant territory:
- Spike A opened: Can input_tokens serve as cache-cold signal? What about true cost accounting (neither meta nor JSONL alone suffices)? What are the implications of batch-generated meta files (stale if session continues after batch)?
- Spike C opened: What is quality? (outcome ≠ errors), friction detection recall gaps, abandoned session handling
- Spike E opened: Is GSD effectiveness causal or selection?, fragmentation causality, collection gap archaeology, "question" sessions as high-friction pattern

These are arguably the most valuable outputs of the spikes, and they're buried in implications rather than elevated as primary outputs.

**I-6: Insufficient self-critique of statistical method.**
- Spike C: rho=0.55 called "strong" without examining session-length confound (drives both friction and interruptions)
- Spike E: Pearson r on non-normal data (entropy bounded, tool_errors zero-inflated) may overstate linear relationship
- Neither C nor E discusses multiple comparisons (4+ correlations inflate false discovery)
- Neither reports confidence intervals or p-values (likely significant at N=106-264 but assumed, not stated)
- Spike A: The 0-8% output match is stated but the 5 "clean" sessions were selected after excluding problematic ones — survivorship bias in reliability claim. The unreliable sessions (JSONL modified post-meta, /continue inheritance) are arguably MORE important to characterize.

**I-7: No epistemic humility annotations.** Per the Phase 57 CONTEXT.md governing principles, every metric should include interpretive context about what it measures, what it doesn't, and how it could mislead. The spike findings should model this practice.

## Recommendations

1. **For Phase 57 planning:** Use spike findings as landscape characterizations, not locked decisions. The researcher should feel free to challenge correlations, propose confounders, and investigate the opened territory.

2. **For Phase 61 (Spike Methodology):** Redesign DECISION.md template to include mandatory "Opened Territory" and "Self-Critique" sections. Consider renaming from "DECISION.md" to something less closure-oriented (e.g., "FINDINGS.md" or "LANDSCAPE.md").

3. **For immediate follow-up:** The data integrity characterization spike (007) is the highest-priority follow-up — every baseline metric depends on knowing which sessions are trustworthy data.
