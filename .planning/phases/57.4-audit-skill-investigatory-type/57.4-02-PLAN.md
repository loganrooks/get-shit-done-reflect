**Investigatory orientation obligations (I1-I4 verbatim from REVIEW.md lines 70-74 — do NOT paraphrase, these phrasings are load-bearing):**

- **I1 — Start from the discrepancy, not a theory.** Describe what was expected, what was delivered, and why those expectations are being treated as the standard — the choice of comparison point is already an interpretive act. *(Why this matters: investigations that start from a theory select evidence to confirm the theory. Investigations that start from the discrepancy remain open to evidence that the theory is wrong.)*

- **I2 — Let the investigation guide artifact selection.** Don't mandate which artifacts to read in advance. The artifact chain is a finding, not an input. *(Why this matters: a pre-specified reading list encodes a hypothesis about where the failure lies. The investigation should discover where to read from what it finds, not from what the orchestrator specified.)*

- **I3 — Present competing explanations.** For each finding, offer at least two interpretations. Don't collapse to one. *(Why this matters: an investigation that presents a single explanation has stopped investigating. The criterion for closing an investigation is not "I found an explanation" but "the evidence rules out the alternatives.")*

- **I4 — Name the position of the investigation.** Every investigation is conducted from somewhere — with particular attunements, particular things it's prepared to notice, particular things it isn't. Name them. *(Why this matters: every investigator is embedded. Pretending otherwise produces the illusion of neutrality and hides the frame from both auditor and reader.)*

Plus two additional investigatory obligations from RESEARCH.md §"Orientation Obligations":
- **Show what remains unknown.** Not everything needs to be resolved. Explicit unknowns are findings.
- **Show how you navigated any tensions between obligations.** If obligations tension against each other, the navigation is itself a finding — see the composition principle in Section 5.

**Exploratory orientation obligations** (lift from RESEARCH.md §"Orientation Obligations"):
- State the question or curiosity that initiated the exploration
- Follow the question wherever it leads — permission to change direction
- Name what you found that you weren't looking for
- Name what the exploration opened (new questions, possibilities)
- Name what you didn't look at (acknowledged partiality, not failure)

**Part A — Add new Section 5: "Composition Principle".**

This is a NEW section (placed between Section 4 task-spec embedding and the existing Section 5 Governing Principle, which becomes Section 6). Write the composition principle as **prose**, not pseudocode or precedence rules. Follow the deliberation's framing (lines 156-164) verbatim or near-verbatim:

**Section 5 header:** "Composition Principle"

**Section 5 body (2-3 paragraphs of prose):**

> Obligations from different axes compose into a flat list. A single audit may carry core obligations (Rules 1-5), orientation obligations (I1-I4 for investigatory, for example), subject obligations (e.g., process_review's "compare against spec"), and cross-cutting obligations (chain integrity if the audit references predecessors). These do not form a hierarchy. They form a set the auditor must engage with.

> When obligations tension against each other — for example, I2 ("let the investigation guide artifact selection") and S1 for phase_verification ("verify execution against success criteria") may pull in opposite directions when the success criteria themselves are part of what's being investigated — the auditor must:
>
> 1. **Name the tension** — say what the two obligations are and how they pull differently in this situation
> 2. **Name what about the situation creates it** — the tension is not abstract; it is occasioned by particulars
> 3. **Show how you navigated it** — responsive to both demands, not cleanly picking one side
> 4. **The resolution emerges from engagement** with the situation, not from a precedence rule applied in advance

> This is a **hermeneutic principle, not an algorithmic one**. If you find yourself cleanly ignoring one obligation in favor of another, you've likely stopped engaging with the tension. The sign of engaged composition is not a clean resolution — it is a navigated one, where both obligations leave traces in the finding.

End Section 5 with the explicit untested warning from CONTEXT.md Q1: "This principle is untested. The first investigatory audits under this framework are the test. If agents consistently collapse tensions to clean resolution, the principle has failed its hermeneutic character and will need revision or algorithmic support."
