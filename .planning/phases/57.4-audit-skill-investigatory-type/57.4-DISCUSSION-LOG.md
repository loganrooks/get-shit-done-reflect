## Claim Justifications

### Three-axis taxonomy

**[decided:reasoned] 9 subject types replacing the original 8**
- **Alternatives considered:** Keep flat 8-type taxonomy; add investigatory as 9th type; add as 4th family
- **Why rejected:** Flat taxonomy conflates three orthogonal concerns. Family addition reinforces family taxonomy as primary organizing principle. Retrospective validated the decomposition across 13 audit sessions.
- **User said:** "cross_model_review doesn't seem like a type, that's more of a delegation" (v1 discuss-phase)

**[decided:reasoned] Subject optional for investigatory/exploratory orientations**
- **Alternatives considered:** Require subject for all audits
- **Why rejected:** The Phase 57 investigation case had no subject at start — the investigation discovered its subject (process_review territory, as it turned out). Requiring subject forecloses this discovery.
- **User said:** Deliberation section "Axis 1: Subject" — "when you don't know what you're investigating yet... the investigation discovers its subject"

**[decided:reasoned] Each subject carries an epistemic profile (oriented_toward, assumes, might_miss)**
- **Alternatives considered:** No profile metadata; profile as free-text description
- **Why rejected:** Orchestrator needs structured data to match situation to subject. The "might_miss" column specifically supports the no-subject case — if every subject's might_miss would miss the situation's features, that's a signal for investigatory-without-subject.
- **Citation:** Deliberation Section "Axis 1: Subject" contains the full profile table

### Obligations model

**[decided:reasoned] Replace templates with composable obligation sets for non-standard orientations**
- **Alternatives considered:** Templates for all types; obligations for all types
- **Why rejected:** Templates can't compose across axes. Obligations for routine standard audits lose consistency (VERIFICATION.md consistency matters for cross-phase skimming).
- **Citation:** Retrospective Section 5 "Three populations confirmed"

**[decided:reasoned] Three populations: templates / obligations / multi-agent**
- **Alternatives considered:** Single paradigm for all audit complexity
- **Why rejected:** Retrospective empirically identified three distinct populations with different design demands. Evidence: VERIFICATION.md templates work perfectly for Population 1; discuss-phase audit chain needed obligations; session-log audit needed mechanisms neither templates nor obligations provide.
- **Citation:** Retrospective Section 5

**[decided:reasoned] Composition principle is hermeneutic, not algorithmic**
- **Alternatives considered:** Algorithmic precedence rules (subject obligations win / orientation obligations win)
- **Why rejected:** Precedence rules impose mechanical resolution on what should be situated judgment.
- **User said:** "we shouldn't let conflicts be so cleanly resolved in perfect adherence to one rule or the other — name the tension, name the demand of the situation"

**[assumed:reasoned] The composition principle is written but untested**
- **Challenge protocol:** If first investigatory audits show agents consistently collapsing to one side of obligation tensions, the principle needs algorithmic support or reframing
- **Evidence checked:** No empirical test yet; the cross-project adoption audit independently met investigatory obligations without framework knowledge, suggesting the practice is achievable when framing is sophisticated
- **Why reasonable:** The framing matters — rigorous framing produced good output in prior audits; the obligations are designed to encode that framing

**[decided:reasoned] Three new obligations: chain integrity, dispatch hygiene, framework invisibility**
- **Alternatives considered:** Leave these as gaps; add as guidelines rather than obligations
- **Why rejected:** Retrospective Gap 4, 5, 7 identified these as real failure modes with concrete evidence. Guidelines vs. obligations distinction doesn't apply — obligations IS the form for things agents must address.
- **Citation:** Retrospective Section 4

**[decided:reasoned] Frame-reflexivity Rule 5 for ALL audit types** (carried forward from v1)
- **Alternatives considered:** Investigatory only; fold into Rule 4 (escape hatch); optional per type
- **Why rejected:** REVIEW.md Part 4 argues the concern (types smuggle assumptions) applies beyond investigatory — every type presupposes a form of failure. Rule 4 catches excess within the frame; Rule 5 catches whether the frame was right. The distinction is clear enough to warrant a separate rule.
- **User said (v1 discuss-phase, verbatim):** "We need a newfound critical eye towards our framing, our perhaps implicit assumptions that we need to expose."
- **Challenge protocol (carried forward from v1 [assumed:reasoned]):** Would falsify if frame-reflexivity and escape hatch are consistently conflated in practice (agents treat them identically). v2 upgrades the type to [decided:reasoned] because the deliberation and retrospective both validated the distinction, but the challenge protocol remains as a monitoring heuristic — watch first investigatory audits for conflation.
- **Lightweight vs. full section (carried forward from v1 [assumed:reasoned]):** Challenge protocol: would falsify if lightweight treatment (1-2 sentences) consistently produces empty boilerplate rather than genuine reflection. Monitor first 3-5 standard-orientation audits for performative engagement.

**[assumed:reasoned] Investigatory vs. exploratory distinction is one of occasion**
- **Challenge protocol (carried forward from v1):** Would falsify if usage shows investigatory and exploratory being invoked interchangeably with no difference in starting condition
- **Evidence checked:** REVIEW.md Part 5 provides Heideggerian analysis (breakdown vs. possibility); deliberation carries this forward. Cross-project adoption audit (investigatory) and 2026-04-02 philosophical audit (exploratory) show the distinction in practice
- **Why reasonable:** The body structures differ because starting conditions differ; distinction may soften with use. Per G-5 guardrail, implementation should not foreclose the possibility that they merge.

### Command architecture

**[decided:reasoned] Two-file architecture: commands/gsd/audit.md + agents/gsdr-auditor.md**
- **Alternatives considered:** Single-file command (no agent); inline orchestration
- **Why rejected:** Orchestrator commands that dispatch to long-running agent work follow this pattern: audit-milestone → gsd-integration-checker, validate-phase → gsdr-nyquist-auditor, execute-phase → gsdr-executor. The audit command is in the same category (dispatches an agent that produces a long output). Single-file commands can't be reused by other workflows.
- **Correction from v1 context-checker:** v1 CONTEXT.md claimed "the pattern is universal" as the justification for the two-file architecture. The gsdr-context-checker verified this and flagged it as incorrect (WARN, documented in the archived v1 DISCUSSION-LOG's context-checker verification log). Many GSD commands (help, signal, add-todo, discuss-phase itself) operate inline without dispatching to an agent. The accurate claim is narrower: the pattern is followed by orchestrator-style commands that dispatch long-running agent work. The decision stands but the rationale is corrected.

**[decided:reasoned] Orchestrator writes full task spec, agent executes (spike-runner pattern)**
- **Alternatives considered:** Agent infers its own scope; shared config file
- **Why rejected:** Task spec preservation is the strongest empirical finding from 57.3 research. Agent inferring scope loses the audit trail.
- **Citation:** 57.3-CONTEXT.md "Task specs must be preserved"

**[decided:reasoned] Type inference from context, not forced selection**
- **Alternatives considered:** Forced type selection menu; always ask
- **Why rejected:** User invokes mid-situation with natural language. Forced selection adds friction.
- **Citation:** deliberate.md established context-inference pattern

### Cross-model delegation

**[decided:reasoned] WF-01 pulled from Phase 62 into 57.4**
- **Alternatives considered:** Keep in Phase 62; handle as documented manual workflow
- **Why rejected:** Audit is the natural home for cross-model dispatch since audits are the primary cross-model use case. Deferring leaves the fragile state unaddressed when the audit command ships.
- **User said:** Deliberation decision captured during retrospective

**[open] Specific cross-model dispatch mechanics**
- **What's been tried:** Current fragile state documented (environment setup issues, agents finishing early, instructions not properly conveyed)
- **Why unresolved:** Mechanics haven't been investigated; failure modes need systematic study
- **Research delegation:** Investigate robust CLI invocation patterns, environment detection, instruction framing, output collection

### Agent design

**[decided:reasoned] Tool access: Read, Bash, Grep, Glob, Write**
- **Alternatives considered:** Read-only; full tool access including Agent/Task
- **Why rejected:** Read-only can't produce output files. Full access with Agent/Task is unnecessary — auditor is the leaf agent.

**[decided:reasoned] gsdr-auditor model: sonnet in frontmatter, orchestrator escalates**
- **Alternatives considered:** Default inherit (opus); type-conditional default
- **Why rejected:** Default opus is expensive and overkill for standard audits. Type-conditional defaults are complex and can be added later if usage patterns warrant it.
- **Citation:** Consistent with gsdr-context-checker precedent

### Frontmatter schema

**[decided:reasoned] Three new fields replacing audit_type semantic content**
- **Alternatives considered:** Single field with compound notation (e.g., `audit_type: process_review/investigatory/self`)
- **Why rejected:** Compound notation is harder to query and filter. Three fields map directly to the three axes and support backward compatibility via mapping.
- **Citation:** Deliberation "Frontmatter Schema Changes"

**[assumed:reasoned] Field count stays within ~12-field budget from 57.3**
- **Challenge protocol:** If the 14-field total (12 + audit_type legacy + 2 new required axis fields) becomes unwieldy, the audit_type legacy field can be dropped after migration completes
- **Evidence checked:** 57.3 established the 12-field budget; this phase adds 2 net fields (3 new replace 1 legacy, but legacy kept for backward compat)
- **Why reasonable:** Backward compat is worth 2 extra fields during migration period

### Philosophical commitments (see forms-excess deliberation for extended trace)

- **Source:** REVIEW.md subjects its own proposals to the same critique; deliberation carries this forward
- **Scope:** Constrains implementation to respect the self-reflexive warnings (especially about performative humility)

**[governing:reasoned] Frame-reflexivity and excess sections must be genuine, not performative**
- **Source:** forms-excess deliberation, REVIEW.md Part 6
- **Scope:** Agent spec must ground these in specific questions rather than generic prompts; the "mode of betrayal" analysis from forms-excess applies here

**[governing:reasoned] The hermeneutic composition principle is a practice, not an algorithm**
- **Source:** Deliberation + user direction ("name the tension, not clean resolution")
- **Scope:** If agents consistently resolve obligation tensions by cleanly picking one side, the principle has failed. The researcher/planner should design conditions for practice, not checks for compliance.
- **Note:** See the live conversation above — this claim strains against the current claim-type vocabulary. The forms-excess deliberation now carries the extended analysis.

**[governing:reasoned] The aporia of epistemic humility is a condition of the framework's existence, not a problem to solve**
- **Source:** Deliberation Section "Open Questions (remaining)" #7
- **Scope:** No "Known Limits" section. The operative mechanisms are structural openings (Rule 4, Rule 5, framework invisibility obligation, excess section). Whether they function as genuine openings or performative gestures depends on practice.
